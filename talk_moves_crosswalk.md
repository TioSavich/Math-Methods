# Talk Moves Deep Crosswalk

**CDM/Talk Moves mapped to Five Practices, Van de Walle, and Indiana Process Standards**

*For E343 pre-service teachers who need to know WHEN and HOW to use each move, not just WHAT it is.*

---

## Quick Orientation: What This Document Does

You are learning six frameworks at once. That is genuinely hard. This crosswalk exists because CDM/Talk Moves (Chapin, O'Connor, Anderson) is the framework students report the most confusion about -- not because the moves themselves are complicated, but because it is unclear how they fit with the Five Practices, Van de Walle's Before/During/After structure, and the Indiana Process Standards you are also learning.

**The bottom line:** Talk Moves are the *specific teacher language* you use to carry out the Five Practices during the *lesson phases* Van de Walle describes, in service of the *mathematical habits of mind* the Indiana Process Standards require. They are not a separate thing. They are the how.

---

## The Four Steps at a Glance

CDM organizes all talk moves into four progressive steps. Each step builds on the one before it.

| Step | Goal | Core Question | Talk Moves |
|------|------|---------------|------------|
| **Step 1** | Help individual students clarify and share their own thoughts | "Can you make your thinking visible?" | Wait Time, Turn and Talk, Say More, Revoicing |
| **Step 2** | Help students orient to the thinking of others | "Did you hear what they said?" | Who Can Repeat? |
| **Step 3** | Help students deepen their own reasoning | "Why does that work?" | Press for Reasoning |
| **Step 4** | Help students engage with the reasoning of others | "What do you think about that?" | Agree/Disagree and Why, Who Can Add On? |

**Key insight from the course:** Steps 1 and 2 are about getting ideas *on the table*. Steps 3 and 4 are about *doing something* with those ideas. Many new teachers get stuck at Step 1 -- they get students talking but never push into Steps 3 and 4 where the real mathematical learning happens.

### Wait Time

**CDM definition:** After asking a question, the teacher waits at least 4-5 seconds before calling on anyone. Not a "move" in the verbal sense -- it is the deliberate use of silence. Mary Budd Rowe's research (1974) showed that most teachers wait less than 1 second. Mrs. Anderson in the CDM book discovered her wait times were under 1 second and trained herself to count to 10.

**CDM Step:** Step 1 -- Helping Individual Students Clarify and Share Their Own Thoughts

**When to use it:**
- After posing any question to the whole class
- After a student finishes speaking and before you respond or call on someone else
- When you see only one or two hands up (the "usual suspects")
- When you want to signal that you expect *thinking*, not just fast recall

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Monitoring (Practice 2), but also supports all practices during whole-class discussion
- **How it shows up:** During monitoring, wait time happens at the small-group level -- you ask a question and then *stay and listen* rather than immediately moving to the next group. During the Connecting discussion, wait time after each student presentation gives the class time to process before you ask for comparisons. Smith & Stein's "assessing questions" require wait time to be effective -- if you ask "Can you explain what you did?" and then jump in after 2 seconds, you have not actually assessed anything.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** All phases (Before/During/After), but especially critical during the After phase
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle discusses the need for "productive struggle" and giving students time to think. The Before/During/After framework assumes students have processing time at every transition. Number Talks, which Van de Walle champions, depend entirely on wait time -- you pose a mental math problem and wait with hands down until you see most students signaling readiness.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.1 (Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them)
- **How this move supports the standard:** Students cannot make sense of a problem if they are rushed. Wait time is the most basic prerequisite for PS.1 -- you are literally giving students time to persevere rather than giving up or guessing.

**In a real lesson:** You are teaching a fraction comparison lesson (3rd grade). You show 3/4 and 2/3 on the board and ask, "Which fraction is larger?" One student's hand shoots up immediately. Instead of calling on her, you say: "I'm going to wait until I see more hands. Take a moment to think about this." You count silently to 8. Three more hands go up. Two students are sketching on their whiteboards. *Now* you proceed.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1A -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 3: "Hands down; let him think." She explicitly tells other students to put their hands down so a struggling student has time.

### Turn and Talk (Partner Talk)

**CDM definition:** The teacher asks students to discuss a question or idea with a neighbor for 1-2 minutes. Sometimes called "think-pair-share." Best used when you ask a question and get silence, or when you want *every* student to articulate their thinking (not just the few who raise their hands). The teacher circulates and listens during the partner talk.

**CDM Step:** Step 1 -- Helping Individual Students Clarify and Share Their Own Thoughts (also serves Step 2 -- students orient to a partner's thinking)

**When to use it:**
- When you ask a question and get blank stares or only one hand
- When you want every student to practice saying an idea out loud before sharing with the whole class
- When a concept is new or confusing and students need a low-stakes rehearsal space
- When you want to find out what students are thinking before calling on anyone
- After a student shares something complex that the class needs time to process

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Monitoring (Practice 2) and Anticipating (Practice 1)
- **How it shows up:** Turn and Talk is a *monitoring tool*. While students talk to partners, you circulate and listen. This is a fast way to scan the room for strategies, misconceptions, and language. You can then use what you overhear to *select* (Practice 3) who to call on. The Five Practices book (pp. 69-84) describes monitoring as "carefully keeping track of student reasoning while students are solving a task." Turn and Talk gives you a miniature monitoring opportunity even during a whole-class discussion. The E343 course also uses Turn and Talk during the Connecting phase -- Ms. Fournier (Clip 1D) has students turn and repeat an important idea about ratios to make sure everyone can articulate it.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** All phases, but most powerful during Launch (Before) and Summarize (After)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle uses "think-pair-share" and describes it as part of creating a classroom where students are expected to explain their reasoning. During the Before phase, Turn and Talk helps students understand the task. During the After phase, it helps students process a peer's strategy before the teacher asks for public responses.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others) and PS.6 (Attend to precision)
- **How this move supports the standard:** Turn and Talk gives students a rehearsal space for constructing arguments (PS.3). When a student practices explaining their fraction comparison strategy to a partner, they are forced to attend to precision (PS.6) -- vague hand-waving does not work when your partner is looking right at you.

**In a real lesson:** You are in the Launch phase of a lesson on addition within 1000 (Grade 3, IM curriculum). You show the problem 398 + 257 and ask, "Before you solve this, turn and talk to your partner: What do you notice about these numbers? What might make this problem tricky?" You circulate and hear one pair say "398 is close to 400" and another say "I don't know what to do when you have to regroup twice." Both pieces of information help you decide what to emphasize during the Explore phase.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1B -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 3: "How many cans of grape juice?" Students turn and talk about a multistep problem. Clip 1C -- Mrs. Rowan, Grade 6: Students turn and talk about why 7/6 and 13/9 are greater than a whole. Clip 1D -- Ms. Fournier, Grade 6: "Can everyone turn and repeat that idea?" -- uses Turn and Talk to consolidate understanding, not just generate it.

### Say More

**CDM definition:** When a student says something short, vague, or unclear, the teacher invites them to expand: "Can you say more about that?" "Tell us more about what you're thinking." "Can you give us an example?" This is not a challenge or a correction -- it is a genuine invitation to elaborate. CDM emphasizes that students often aim to say the absolute minimum, and Say More sends the message that the teacher wants to understand their *thinking*, not just hear a correct answer.

**CDM Step:** Step 1 -- Helping Individual Students Clarify and Share Their Own Thoughts

**When to use it:**
- When a student gives a one-word or one-phrase answer ("I added")
- When you can tell the student has more to say but stopped short
- When the answer is correct but the reasoning is invisible
- When the answer is wrong and you need to understand *why* the student thinks that before you can help
- **Critical moment from the course:** When a student says "I added" during a number talk, do NOT ask "Why did you add?" (that feels like a challenge). Instead ask "Can you say more about what you did?" (that feels like an invitation)

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Monitoring (Practice 2) -- specifically, assessing questions
- **How it shows up:** Smith & Stein distinguish between *assessing questions* (surface what the student is thinking) and *advancing questions* (push thinking forward). Say More is the purest form of an assessing question. When you are circulating during the Explore phase and you see a student's work, Say More is how you find out what is going on in their head. The Wk06D2 class (Monitoring) explicitly identifies Say More as a key tool: "Try to meet students where they are by asking about what they have written or drawn because it can help uncover what students are doing."

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** During (Explore) and After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle's emphasis on formative assessment and understanding student thinking maps directly here. His approach to diagnostic interviews (think-aloud interviews in E343) relies heavily on Say More -- you are trying to understand what a student knows, not teach them anything. The TAI assignment in E343 is essentially 20 minutes of practicing Say More with a real child.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments) and PS.6 (Attend to precision)
- **How this move supports the standard:** When you ask students to say more, you are training them to construct arguments that include evidence and reasoning (PS.3), not just conclusions. You are also pushing them toward precision (PS.6) -- "I added" is imprecise; "I added 398 and 2 to make 400, then added 255 more" is precise.

**In a real lesson:** During a Think-Aloud Interview on subtraction (Grade 2), a student looks at 52 - 17 and writes 45. You ask, "Can you say more about how you got 45?" The student says, "I took away... um... I counted back." You ask, "Can you show me how you counted back?" The student counts back from 52 on their fingers: 51, 50, 49, 48, 47, 46, 45. They counted back 7, not 17. Now you understand the error -- they subtracted only the ones digit. Without Say More, you might have just marked it wrong.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1G -- Mrs. Luizzi, Kindergarten: "Can you tell me what you did?" after a turn-and-talk. Clip 1H -- Mr. Danella, Grade 3: "Tell me a little bit more" when a student makes a claim about a logical puzzle.

### Revoicing ("So you're saying...")

**CDM definition:** The teacher attempts to restate what a student said, then checks with the student: "So it sounds like you're saying ___. Is that what you're saying?" This is NOT just repeating the student's words. It is the teacher's interpretation, offered as a question. Revoicing serves two purposes simultaneously: (1) it helps the original speaker clarify their own thinking, and (2) it makes the idea accessible to other students who may not have understood the original statement.

**CDM Step:** Step 1 -- Helping Individual Students Clarify and Share Their Own Thoughts

**When to use it:**
- When a student says something mathematically important but garbled or unclear
- When you *think* you understand what a student means but are not sure
- When you need the whole class to hear an idea that was stated unclearly
- When you want to "translate" a student's informal language into more precise mathematical language while still crediting the student
- **Important:** Always end with a check -- "Is that right?" "Is that what you meant?" This distinguishes revoicing from the teacher simply putting words in a student's mouth.

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Monitoring (Practice 2) and Connecting (Practice 4)
- **How it shows up:** During Monitoring, revoicing is an assessing question -- you are checking whether you understand the student's strategy correctly before recording it on your monitoring chart. During Connecting (the whole-class discussion), revoicing is how you make a student's strategy *hearable* by the rest of the class. When a student shares a strategy and half the class looks confused, you revoice to bridge the gap. Smith & Stein's Connecting practice requires the teacher to "make explicit what might otherwise remain implicit" -- revoicing is one of the primary tools for doing this.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** During (Explore) and After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle discusses the teacher's role in "orchestrating discussion" and helping students articulate mathematical ideas. He emphasizes that the teacher should not simply tell students the answer but should help them refine their own explanations. Revoicing is the mechanism for this. During the After phase, when students are sharing strategies, revoicing helps connect informal student language to formal mathematical vocabulary.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.6 (Attend to precision) and PS.3 (Construct viable arguments)
- **How this move supports the standard:** Revoicing models precise mathematical communication (PS.6). When a student says "I put the pieces together" and you revoice "So you're saying you combined the two groups, and that's addition?", you are showing what precision looks like without correcting the student. You are also helping them construct a clearer argument (PS.3).

**In a real lesson:** During the Summarize phase of a fraction lesson (Grade 4), a student is comparing 3/8 and 5/8 and says: "The top number is bigger so it's more." You revoice: "So you're saying that because 5 is greater than 3, and both fractions have 8 as the denominator, 5/8 is greater than 3/8 -- because you're comparing the same-sized pieces and 5 of them is more than 3 of them? Is that what you mean?" The student nods. Now the class has heard the reasoning stated precisely, and the student still owns the idea.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1E -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 3: "So you're saying I can't buy one can...?" Revoicing to clarify a student's understanding of "at least 26 cans." Clip 1F -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 5: "So you multiplied the length...?" Revoicing a student's volume computation strategy.

### Who Can Repeat?

**CDM definition:** After a student shares an idea, the teacher asks another student to restate it: "Who can put that in their own words?" "Who can repeat what Jaylen just said?" This is NOT a classroom management trick to catch students who are not listening. It is a genuine effort to make sure the idea has been *heard and understood* by more than one person. CDM notes that students can restate in their own words or try to repeat the original -- both are valuable, and the teacher should decide which is more useful in the moment.

**CDM Step:** Step 2 -- Helping Students Orient to the Thinking of Others

**When to use it:**
- After a student says something mathematically important that others need to work with
- When you suspect students were not listening or did not understand
- When a student gives a long or complex explanation and you want the class to consolidate it
- When you want to position a student's idea as *worth paying attention to* (this is an equity move -- it signals that this student's thinking matters)
- After a student shares during the Connecting discussion and before you ask for comparisons

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Connecting (Practice 4) -- and supports Selecting/Sequencing (Practice 3)
- **How it shows up:** The Connecting practice requires students to see relationships between different strategies. They cannot do this if they did not actually hear and understand the strategies being shared. Who Can Repeat? is the bridge between Selecting/Sequencing (which determines *what* gets shared) and Connecting (which builds meaning from what was shared). After a selected student presents their strategy, asking "Who can repeat?" ensures the class is ready to engage with it. Mrs. Foley (Clip 1K) uses Who Can Repeat? in between Press for Reasoning questions -- she gets a student to repeat the volume strategy before pushing for deeper reasoning about *why* it works.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle discusses the importance of students listening to each other during the "after" phase of problem-solving lessons. His emphasis on classroom discourse assumes students are actually processing each other's contributions. Who Can Repeat? operationalizes this expectation.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others) and PS.1 (Make sense of problems)
- **How this move supports the standard:** You cannot critique the reasoning of others (PS.3) if you did not hear or understand it. Who Can Repeat? builds the listening foundation that PS.3 requires. It also supports PS.1 because restating a solution in your own words is itself an act of sense-making.

**In a real lesson:** During the Summarize phase of a multiplication lesson (Grade 3), a student explains that she solved 6 x 7 by thinking of it as 6 x 5 plus 6 x 2. The explanation is quick and some students are looking elsewhere. You say: "That was an important strategy. Who can put what Maria just said into their own words?" A second student says: "She broke the 7 into 5 and 2, then multiplied each part by 6 and added them." Now the class has heard the distributive property articulated twice, and you can proceed to ask how this compares to another student's approach.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1I -- Mrs. Hayward, Kindergarten: "He's not all done yet." Clip 1J -- Mrs. Delaney, Grade 1: "She was counting by fives." Clip 1K -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 5: Volume of rectangular prisms. Clip 1L -- Mrs. Rowan, Grade 6: Fraction comparison reasoning. Clip 1M -- Ms. Fournier, Grade 6: Ratio problem strategy.

### Press for Reasoning ("Why does that work?")

**CDM definition:** The teacher pushes a student to explain *why* their answer or strategy works, not just *what* they did. Variations include: "Why do you think that?" "What is your evidence?" "How did you get that answer?" "Can you prove it?" "What convinced you?" CDM cautions that this must be done in a genuinely curious tone, not an interrogating one. The goal is to make reasoning visible, whether the answer is correct or incorrect.

**CDM Step:** Step 3 -- Helping Students Deepen Their Own Reasoning

**When to use it:**
- After a student gives a correct answer without explaining how they got it
- After a student uses a strategy and you want the *mathematical logic* behind it made explicit
- When a student holds a misconception and you want to surface the reasoning so the class can examine it (as with Paulo's even/odd misconception in CDM Ch. 1)
- During monitoring, as an *advancing question* -- pushing a student to go further than they have gone on their own
- **Critical warning from the course:** Do not lead with "Why?" questions early in a discussion. The Wk05D1 slides say: "My recommendation always is to ask *what* questions first... *why* questions are often confusing and don't have a ready answer." Use Say More and Revoicing first (Steps 1-2), THEN press for reasoning (Step 3) once the idea is on the table.

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Monitoring (Practice 2) -- specifically, advancing questions; and Connecting (Practice 4)
- **How it shows up:** Smith & Stein distinguish assessing questions (what are you doing?) from advancing questions (can you take this further?). Press for Reasoning is the paradigmatic advancing question. During monitoring, it pushes students toward the lesson's mathematical goal. During the Connecting discussion, it surfaces the mathematical logic that makes connections between strategies visible. "Why does that work?" is the question that transforms a show-and-tell session into a mathematical discussion. The Wk06D2 slides explicitly connect monitoring to pressing: "Advancing questions can have different goals -- some may help them reconsider what they are doing, others may press them to explain their thinking."

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** During (Explore) and After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle's emphasis on "teaching through problem solving" depends on students articulating *why* strategies work, not just executing them. His approach to building conceptual understanding (as opposed to procedural fluency) is grounded in exactly this move. When Van de Walle says students should understand the meaning of operations (Ch. 9) rather than just perform them, the mechanism for building that understanding is Press for Reasoning.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others) -- this is the single strongest connection in the entire crosswalk
- **How this move supports the standard:** PS.3 says students should "construct viable arguments." Press for Reasoning is how you *teach* students to do this. Every time you ask "Why does that work?" you are telling students that claims require justification. Over time, students internalize this and begin providing reasoning without being asked. PS.2 (Reason abstractly and quantitatively) is also strongly supported -- pressing for reasoning forces students to move beyond concrete answers into abstract explanations.

**In a real lesson:** During a fact fluency lesson (Grade 2), a student says that 8 + 7 = 15. Instead of saying "Good," you ask: "How did you figure that out?" The student says: "I just knew it." You press: "Can you think about how you might explain it to someone who didn't know?" The student thinks, then says: "Well, 8 + 2 is 10, and then 5 more is 15." Now the class has access to the Make Ten strategy, and the student has articulated reasoning that can transfer to other problems.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1N -- Ms. Powers, Grade 2: "Are you sure? How do you know?" Clip 1O -- Mrs. Burgess, Grade 4: "Can you prove it?" (mystery number puzzle). Clip 1P -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 5: "Why does that work?" (volume) -- this clip beautifully shows Press for Reasoning interleaved with Who Can Repeat?, demonstrating how Steps 2 and 3 work together.

### Agree/Disagree and Why

**CDM definition:** After an idea and its reasoning have been shared and understood by the class, the teacher asks students to take a position: "Do you agree or disagree with what Paulo said? Why?" "What do you think about that?" "Does that make sense to you? Why?" This move asks students to *evaluate* another person's mathematical reasoning, not just hear it. CDM notes this can feel socially risky for students and offers strategies: use "I respectfully challenge..." instead of "I disagree," or have groups form positions together.

**CDM Step:** Step 4 -- Helping Students Engage with the Reasoning of Others

**When to use it:**
- After a student has shared reasoning AND the class has repeated/understood it (do not skip to this step too early)
- When there is a genuine mathematical disagreement worth exploring
- When a common misconception has been clearly articulated and you want the class to examine it
- When you want to surface whether students actually understand a strategy or are just nodding along
- During the Connecting discussion, after two strategies have been shared and you want students to evaluate which is more efficient or generalizable

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Connecting (Practice 4) -- this is the core mechanism
- **How it shows up:** The Connecting practice requires students to "compare strategies" and "highlight mathematical relationships between solutions." Agree/Disagree is how students begin doing this. When you ask "Do you agree with Maria's approach? Why?", you are asking students to connect Maria's strategy to their own understanding. Smith & Stein list "Ask students to compare strategies" and "Press for generalizations" as teacher moves during Connecting -- both are enacted through Agree/Disagree and Why. The Ms. D example in CDM Ch. 1 is a textbook case: Eduardo disagrees with Paulo's claim that 24 is odd, providing a mathematical argument grounded in the definition of even numbers. This IS the Connecting practice in action.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle describes the After phase as the time when "the teacher helps students make connections and see relationships." This requires students to evaluate strategies, not just passively listen. Van de Walle's emphasis on conceptual understanding over procedural compliance means students must be able to assess whether a strategy *makes mathematical sense* -- and Agree/Disagree is the move that makes this assessment visible.

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments and *critique the reasoning of others*) -- the second half of this standard
- **How this move supports the standard:** PS.3 explicitly includes "critique the reasoning of others." Agree/Disagree is the talk move that operationalizes the word "critique." Without it, PS.3 is aspirational; with it, students are actually doing the thing the standard describes. PS.7 (Look for and make use of structure) is also supported when students agree or disagree based on mathematical structure rather than gut feeling.

**In a real lesson:** During the Summarize phase of a division lesson (Grade 4), one student solved 84 / 7 by repeated subtraction (84 - 7 - 7 - 7...) and another used the relationship to multiplication (7 x 12 = 84). After both strategies are shared and repeated, you ask: "Do you agree that both of these give us the same answer? Why do they both work?" A student says: "I agree because they're both asking the same question -- how many groups of 7 fit in 84 -- just one counts down and the other counts up." This student has just articulated the inverse relationship between multiplication and division. That is PS.3 and PS.7 in one sentence.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1Q -- Mrs. Delaney, Grade 1: "Why do you agree with him?" (nickels and pennies). Clip 1R -- Mrs. Schineller, Grade 3: "Do almost all of the people do their homework before dinner?" (data analysis -- students evaluate a data claim). Clip 1S -- Mr. Danella, Grade 3: "Not to be rude but..." -- a student respectfully disagrees about a logical puzzle.

### Who Can Add On?

**CDM definition:** An open invitation for any student to contribute to the ongoing discussion: "Who can add on to what Jaylen just said?" "Does someone want to take that idea another step?" "Who has something to add?" This is the most open-ended of the talk moves. Unlike Agree/Disagree (which asks students to evaluate a specific claim), Who Can Add On? invites anyone to extend, elaborate, provide another example, or take the discussion in a new direction. CDM notes this is especially useful when a student starts an explanation but cannot finish it -- another student can pick up where they left off, and together they produce one complete mathematical argument.

**CDM Step:** Step 4 -- Helping Students Engage with the Reasoning of Others

**When to use it:**
- When a student gives a partial explanation and gets stuck
- When the discussion has enough common ground that multiple students can contribute
- When you want to shift from a teacher-student dialogue to a student-student-student discussion
- When you want to build a *collective* mathematical explanation rather than hearing from one student at a time
- At the end of a Connecting discussion, to see if students have additional insights

**Five Practices equivalent:**
- **Which practice?** Connecting (Practice 4) -- specifically, the move to build generalization
- **How it shows up:** Connecting is not just about sharing individual strategies. It is about building toward a mathematical generalization that transcends any single strategy. Who Can Add On? is the move that opens the floor for this kind of collective building. Smith & Stein describe the teacher's role as "connecting student work to the lesson's mathematical goal" -- and Who Can Add On? invites students to make those connections themselves rather than waiting for the teacher to do it. When a student says "7 x 12 works because it's the opposite of division" and you ask "Who can add on?", another student might say "So multiplication and division always undo each other, like addition and subtraction do." That second contribution is the generalization you were aiming for.

**Van de Walle equivalent:**
- **Which phase?** After (Summarize)
- **VdW's term for this:** Van de Walle emphasizes that mathematical understanding builds through the social construction of knowledge. Who Can Add On? is the move that makes this social construction visible -- multiple students contribute pieces of an explanation until the class arrives at shared understanding. This is the heart of what Van de Walle means by "teaching through problem solving."

**Indiana Standards connection:**
- **Which Process Standard?** PS.3 (Construct viable arguments) and PS.8 (Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning)
- **How this move supports the standard:** When students add on to each other's reasoning, they are collectively constructing an argument (PS.3). And when the thing they are building toward is a generalization or pattern, they are expressing regularity in repeated reasoning (PS.8). The "add on" structure also supports PS.1 (persevere in solving) because it models that mathematical thinking is iterative -- you do not have to produce a complete solution alone.

**In a real lesson:** During a fraction lesson (Grade 3), a student explains that 1/4 of a pizza is bigger than 1/6 of a pizza: "Because if you cut it into fewer pieces, each piece is bigger." You ask: "Who can add on to that?" A second student says: "It's like if you had a cookie and you shared it with 3 friends versus 5 friends -- with 3 friends everyone gets more." A third student adds: "So the bigger the bottom number, the smaller the pieces." The class has collectively built the concept of unit fraction comparison through three contributions, each building on the last.

**CDM video examples:** Clip 1T -- Mrs. Delaney, Grade 1: "Would you like to add on to the beginning of her thinking?" (a student reaches an impasse and another student helps). Clip 1U -- Ms. Powers, Grade 2: "Who can add on to that?" (linear measurement). Clip 1V -- Mrs. Foley, Grade 5: "What is a rectangular prism?" -- students collaboratively build a definition.

## Talk Move Decision Tree

*When should I use which move? Follow this flow.*

```
You asked a question. What happened?

[Silence / only one hand up]
  |
  +--> Use WAIT TIME (count to 5-10)
  |      |
  |      +--> Still silence? --> Use TURN AND TALK
  |                               |
  |                               +--> Circulate, listen, then ask:
  |                                    "Will you share that with the class?"
  |
[A student responded]
  |
  +--> Can you understand what they said?
       |
       +-- NO --> Use SAY MORE ("Can you tell us more?")
       |           |
       |           +--> Still unclear? --> Use REVOICING
       |                                  ("So you're saying ___?")
       |
       +-- YES --> Did the REST of the class hear and understand?
                   |
                   +-- NOT SURE --> Use WHO CAN REPEAT?
                   |                ("Who can put that in their own words?")
                   |
                   +-- YES --> Is the reasoning visible, or just the answer?
                               |
                               +-- JUST THE ANSWER --> Use PRESS FOR REASONING
                               |                       ("Why does that work?")
                               |
                               +-- REASONING IS VISIBLE -->
                                   Is the class ready to evaluate this idea?
                                   |
                                   +-- YES --> Use AGREE/DISAGREE AND WHY
                                   |           ("Do you agree? Why?")
                                   |
                                   +-- WANT MORE IDEAS --> Use WHO CAN ADD ON?
                                                           ("Who has something to add?")
```

**The key principle:** Move through the steps in order. Do not jump to Press for Reasoning before the idea is clear (Steps 1-2 first). Do not ask for Agree/Disagree before the class has heard and understood the claim (Step 2 first). The steps are progressive for a reason.

## Talk Moves by Lesson Phase Matrix

*Which moves are most useful during Launch, Explore, and Summarize?*

| Talk Move | Launch (Before) | Explore (During) | Summarize (After) | Notes |
|-----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|-------|
| **Wait Time** | HIGH | HIGH | HIGH | Use everywhere, always. Most teachers underuse this. |
| **Turn and Talk** | HIGH | Medium | HIGH | Crucial at Launch (helps students understand the task) and Summarize (helps students process strategies). During Explore, it happens naturally in small groups. |
| **Say More** | Medium | HIGH | HIGH | During Explore, this is your primary assessing tool as you circulate. During Summarize, it helps presenters elaborate. |
| **Revoicing** | Low | HIGH | HIGH | During Explore, use it to check your understanding of a student's strategy. During Summarize, use it to make strategies accessible to the whole class. |
| **Who Can Repeat?** | Low | Low | HIGH | This is primarily a Summarize move. After a student presents, check that the class heard it before moving on. |
| **Press for Reasoning** | Low | HIGH | HIGH | During Explore, this is your primary advancing tool. During Summarize, it pushes beyond "what I did" to "why it works." |
| **Agree/Disagree** | Low | Low | HIGH | This is almost exclusively a Summarize move. It requires ideas to be fully articulated and understood first. |
| **Who Can Add On?** | Low | Medium | HIGH | Primarily Summarize. During Explore, useful in small groups when one student is stuck. |

### Reading the Matrix

**Launch:** Your main tools are Wait Time and Turn and Talk. You are helping students understand the task and activate prior knowledge. You are NOT yet asking for deep reasoning.

**Explore:** Your main tools are Say More, Revoicing, and Press for Reasoning. You are circulating, understanding student thinking (assessing), and pushing thinking forward (advancing). This is where Monitoring happens.

**Summarize:** All moves are in play, but the progression matters. Start with Who Can Repeat? (make sure ideas are heard), then Press for Reasoning (make reasoning visible), then Agree/Disagree and Who Can Add On? (build connections). This is where Selecting, Sequencing, and Connecting happen.

## The Five Practices as a Talk Moves Sequence

Here is another way to see the relationship. Each Five Practice has a "primary talk move" that makes it work:

| Five Practice | When | Primary Talk Move(s) | What Happens |
|---------------|------|---------------------|--------------|
| **Practice 0: Goals & Tasks** | Before class | (none -- this is planning) | You decide what mathematical ideas you want students to discuss |
| **Practice 1: Anticipating** | Before class | (none -- this is planning) | You predict what students will say so you recognize it when you hear it |
| **Practice 2: Monitoring** | Explore phase | **Say More + Revoicing + Press for Reasoning** | You circulate, ask assessing questions (Say More, Revoicing) and advancing questions (Press for Reasoning) |
| **Practice 3: Selecting & Sequencing** | Transition to Summarize | (none -- this is decision-making) | You choose who presents and in what order, based on what you heard during monitoring |
| **Practice 4: Connecting** | Summarize phase | **Who Can Repeat? + Press for Reasoning + Agree/Disagree + Who Can Add On?** | You orchestrate whole-class discussion that builds connections between strategies |

## The Indiana Process Standards Through the Talk Moves Lens

| Indiana Process Standard | Primary Talk Moves That Support It | Why |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------------|-----|
| **PS.1** Make sense of problems and persevere | Wait Time, Turn and Talk | Making sense requires time to think. Perseverance requires a classroom where it is safe to be confused. |
| **PS.2** Reason abstractly and quantitatively | Press for Reasoning, Revoicing | Moving from concrete to abstract requires articulating *why* strategies work, not just *what* you did. Revoicing helps bridge informal and formal language. |
| **PS.3** Construct viable arguments and critique reasoning | Press for Reasoning, Agree/Disagree, Who Can Add On? | This is the standard most directly supported by Talk Moves. Construction = Press for Reasoning. Critique = Agree/Disagree. Collective building = Who Can Add On? |
| **PS.4** Model with mathematics | Say More, Turn and Talk | When students model, they need to explain their representations. Say More gets them to articulate *what* the model shows and *why* they chose it. |
| **PS.5** Use appropriate tools strategically | Say More, Press for Reasoning | When students choose tools (manipulatives, drawings, number lines), Ask "Say more about why you chose that tool" to make the strategic choice visible. |
| **PS.6** Attend to precision | Revoicing, Say More | Revoicing models precise mathematical language. Say More pushes students past vague answers toward precise explanations. |
| **PS.7** Look for and make use of structure | Press for Reasoning, Agree/Disagree | "Why does that work?" often reveals mathematical structure. "Do you agree?" forces students to evaluate whether a structural claim holds. |
| **PS.8** Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning | Who Can Add On?, Press for Reasoning | Building generalizations is inherently collective. Who Can Add On? lets students construct patterns together. Press for Reasoning surfaces the regularity. |

## Common Confusions and Clarifications

### "Aren't Talk Moves just the Five Practices with different names?"
No. The Five Practices is a *planning and orchestration framework* -- it tells you how to structure a lesson. Talk Moves are the *specific language tools* you use to carry out that lesson. You can know the Five Practices perfectly and still fail if you do not have the language to enact them. Conversely, you can be skilled at individual Talk Moves but waste them if you have not planned which strategies to listen for (Anticipating) or which to share (Selecting).

### "When the course says 'assessing questions' and 'advancing questions,' are those Talk Moves?"
They are categories that *contain* Talk Moves. Assessing questions (designed to surface what a student is thinking) include Say More and Revoicing. Advancing questions (designed to push thinking forward) include Press for Reasoning. The Wk06D2 slides present this distinction during the Monitoring unit.

### "Does Van de Walle use Talk Moves?"
Not by name. Van de Walle's pedagogical chapters describe the *same teaching practices* without using CDM's specific terminology. When Van de Walle says the teacher should "help students articulate their reasoning during the After phase," he means use Say More, Revoicing, Press for Reasoning, etc. The CDM framework gives you the specific words to say; Van de Walle gives you the mathematical content knowledge to understand what you are hearing.

### "I did my TAI (Think-Aloud Interview) and I mostly used Say More. Should I have used other moves?"
Say More is exactly right for a TAI. The TAI is an *assessment* context, not a teaching context. You are trying to understand what a student knows, not push them toward a learning goal. Say More and Revoicing are the moves for this. Press for Reasoning is sometimes appropriate ("Can you explain how you got that?") but Agree/Disagree and Who Can Add On? do not apply -- there is only one student.

### "Which Talk Move do I use when a student is wrong?"
Do NOT correct the student. Use this sequence:
1. **Say More** -- "Can you tell me more about that?" (maybe you misunderstood, or maybe they will self-correct)
2. **Revoicing** -- "So you're saying ___? Is that right?" (make the claim visible and check)
3. **Who Can Repeat?** -- "Who can put that in their own words?" (get the claim in front of the class)
4. **Press for Reasoning** -- "Why do you think that?" (surface the logic so the error becomes visible)
5. **Agree/Disagree** -- "Does anyone agree or disagree? Why?" (let the class provide the correction with mathematical evidence)

This is exactly what Ms. D does with Paulo's even/odd misconception in CDM Chapter 1. The misconception becomes a *learning opportunity* because every student engages with the reasoning, not just the answer.
