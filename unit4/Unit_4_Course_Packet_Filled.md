## Week 13 — Beginning Work on Fractions

### Day 1 — Area and Length Models

#### Class Discussion → BEGINNING WORK ON FRACTIONS

#### Problems 1 & 2: Area Model (Rectangle and Circle)

**Problem 1:** Rectangle representing a sub sandwich shared among 5 people. Show how much 2 people get by shading.

**Problem 2:** Circle representing a pie shared among 5 people. Show how much 2 people get by shading.

**Instructor Notes:** Students shade 2/5 of the rectangle and 2/5 of the circle. The key difference emerges in partitioning: rectangles can be easily divided into 5 equal parts using parallel horizontal or vertical cuts, while circles are much harder to partition accurately without curved cuts.

#### Reflection Questions on Problems 1 & 2

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **1. Which shape did you find easier to divide into five equal parts? Why?** | The rectangle is easier. Students can use parallel lines to create equal parts; the circle requires dividing into pie-shaped wedges, which is harder to do visually and without tools. |
| **2. Can you think of a number of parts that would be easier to make the rectangle and circle into? A number of parts that would be harder? Why?** | Powers of 2 (halves, fourths, eighths) are easier for both shapes because they involve repeated folding. Numbers like 5 or 7 are harder because they don't use simple binary subdivision. Odd denominators generally require more careful spatial reasoning. |
| **3. Which model (area, length, or set) from your textbook does each problem use? Why?** | Area model. The rectangle and circle represent the whole as a 2D region, and the fraction is shown by shading a portion of that region. This is the most natural representation for area-based wholes. |
| **4. Which "fraction construct" (measure, division, part-whole, operator, or ratio) from your textbook do these two problems involve? Why?** | Part-whole construct. We are identifying what fraction of the whole (the entire sandwich or pie) the shaded region represents. We are partitioning the whole and identifying the relationship between the part (2 people's share) and the whole (5 people total). |

#### Problems 3 & 4: Length Model (Licorice Rope)

**Problem 3:** Licorice rope shared among 4 people. Mark where one person's share ends. Test without marking the rest.

**Problem 4:** New licorice rope shared among 5 people. Mark one person's share, then redraw and repeat three times to show 3/5.

**Instructor Notes:** Problem 3 introduces measuring the unit fraction (1/4). Testing can be done by folding or iterating. Problem 4 explicitly has students iterate the unit fraction 1/5 three times, showing the crucial difference from problems 1–2: iteration is necessary here.

#### Reflection Questions on Problems 3 & 4

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **1. Which model (area, length, or set) from your textbook does each problem use? Why?** | Length model. The licorice rope is one-dimensional and is partitioned by marking points. The fraction is shown as a portion of the total length. |
| **2. Which "fraction construct" (measure, division, part-whole, operator, or ratio) from your textbook do these two problems involve? Why?** | Measurement construct. The licorice rope is partitioned into equal units (the unit fraction). We then iterate (repeat) that unit the required number of times to build fractions like 1/4, 3/5. This is a measurement approach: how many unit-fractions fit into the whole? |
| **3. Compare problem 1 & 2 to problem 3 & 4. What more in terms of "reasoning" do problems 3 & 4 involve?** | Problems 3 & 4 require iteration (repeating the unit fraction). In problems 1 & 2, students could simply shade 2/5 directly without explicitly building the fraction from unit fractions. In problems 3 & 4, students must mark the unit fraction (1/4 or 1/5) and then repeat it, showing that fractions are built by iterating a unit fraction. This is a more sophisticated understanding of fractions as multiples of unit fractions. |

#### Problems 5 & 6: Set Model (Rock Collection and Money)

**Problem 5:** John's rock collection; he brings 1/5 to school. Show the amount and justify.

**Problem 6:** Tammy has $24; Charice has 3/4 of that. Show how much money Charice has and justify.

**Instructor Notes:** These problems use the set (or discrete) model. The "whole" is no longer a continuous region or length, but a group/collection. In problem 5, the whole is the entire collection; in problem 6, the whole is the entire $24. Students find 1/5 of the collection or 3/4 of the set of 24 dollars.

#### Reflection Questions on Problems 5 & 6

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **1. Which model (area, length, or set) from your textbook does each problem use? Why? What is the whole for this kind of problem?** | Set model. We are working with a discrete collection (rocks, dollars). The whole is the entire set. A fraction names a portion of that set. |
| **2. Which "fraction construct" (measurement, division, part-whole, operator, or ratio) from your textbook do these two problems involve? Why?** | Problem 5 uses a part-whole construct (1/5 of the collection). Problem 6 uses an operator construct (3/4 of 24 means multiply the set by 3/4), which connects fractions as multipliers. |
| **3. What are some common errors for this kind of problem?** | Students may count only the shaded/selected items without identifying what the whole is. For problem 5, they might say "he brought 3 rocks" without connecting it to the whole collection. For problem 6, they might multiply 24 by 3 and divide by 4 correctly but not interpret why. Confusion about what the "whole" is (the entire set vs. a part) is very common. |

#### Problems 7, 8, 9: The Whole Matters!

**Problem 7:** Two water bottles of different sizes. Each backpacker drinks 1/3. Do they drink the same amount? (Answer: No—the whole matters.)

**Problem 8:** Jeff and Maria run different-length events. Compare 1/4 of Jeff's event to 1/2 of Maria's. Who runs farther?

**Problem 9:** Mark shares one sub with 7 people; John shares one sub with 8 people. The subs are the same size. Who gets more sub? (Answer: Mark's guests; 1/7 > 1/8.)

**What is essential for making a fraction comparison?**

The whole must be the same. 1/3 of a large water bottle is not equal to 1/3 of a small bottle. When comparing fractions, both must be expressed relative to the same whole. This is a critical foundational understanding that underlies all fraction comparison and operations.

### Day 2 — Sharing Task and Division Construct

#### Class Discussion → SHARING TASK FOR FRACTIONS

#### Sharing Task: 3 Loaves Among 5 People

**Setup:** 3 equally sized loaves of bread shared equally among 5 people.

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **1. Show how you would share the bread equally among 5 family members.** | Each loaf is divided into 5 equal pieces. Each person receives 3 pieces (one from each loaf), giving them 3/5 of a loaf total. |
| **2. Make a picture of how much each person gets that is separate from the original loaves.** | Draw a small rectangle or bar labeled as 3/5 of a loaf, showing the 3 pieces collected together. |
| **3. Compare the total amount one person gets to one loaf. What fraction are all pieces compared to a single loaf?** | Each person gets 3/5 of a single loaf. |
| **4. Compare the total to all three loaves. What fraction of three loaves does one person get?** | Each person gets 3/15 or 1/5 of the total (three loaves). Note: 3/5 + 3/5 + 3/5 = 9/5 for all three people... actually, reconsider: if 3 loaves are shared among 5 people, one person gets 3/5 of a loaf, and 3/5 of the three-loaf whole is the same as saying 3/5 of 3 = 9/5 for one person? No—one person gets 3/15 = 1/5 of the entire amount of bread (three loaves = the unit). So one person gets 1/5 of all three loaves combined. |
| **5. Can you write a division problem for this situation?** | 3 ÷ 5 = 3/5. The division construct interprets 3/5 as the quotient when 3 loaves are divided among 5 people. This is a core understanding: fractions emerge naturally from fair-sharing (division) problems. |
| **6. Compare how you made 3/5 in this problem to how you made 3/5 on Monday (problem 4). Which is more complicated for students? Why?** | Monday (Length Model): Measure a unit fraction (1/5) and iterate it three times. Today (Sharing Task): Divide 3 wholes among 5 people. More complicated: The sharing task is often harder because students must think about: (1) partitioning each loaf, (2) distributing pieces to multiple people, and (3) collecting a person's pieces into a single amount. The length model is more concrete once the unit fraction is marked. |

## Week 14 — Partitioning, Iterating, and Fraction Sequences

### Day 1 — Using Partitioning and Iterating

#### Class Discussion → USING PARTITIONING AND ITERATING TO CREATE FRACTIONS AS NUMBERS

#### Key Concepts: Partitioning and Iterating

- **Partitioning:** Dividing a whole into a specific number of equal pieces.
- **Iterating:** Repeating (counting) the unit fraction to build fractions.

**Key Insight:** The denominator tells you how many equal pieces to partition the whole into. The numerator tells you how many times to iterate the unit fraction. For example, in 2/3: the denominator 3 says "partition into thirds," and the numerator 2 says "take two of those thirds."

#### Fraction Sequences Using Fraction Bars

Groups will create sequences of fractions by partitioning and iterating.

- **Group 1 (Halves):** Create the sequence 1/2, 2/2, 3/2, 4/2, 5/2, …, 10/2 using fraction bars. Fold bars in half; cut and repeat the unit fraction (1/2) for each fraction in the sequence.

- **Group 2 (Thirds):** Create the sequence 1/3, 2/3, 3/3, 4/3, 5/3, …, 10/3 using fraction bars. Partition into thirds; iterate the unit fraction.

- **Group 3 (Fourths):** Create the sequence 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4, 5/4, …, 10/4 using fraction bars. This shows relationships between halves and fourths.

- **Group 4 (Sixths):** Create the sequence 1/6, 2/6, 3/6, 4/6, 5/6, …, 10/6 using fraction bars. This shows relationships between thirds and sixths.

- **Group 5 (Eighths):** Create the sequence 1/8, 2/8, 3/8, 4/8, 5/8, …, 10/8 using fraction bars. This shows relationships between halves, fourths, and eighths.

#### Reflection: Relationships Among Sequences

**What relationships do you notice between the sequence of halves, fourths, and eighths? Between thirds and sixths?**

- **Halves, Fourths, Eighths:** These are related by doubling the denominator. 2/2 = 4/4 = 8/8 = 1 whole. 1/2 = 2/4 = 4/8. When you double the denominator, you also double the numerator to keep the same value.
- **Thirds and Sixths:** Sixths are created by partitioning thirds in half. 1/3 = 2/6, 2/3 = 4/6, 3/3 = 6/6. These relationships are why the standards emphasize denominators 2, 3, 4, 6, 8 in 3rd grade—they are part of natural multiplicative families.

| 5/6 | Denominator = 6. Partition the whole into 6 equal pieces. |

#### Language for Decimals

**Recognize that 2/100 = 0.02. How should we say 0.02 vs. 0.20?**

- **0.02:** "Two hundredths" (or "two one-hundredths").
- **0.20 (or 0.2):** "Twenty hundredths" (or "two tenths").

These are not the same, even though in everyday speech people might say "point zero two." Using fraction language helps students understand that decimals are fractions with denominators that are powers of ten. 0.5 is "five tenths," and 0.50 is "fifty hundredths" (which equal five tenths, but the language shows the denominator).

### Day 2 — Equivalent Fractions

#### Class Discussion → Equivalent Fractions and Fraction Comparisons

#### The Lasagna Problem

**Scenario:** Two equal-sized pans of lasagna at a picnic. Parents cut each into equal pieces. Tanesha eats 2 portions from one pan; David eats 4 portions from another. They both get the same amount.

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **1. Show how the lasagna was divided so Tanesha's 2 portions equal David's 4 portions. Shade each child's portion.** | Tanesha's pan is cut into 4 equal pieces; she eats 2. David's pan is cut into 8 equal pieces; he eats 4. So 2/4 = 4/8. Other valid answers: Tanesha eats 2/6 (pan cut into 6) and David eats 4/12 (pan cut into 12); or any pair where doubling both numerator and denominator. |
| **2. Write fractions describing each portion.** | Tanesha: 2/4; David: 4/8 (or other equivalent pairs). These fractions name the same amount even though the denominators differ. |
| **3. Explain how you know they got the same amount.** | Visual comparison of shaded regions. Both children's shaded portions take up exactly half of their pan. Or: 2/4 = 1/2 and 4/8 = 1/2, so they're equal. |

#### Reflection on Multiple Answers

**Why does this problem have multiple possible answers? How are all answers connected to equivalent fractions?**

The problem has infinite correct answers because infinitely many equivalent fractions exist. 2/4 = 4/8 = 6/12 = 10/20, etc. All of these answer the question. Key idea: Equivalent fractions represent the same amount by partitioning existing pieces into smaller pieces (or combining smaller pieces into larger ones). When David's pan is cut into 8 pieces instead of 4, each original piece is split in half. So 4 pieces of size 1/8 equal 2 pieces of size 1/4.

#### Ms. Statsny's Anticipated Solutions

**Compare your anticipated solutions to Ms. Statsny's from the textbook.**

- **Key Observation:** Ms. Statsny likely anticipated correct partitioning strategies (cutting each pan into equal pieces and identifying equivalent fractions). She may not have anticipated every creative approach or incorrect solution that appeared.
- **Value of Anticipation:** Her correct anticipations helped her guide the classroom discussion by sequencing solutions and asking probing questions. When unexpected solutions appeared, her anticipations gave her a framework for evaluating them.

**Did Ms. Statsny anticipate solutions you did not? Did you anticipate solutions she missed?**

Student responses will vary. The key is recognizing that anticipation informs—but doesn't limit—instruction. Unexpected student solutions can become teaching moments if the teacher can evaluate them in the moment. This is where deep content knowledge and anticipation intersect.

## Week 15 — Equivalent Fractions, Comparisons, and Operations

### Day 1 — Equivalent Fractions and Comparison Strategies

#### Class Discussion → Equivalent Fractions, Fraction Comparisons, and Operations

#### Making Equivalent Fractions with Fraction Bars

Make the fractions below using a length model (Fraction Bars). Then find equivalent fractions by partitioning or joining pieces.

| Fraction | Solution |
|:---|:---|
| **3/4** | Make 3/4. To create equivalent fractions: partition the fourths into eighths (get 6/8), or into twelfths (get 9/12). You can also join: if fourths were part of a larger unit, 3/4 might equal other fractions. Second equivalent: 6/8 or 9/12. |
| **2/4** | Make 2/4. This equals 1/2. Partitioning into eighths gives 4/8. Joining pieces gives 1/2. Second equivalent: 1/2 or 4/8. |
| **2/3** | Make 2/3. Partition into sixths gives 4/6. Partitioning further into ninths gives 6/9. Second equivalent: 4/6 or 6/9. |
| **2/6** | Make 2/6 (which equals 1/3). Partitioning into twelfths gives 4/12. This also equals 1/3. Second equivalent: 4/12 or 1/3. |

#### Finding Common Denominators with Fraction Bars

| Question | Answer |
|:---|:---|
| **Make a whole bar. Pull out both halves and thirds. What fraction family must you use to represent both?** | To pull halves and thirds from the same whole, you must use sixths (the least common multiple of 2 and 3). 1/2 = 3/6 and 1/3 = 2/6. Now both fit into the same denominator. |
| **Make a whole bar. Pull out both thirds and fourths. What fraction family must you use?** | To pull thirds and fourths from the same whole, you must use twelfths (LCM of 3 and 4). 1/3 = 4/12 and 1/4 = 3/12. |

#### Four Strategies for Fraction Comparison

- **Strategy 1: Same Denominator.** If two fractions have the same denominator, the one with the larger numerator is larger. Example: 4/7 > 5/7? No. 5/7 > 4/7.

- **Strategy 2: Same Numerator.** If two fractions have the same numerator, the one with the smaller denominator is larger (the pieces are bigger). Example: 3/5 vs. 3/8. Since fifths are bigger than eighths, 3/5 > 3/8.

- **Strategy 3: More/Less Than a Benchmark (1/2 or 1).** Compare both fractions to a benchmark. Example: 3/4 vs. 5/9. Both are more than 1/2. 3/4 is 1/4 away from 1; 5/9 is 4/9 away from 1. So 3/4 > 5/9.

- **Strategy 4: Closeness to a Benchmark.** Which fraction is closer to 1/2 or to 1? Example: 4/9 is just less than 1/2; 3/8 is also just less than 1/2. Check distance: 4/9 is 1/18 away from 1/2; 3/8 is 1/8 away from 1/2. So 4/9 is closer.

#### Use One of These Four Strategies to Compare Each Pair (E–L)

Use a number line and write how you reasoned.

| Problem | Solution |
|:---|:---|
| **E.** 3/4 vs. 9/10 | **Benchmark 1.** Both are more than 1/2. 3/4 = 0.75; 9/10 = 0.90. So 9/10 > 3/4. (Closeness to 1: 3/4 is 0.25 away; 9/10 is 0.1 away. 9/10 is closer.) |
| **F.** 3/8 vs. 4/7 | **Benchmark 1/2.** 3/8 = 0.375 (less than 1/2); 4/7 ≈ 0.571 (more than 1/2). So 4/7 > 3/8. |
| **G.** 7/12 vs. 5/12 | **Same Denominator.** Since 7 > 5, we have 7/12 > 5/12. |
| **H.** 3/5 vs. 3/7 | **Same Numerator.** Fifths are larger pieces than sevenths, so 3/5 > 3/7. |
| **I.** 5/8 vs. 7/10 | **Benchmark 1/2.** Both are more than 1/2. 5/8 = 0.625; 7/10 = 0.70. So 7/10 > 5/8. |
| **J.** 9/8 vs. 4/3 | **Benchmark 1.** Both are greater than 1 (improper fractions). 9/8 = 1 1/8 = 1.125; 4/3 = 1 1/3 ≈ 1.333. So 4/3 > 9/8. |
| **K.** 4/6 vs. 7/12 | **Convert to common denominator or benchmark.** 4/6 = 8/12. So 8/12 > 7/12. |
| **L.** 8/9 vs. 7/8 | **Closeness to 1.** 8/9 is 1/9 from 1; 7/8 is 1/8 from 1. Since 1/9 < 1/8, 8/9 is closer to 1. Therefore 8/9 > 7/8. |

#### Fraction Multiplication Context

**John travels 2½ miles to work each day. He has traveled ¼ of the way. How far has he traveled?**

- **Setup:** This is a fraction multiplication problem: 1/4 of 2½ miles.
- **Answer:** 5/8 miles (since 1/4 × 5/2 = 5/8).
- **Different solution strategies:**
  1. Use a number line or bar model to show 2½ miles, mark 1/4 of that.
  2. Use an area model showing 1/4 of a 2½-unit length.
  3. Use a tape diagram showing the journey divided into 4 equal parts.

**Record a different classmate's solution strategy.**

Accept any correct visual representation (area model, number line, tape diagram) that shows 1/4 of 2½ miles. Strategies should be substantially different in their reasoning approach, not just the representation style.

**A student says, "I thought about this as 2½ groups of ¼ rather than taking ¼ of 2½." Make a representation for this thinking. Compare: does it match the context? How would you respond?**

- **This Student's Interpretation:** 2½ × 1/4 (multiply 2½ by 1/4, thinking of it as 2½ groups of 1/4). This is technically the same answer due to commutativity of multiplication: 1/4 × 2½ = 2½ × 1/4.
- **Context Match:** Not as natural. The problem says John traveled "1/4 of the way," which means we find 1/4 OF 2½, not 2½ OF 1/4.
- **Teacher Response:** Affirm the mathematical correctness while clarifying the language: "You're right that multiplication is commutative, so you get the same answer. But in this problem, we're finding 1/4 OF the 2½ miles, which is slightly different language. Both work mathematically, but your way of thinking is 2½ groups of 1/4 mile each, which is a different real-world picture."

### Day 2 — Fraction Addition and Subtraction

#### Class Discussion → Fraction Addition & Subtraction

#### Pattern Blocks and Unit Fractions

**Setup:** Use pattern blocks with the hexagon as the whole.

**Determine what fraction each shape is of the hexagon. Use outlines to show.**

| Shape | Fraction |
|:---|:---|
| Hexagon | 1 (the whole) |
| Trapezoid | 1/2 (two trapezoids make a hexagon) |
| Rhombus (or Diamond) | 1/3 (three rhombi make a hexagon) |
| Triangle | 1/6 (six triangles make a hexagon) |

#### Fraction Addition with Pattern Blocks

Key Idea: Use pattern blocks to model addition. Draw the blocks and write equivalent fractions to show common denominators when needed.

| Problem | Drawing & Solution |
|:---|:---|
| **1/3 + 1/2 =** | Draw 1 rhombus (1/3) and 1 trapezoid (1/2). Both fit in the hexagon. Together they cover: 1 rhombus + 1 trapezoid = 2 small triangles + 3 small triangles = 5 triangles = 5/6 of the hexagon. **Answer:** 1/3 + 1/2 = 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6 |
| **1/6 + 2/3 =** | Draw 1 triangle (1/6) and 2 rhombi (2/3). 1 triangle + 2 rhombi = 1 triangle + 4 triangles = 5 triangles = 5/6. **Answer:** 1/6 + 2/3 = 1/6 + 4/6 = 5/6 |
| **3/5 + 2/3 =** | No pattern blocks fit fifths perfectly. Use a length model (number line or bar). 3/5 + 2/3: common denominator is 15. 3/5 = 9/15; 2/3 = 10/15. Total = 19/15 = 1 4/15. **Answer:** 3/5 + 2/3 = 9/15 + 10/15 = 19/15 = 1 4/15 |
| **1 1/3 + 5/6 =** | Draw 1 hexagon + 1 rhombus for the 1 1/3, then add 5 triangles (5/6). Or: 1 + 1/3 + 5/6 = 1 + 2/6 + 5/6 = 1 + 7/6 = 1 + 1 1/6 = 2 1/6. **Answer:** 1 1/3 + 5/6 = 1 + 1/3 + 5/6 = 1 + 2/6 + 5/6 = 1 7/6 = 2 1/6 |

#### Fraction Subtraction with Pattern Blocks

Key Idea: Remove pattern blocks or compare lengths. Use equivalent fractions to show common denominators.

| Problem | Drawing & Solution |
|:---|:---|
| **1 − 1/6 =** | Draw 1 hexagon. Remove 1 triangle (1/6). Remaining: 5 triangles = 5/6. **Answer:** 1 − 1/6 = 6/6 − 1/6 = 5/6 |
| **5/6 − 1/3 =** | Draw 5 triangles (5/6). Remove 1 rhombus (which is 2 triangles or 1/3). Remaining: 5 triangles − 2 triangles = 3 triangles = 3/6 = 1/2. **Answer:** 5/6 − 1/3 = 5/6 − 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2 |
| **3/2 − 5/6 =** | Draw 1 hexagon + 1 trapezoid (3/2 = 1 1/2). Subtract 5 triangles (5/6). Convert: 3/2 = 9/6. Remove 5 triangles from 9 triangles = 4 triangles = 4/6 = 2/3. **Answer:** 3/2 − 5/6 = 9/6 − 5/6 = 4/6 = 2/3 |
| **5/8 − 1/3 =** | No pattern blocks for eighths. Use a length model (number line). Common denominator: 24. 5/8 = 15/24; 1/3 = 8/24. Difference = 7/24. **Answer:** 5/8 − 1/3 = 15/24 − 8/24 = 7/24 |

## Summary: Unit 4 Key Learning Goals

- **Three Fraction Models:** Area (rectangles, circles), Length (number lines, ropes), Set (collections, discrete objects). Each reveals different aspects of fractions.

- **Five Fraction Constructs:** Part-whole, measurement, division, operator, ratio. Problems can be framed using one or more constructs.

- **Partitioning and Iterating:** The denominator tells how many parts; the numerator tells how many times to iterate the unit fraction. This is foundational for fraction sense.

- **The Whole Matters:** Without a clear, consistent whole, fractions are meaningless. 1/3 of a large pizza ≠ 1/3 of a small pizza.

- **Equivalent Fractions:** Arise by partitioning pieces further or combining smaller pieces. Language: "Two-fourths and four-eighths name the same amount."

- **Fraction Comparisons:** Use benchmarks (1/2, 1) and reasoning strategies rather than always converting to a common denominator. This supports number sense.

- **Decimals as Fractions:** Decimals are fractions with denominators of 10, 100, 1000, etc. Use language like "five tenths" and "fifty hundredths" to connect the two notations.

- **Visual Models are Essential:** At all grade levels (3–6), support fraction understanding with length models, area models, and set models before expecting symbolic work.
