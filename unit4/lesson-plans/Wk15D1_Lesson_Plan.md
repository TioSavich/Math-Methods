## Lesson Outline

### Opening (5 min)

Display: "Which is bigger: 2/3 or 3/5?"
Take 30 seconds of individual thinking. Then ask: "How did you figure it out?" Collect 2–3 methods without evaluating.
Frame: Today, we're building fast, reliable strategies for comparing fractions. We'll also dive back into selecting and sequencing student work on division-as-fractions.

### Part A: Fraction Bar Equivalence Work (15 min)

**Setup:** Each student has fraction bars (colored strips representing halves, thirds, fourths, fifths, sixths, eighths) and blank number lines.

**Activity 1: Finding Equivalence**
- "Use your fraction bars to find all the fractions equivalent to 1/2."
- Students place 1/2 bar on the number line, then match other bars to the same distance:
  - 2/4 matches 1/2.
  - 3/6 matches 1/2.
  - 4/8 matches 1/2.
- Record: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8.
- Repeat for 1/3 or 2/3.

**Activity 2: Understanding the Pattern**
- Look at the equivalence list: 1/2, 2/4, 3/6, 4/8.
- What's changing? (Numerator and denominator both growing.)
- What stays the same? (The position on the number line; the amount.)
- Why does this pattern work? (If you partition finer—more pieces—you need to count more pieces to reach the same spot.)

**Key Insight:** Equivalent fractions are just different ways of counting pieces from the same whole, using different-sized pieces.

### Part B: Fraction Comparison Strategies (20 min)

**Setup:** Display 4 comparison problems.
1. 2/3 vs. 3/5
2. 1/2 vs. 3/8
3. 5/6 vs. 7/8
4. 1/4 vs. 2/5

**Strategy 1: Benchmark Benchmarks**
- "Is each fraction closer to 0, 1/2, or 1?"
- 2/3 is close to 1. 3/5 is close to 1/2. So 2/3 > 3/5.
- Why? We use known reference points to compare without detailed calculation.

**Strategy 2: Common Denominator**
- 2/3 = 10/15, 3/5 = 9/15. So 2/3 > 3/5.
- Why? Same-denominator fractions are easy to compare.
- Drawback: Requires finding LCD; less intuitive than benchmarks.

**Strategy 3: Using Fraction Bars**
- Place 1/2 bar and 3/8 bars on the same number line.
- Visual comparison: 1/2 bar reaches farther.
- Why? Direct visual method; very concrete but requires manipulatives.

**Strategy 4: Decimal Conversion**
- 1/2 = 0.5, 3/8 = 0.375. So 1/2 > 3/8.
- Why? Decimals are familiar and easy to compare numerically.
- Drawback: May lose fractional meaning; good for checking but less instructional.

**Student Work (10 min):**
Students try all 4 problems using 2–3 different strategies. Which feels fastest? Most reliable? Why?

**Debrief:**
- Different strategies work for different problems and contexts.
- Benchmark fractions (0, 1/4, 1/2, 3/4, 1) are especially useful because they're quick and don't require computation.
- Precision in naming matters: "2/3 is bigger than 3/5 because..." shows understanding, not just a memorized answer.

### Part C: Division-as-Fractions Student Work Analysis (20 min)

**Problem Recap: 7 Bars Among 4 People**
From last week, we analyzed this problem. Today, we revisit with an emphasis on selecting and sequencing work thoughtfully.

**Learning Goals (Displayed):**
1. Students understand that multiple strategies can share bars equally.
2. Students see that different strategies may use different unit fractions but produce the same total per person.
3. Students name the final answer relative to an appropriate whole.

**Student Work Review:**
Display 2–3 clean examples (chosen from last week's samples or new ones):

*Example A: Fair-Share Cutting*
- Draw 7 bars. Divide each into 4 equal parts.
- Each person gets 1 piece from each bar = 7 pieces of 1/4 bar = 7/4 bars = 1 3/4 bars.
- Concrete, clear, but not elegant for larger numbers.

*Example B: Strategic Distribution*
- Each person gets 1 full bar (4 given out, 3 left).
- Remaining 3 bars divided into 4 parts per person = 3/4 each.
- Total: 1 + 3/4 = 1 3/4.
- More efficient; shows strategic thinking.

*Example C: Iteration*
- Use 1/4 bar as unit. Count: "1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 (=1), 5/4, 6/4, 7/4."
- 7/4 = 1 3/4.
- Abstract, efficient, but requires comfort with improper fractions.

**Discussion:**
- Which strategy would you show first in a lesson? Why?
- Which reveals the deepest understanding?
- How would you sequence these to help students move from concrete to abstract thinking?

**Possible Sequencing:**
1. Start with Example A (fair-share cutting). Concrete; all students can visualize.
2. Compare with Example B. "Notice how strategic: each person gets a full bar first, then we share the remainder. Same answer, less drawing."
3. End with Example C or a mixed form (1 + 3/4). "We can also name this as 1 3/4 or 7/4. What's the difference?"

### Part D: Introduction to Fraction Multiplication Context (10 min)

**Preview (for next unit):**
Display: "1/2 of a 3/4 pizza. How much is that?"

Show the image or draw:
- Start with a whole pizza (1).
- Shade 3/4 of it.
- Then take 1/2 of that shaded part.
- What fraction of the whole pizza remains shaded?

**Student Thinking:**
- Visual: Students see 3/8 of the pizza is shaded (1/2 × 3/4 = 3/8).
- Conceptual: "If I have 3/4 and take half of it, I get 3/8."
- Symbolic: 1/2 × 3/4 = 3/8.

**Why This Matters:**
Fraction multiplication makes sense only if students understand fractions as amounts and can visualize taking a fractional part of a fractional amount. This week builds that foundation.

### Closing (5 min)

Summarize: Fractions can be compared using multiple strategies—benchmarks are fast, common denominators are reliable, visuals are concrete. Selecting student work thoughtfully scaffolds learning from concrete to abstract. Next week, we'll extend into fraction operations using this understanding.

- Circulate during student work analysis. Do they see the connection between different solutions?

## Differentiation & Notes

- **Struggling:** Provide pre-made fraction bars and number lines with whole numbers marked. Focus on visual comparison; skip decimal conversion or benchmark strategy initially.
- **Advanced:** Pose multi-step comparisons: "Order these from smallest to largest: 1/2, 3/5, 4/9, 7/12." Requires multiple strategies or finding a common denominator for many fractions.
- **Common Confusion:** Students often assume "more denominator = bigger fraction" (e.g., 1/8 > 1/4). Return to the visual consistently: "Which piece is bigger when you divide something into 4 parts or 8 parts?"
- **Timing:** If comparison strategies run long, assign benchmark-based comparisons as a quick homework and extend student work analysis in class.
- **Extension:** "Can you always compare fractions using benchmarks alone? When does that not work? When do you need common denominators or another strategy?" (Builds meta-cognitive awareness of strategy selection.)
