## Lesson Outline

### Opening (5 min)

Display: "Maria ate 1/2 of a pizza. Juan ate 2/4 of a pizza. Did they eat the same amount?"
Discuss: If the pizzas are the same size, yes—1/2 and 2/4 are equivalent.
Frame: Today, we're understanding why and how different fractions can be equal. We'll also watch a real teacher work with students on this idea.

### The Lasagna Problem (25 min)

**Problem Setup:**
A lasagna recipe calls for 2/3 of a cup of ricotta. Maya only has a 1/4-cup measuring cup. How many 1/4-cup measurements does she need?

**Student Work Phase (10 min):**
Students work individually or in pairs to solve. Encourage multiple representations:
- Visual: Draw lasagna or circles/rectangles divided into thirds and fourths.
- Symbolic: Write equivalent fractions (2/3 = 8/12? Compare to 1/4 = 3/12?).
- Manipulative: Use fraction bars to physically compare 2/3 and groups of 1/4.

**Anticipated Student Strategies:**
1. **Visual Partition:** Student draws 1 whole circle, divides into 3 parts, shades 2. Then divides the same circle into 4 parts and counts how many 1/4s fit into 2/3. (Visual but may not be precise.)
2. **Common Denominator:** Student finds a common denominator: 2/3 = 8/12, 1/4 = 3/12. Compares: 8/12 ÷ 3/12 = 2 2/3. (Symbolic and efficient.)
3. **Fraction Bar Iteration:** Student uses fraction bars: lines up two 1/3 bars (totaling 2/3), then counts how many 1/4 bars fit alongside. (Concrete and visual.)
4. **Decimal Bridge:** Student converts: 2/3 ≈ 0.67, 1/4 = 0.25. Divides: 0.67 ÷ 0.25 = 2.68 ≈ 2 2/3. (Numeric but less transparent.)
5. **Multiplication/Division Rule:** Student recalls "cross-multiply" or "multiply numerator by 4" without understanding. Answer correct but reasoning absent. (Procedural, may not generalize.)

**Facilitation:**
- Circulate and listen. Note which students use which strategies.
- Ask: "Show me your thinking. How do you know 2/3 needs how many 1/4 cups?"
- Don't correct; collect the strategies.

**Whole-Group Debrief (15 min):**
- Select 3–4 diverse strategies (visual, bar model, common denominator, decimal).
- Display in order of concreteness → abstraction.
- For each: "What does this student understand? What makes sense here? How is this approach connected to the others?"
- Summarize: Equivalent fractions represent the same amount but with different partitionings (different numbers of parts). We can find equivalence by using common denominators, visual models, or other strategies—they all work because they preserve the total amount.

### Video Analysis: Ms. Statsny's Classroom (15 min)

**Setup:** Show 10–15 min clip of Ms. Statsny working with students on equivalent fractions (or a similar fraction lesson from Canvas).

**During Viewing:**
Students watch and note:
- What fraction concept is Ms. Statsny teaching?
- What talk moves does she use? (Rephrase, restate, repeat, add on, apply, extend, etc.—from earlier lessons.)
- How does she help students understand equivalence?

**After Viewing (Discussion):**
- "What did you notice about how Ms. Statsny responded to student ideas?"
- "Did she tell them the answer or ask questions? What questions did she ask?"
- "How did the concrete model (if used) support student thinking?"
- "What would you do the same in your classroom? What would you do differently?"

**Key Observation:** Effective teaching of equivalence doesn't rely on rules ("just multiply the numerator and denominator by the same number"). Instead, it uses visual models and student reasoning to build deep understanding. Ms. Statsny's talk moves invite students to explain and refine their thinking.

### Fraction Equivalence Language (10 min)

**Vocabulary & Phrases:**
- "Equal to," "equivalent to" (2/3 is equivalent to 4/6).
- "The same amount," "the same size" (1/2 and 3/6 are the same amount).
- "Different names for the same number" (2/3 and 4/6 are different names for the same amount).
- "Partitioned differently" (2/3 has 3 parts total; 4/6 has 6 parts total, but each represents the same fraction of the whole).

**Precision Activity:**
Display pairs of equivalent fractions:
- 1/2 and 2/4
- 3/4 and 6/8
- 2/5 and 4/10

For each, students complete the sentence: "_____ and _____ are equivalent because..."

**Acceptable Completions:**
- "...they're the same amount."
- "...they're different names for the same number."
- "...one partitions the whole more finely, but the total is the same."
- "...if I double the numerator and denominator, I get the other fraction."

**Why Language Matters:**
Precise language helps students think clearly and communicate mathematically. "Different names for the same number" is more precise than "they're the same," and it hints at the algebraic idea that equivalent fractions are just different representations.

### Closing (5 min)

Summarize: Equivalent fractions have different numerators and denominators but represent the same amount. We can find them using visual models, common denominators, or thinking about partitioning. Precise language—"different names for the same number"—helps us communicate and remember the idea.

## Differentiation & Notes

- **Struggling:** Provide pre-made fraction bars or a number line tool. Focus on the visual approach to the Lasagna Problem.
- **Advanced:** Pose the problem in reverse: "If Maya has 2 and 2/3 cups of ricotta, how many 1/4-cup servings is that?" (Builds toward division and fractions.)
- **Common Confusion:** Students sometimes think "more parts = bigger fraction" (so 8/12 > 2/3). Use consistent visuals (number lines, fraction bars) to show they're the same. Always return to the whole.
- **Language Precision:** Some students may say "2/3 is bigger than 1/4" without specifying they mean "in the same context" or "when the whole is the same." Gently correct: "Right, and that's because...?"
- **Timing:** If video analysis extends, assign reflection as homework and use the extra time for Lasagna Problem discussion.
- **Extension:** For advanced students, explore why 2/3 = 4/6 = 6/9 = 8/12 = ... (infinite equivalent fractions). What's the pattern?
