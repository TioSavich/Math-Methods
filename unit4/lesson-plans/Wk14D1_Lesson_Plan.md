# Week 14, Day 1 - Unit Fractions, Partitioning & Iterating, Fractions to Decimals

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand unit fractions (1/n) as the foundation for all fractions
2. Distinguish between partitioning (dividing a whole into equal parts) and iterating (repeating a unit fraction)
3. Connect fractions to decimal notation and place value
4. Build fluency moving between fractional, decimal, and decimal notation

- Unit 3 Course Packet (pp. 25–30)

- Colored fraction strips (pre-made or student-folded)
- Rulers and markers

## Lesson Outline

### Opening (5 min)

Display: "What is a unit fraction?"
Elicit: A unit fraction has a numerator of 1. Examples: 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/8.
Frame: Today, we're learning that all fractions are built from unit fractions. If you understand 1/4, you can understand 3/4 (three 1/4s).

### Part A: Folding Activity—Building Unit Fractions (20 min)

**Setup:** Each student gets a long strip of paper and a colored strip (the unit).

**Directions:**
1. Draw a number line the entire length of your long paper. Mark 0 on the left.
2. Use the colored strip (1 unit) to measure out 5 wholes on your number line. Mark 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5.
3. Fold and cut:
   - **Halves (green strip):** Fold colored strip in half; cut. Label each piece "1/2." You now have 10 halves to place on a new number line (0, 1/2, 1, 1 1/2, 2, ...).
   - **Thirds (pink):** Fold into thirds; cut. Label "1/3." Place 10 thirds on a number line.
   - **Fourths (orange):** Fold into fourths. Label "1/4." Place 10 fourths on a number line.
   - **Sixths (yellow):** Fold into sixths. Label "1/6." Place 10 sixths on a number line.
   - **Eighths (blue):** Fold into eighths. Label "1/8." Place 10 eighths on a number line.

**Key Pedagogy:**
- Folding is tactile and visual. Students see that 2 halves make a whole, 3 thirds make a whole, etc.
- Number lines show fractions as measures/distances from 0.
- The unit (the whole) is visually constant—only the partitioning changes.

**Circulate:** Are students folding accurately? Are they labeling clearly? Are they placing strips in order on the number line?

### Part B: Discovering Equivalence & Comparison (15 min)

**Question 1: Equivalence**
- "Using your number lines, find three pairs of fractions that are the same length/position."
- Example: 2/4 and 1/2 both reach the 1/2 mark. 3/6 and 1/2 also reach the 1/2 mark.
- Record: 2/4 = 1/2 = 3/6.
- **Why?** They occupy the same position on the number line. They represent the same amount.

**Question 2: Comparison with Same Denominator**
- "Compare 2/5 and 3/5. Which is longer? Why?"
- Students use number lines to see that 3/5 reaches farther than 2/5.
- **Why?** More unit fractions (more 1/5s) = larger amount.

**Question 3: Comparison with Same Numerator**
- "Compare 1/4 and 1/6. Which is longer? Why?"
- Students see that 1/4 reaches farther than 1/6 on the number line.
- **Why?** When you divide a whole into 4 parts, each part is bigger than when you divide into 6 parts.

**Discussion:**
- What did you notice about fractions with the same denominator? (Larger numerator = larger amount.)
- What did you notice about fractions with the same numerator? (Larger denominator = smaller amount.)
- When can we compare fractions? (When the whole is the same size.)

### Part C: Fractions to Decimals with Base-Ten Blocks (20 min)

**Bridge to Decimals:**
Display a base-ten flat (represents 1 whole or 100 small unit squares).

**Activity:**
- "A flat is a whole. A rod (10 units) is 1/10 of a flat. A unit is 1/100 of a flat."
- Fraction names: 1/10 = 0.1, 1/100 = 0.01.
- Decimal notation: We're dividing by powers of 10.

**Examples (hands-on):**
1. Show 1/4 with base-ten blocks:
   - The flat represents the whole.
   - Divide mentally into 4 parts: Each part is 25 unit cubes.
   - 1/4 = 0.25 (25 of the 100 units).
   - Why? 1/4 = 25/100.

2. Show 1/5 with base-ten:
   - The flat is the whole.
   - 1/5 = 20/100 = 0.20 = 0.2.
   - Why? Divide 100 by 5 to get 20.

3. Show 3/4 with base-ten:
   - 3/4 = 75/100 = 0.75.
   - Iterate the unit fraction: three 1/4s = three 25-unit piles.

**Student Practice:** Provide 5–6 fraction-decimal pairs (1/2 & 0.5, 1/10 & 0.1, 3/5 & 0.6, etc.). Students represent each with base-ten blocks and name both the fraction and decimal.

**Key Insight:** Decimals are fractions with denominators that are powers of 10. Base-ten blocks make this concrete.

### Part D: Connections & Closing (10 min)

**Synthesis Questions:**
- "What's a unit fraction? How do they build other fractions?"
- "Why does the whole matter?"
- "How are fractions and decimals related?"

**Informal:**

- Fraction equivalence findings (written pairs).

## Differentiation & Notes

- **Struggling:** Provide pre-made fraction strips. Focus on the number line work and one comparison at a time.
- **Advanced:** Challenge: "Why is 1/3 approximately 0.33? Can you show this with base-ten blocks?" (Leads to repeating decimals and rounding.)
- **Common Confusion:** Students may confuse larger denominators with larger amounts. Use consistent language: "When you have more pieces, each piece is smaller." The number line makes this visible.
- **Timing:** If folding takes longer than 20 minutes, provide pre-folded strips and extend number line work.
- **Accessibility:** For students with motor challenges, provide pre-cut strips or digital manipulatives (fractionlab.com or similar).
- **Real-World Connection:** "Recipes often use fractions and decimals. 1/4 cup is sometimes labeled as 0.25 cups. Why is that useful?"
