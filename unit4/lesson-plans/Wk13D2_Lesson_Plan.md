# Week 13, Day 2 - The Whole in Fractions & Division as Fractions

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand that the whole (the unit) is critical when naming fractional amounts
2. Connect division with remainders to fractional notation (e.g., 359 ÷ 7 = 51 2/7)
3. Analyze and select student work on a division-as-fractions problem
4. Practice anticipating and sequencing student reasoning

## Lesson Outline

### Opening (5 min)

Display: "If 1/2 of a pizza and 1/2 of a personal pizza are on the table, are they the same amount?"
Discuss: No—the whole (the pizza size) matters. The fraction only makes sense relative to the whole.
Frame: Today, we're diving deep into this critical idea and showing how division naturally produces fractions.

### Problems 7–9: The Whole Matters (15 min)

**Handout Problems 7–9** (from previous lesson or at-home)
- Problem 7: "Rosa has 1/2 of a large pizza. Josh has 1/2 of a small pizza. Who has more?" (Answer depends on pizza sizes.)
- Problem 8: "Draw a model where 1/3 is a small amount and another model where 1/3 is a large amount." (Same fraction, different referent wholes.)
- Problem 9: "Can we compare 1/4 of a chocolate bar with 1/4 of a dollar?" (Only if we know the sizes of the whole bar and whole dollar. They're different wholes.)

**Facilitated Discussion:**
- Group students; assign each group a problem.
- Groups work 7 minutes, then present.
- Listen for: Do they recognize that the fraction name alone (1/2, 1/3, 1/4) doesn't tell the actual amount?
- Summarize: Fractions are relative. We always need to know the referent whole.

### Division as Fractions (20 min)

**Context Problem: 3 Loaves Among 5 People** (VdW pp. 341–350)
- Whole problem display: "Three loaves of bread need to be shared equally among 5 people. How much does each person get?"
- This is a division problem: 3 ÷ 5 = ?
- But the answer is a fraction: each person gets 3/5 of a loaf.

**Modeling Approaches (students try two):**

*Approach 1: Fair-Share Cutting (Area Model)*
- Draw 3 rectangles (loaves).
- Divide each into 5 equal parts (horizontally).
- Each person gets 1 piece from each loaf = 3 pieces = 3/5 loaves.
- Key: We fractioned the loaves to ensure equal shares.

*Approach 2: Fraction Iteration (Length Model)*
- Draw a number line for 0 to 1 loaf per person.
- Show that 1/5 of a loaf is one "bite" or share from each loaf.
- Each person gets 3 bites = 3/5 loaves.
- Key: The unit (1 loaf) was the referent whole, and we iterated the unit fraction.

**Connection:**
- In notation: 3 ÷ 5 = 3/5 (division creates fractions!).
- The remainder from integer division becomes the numerator; the divisor becomes the denominator.
- Example: 359 ÷ 7 = 51 remainder 2 = 51 2/7 (the whole is 1 loaf; each loaf is divided into 7 parts, and 2 parts are left).

### Student Work Analysis (20 min)

**Problem: 7 Bars Among 4 People** (Selecting & Sequencing)
- Display 7 different student solutions (from handout or previously provided).
- Students work in pairs; each pair analyzes 2 solutions.
- Focus questions:
  - What strategy does this student use? (cutting, iterating, other?)
  - How does the student name the answer? (1 3/4, 1.75, other?)
  - What does the student understand? What's missing?

*Sample Student Work Strategies:*
1. Fair-share cutting: Draw 7 rectangles, divide into 4 parts each. Count pieces per person: 1 full bar + 3/4. Clear and concrete.
2. Iteration: Mark the unit (1 bar), then show each person gets 1 full bar; remaining bar cut into 4 parts per person. Efficient.
3. Partial confusion: Student cuts each bar into 4 parts, counts pieces, but doesn't name the whole correctly (e.g., says "28 fourths" without recognizing it as 1 3/4).
4. Decimal approach: Student reasons 7 ÷ 4 = 1.75. Correct but not yet fraction-named.
5. Mixed visual-symbolic: Student draws the fair shares AND writes fractions, showing connection between the two.
6. Oversimplification: Student gives one piece to each person, says "1/7 each," misunderstanding the problem.
7. Strategic insight: Student recognizes each person gets 1 full bar + a fraction of the remaining bar.

**Facilitation:**
- Display each sample.
- Ask pairs: Which strategies are mathematically sound? Which show deep understanding? Which would you use in a lesson?
- Discuss: Why might a student approach this one way rather than another?

### Student Work Selection & Sequencing (10 min)

**Your Turn:** If you were teaching this lesson and wanted students to understand 3/4 as both a fractional share and an iterated unit fraction, which student work would you show? In what order?

**Possible Sequencing Logic:**
- Start with the clearest fair-share cutting (Strategy 1). Concrete, visual.
- Add the iteration approach (Strategy 2). Connects to measurement meaning of fractions.
- Compare Strategies 4 and 5 (decimal vs. mixed fraction). Discuss: Are they saying the same thing?
- End with Strategy 3 (partial confusion). "What's missing here? How could we help this student?"

**Why does order matter?**
- Early strategies build shared understanding.
- Comparing two strategies deepens thinking.
- Ending with a "misconception opportunity" invites problem-solving.

### Closing (5 min)

Summarize: Fractions name an amount relative to a whole. Division with remainders naturally produces fractions. Student work varies—it shows different ways of thinking. Selecting and sequencing work thoughtfully shapes what students notice and learn.

## Differentiation & Notes

- **Struggling:** Provide templates for fair-share drawings. Pair with a partner who can model the cutting process.
- **Advanced:** Challenge: "If we cut the 3 loaves into 10 equal parts instead of 5, how much does each person get?" (Leads to decimal thinking or equivalence.)
- **Common Confusion:** Students often name a fraction without considering the whole. When they say "1/4," ask, "1/4 of what?" Always return to the referent whole.
- **Timing:** If student work analysis is running long, assign strategies 1, 2, 5 (clearest) and defer strategies 3, 6 (misconceptions) to the next session or reflection homework.
- **Extension:** After this lesson, introduce the notation for mixed numbers (1 3/4) formally and contrast with improper fractions (7/4). Both represent the same amount but in different forms.
