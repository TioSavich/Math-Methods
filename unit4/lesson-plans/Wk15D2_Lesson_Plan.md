### Part B: Real-World Resources & Realistic First-Year Teaching (15 min)

**Free, High-Quality Curricula** (Slides 5–6, handout)

*Illustrative Math (IM):*
- Free, problem-based K–12 curriculum aligned to state and national standards.
- Strengths: Develops ideas systematically, spiraled, balances procedural and conceptual fluency.
- Potential drawback: Paper-heavy (but that's an opportunity to add manipulatives, class discussions, and creativity).
- When you have your own classroom: Explore IM for your grade level. It's worth it.
- Website: illustrativemathematics.org

*DREME (Development and Research on Early Math Education):*
- Focus on Pre-K and early elementary.
- Offers research, activities, and professional learning resources for free.
- Great for understanding the foundations of number sense and early operations.
- Website: dremea.org

**Why These?**
- Both are freely available and high-quality.
- Both prioritize understanding over rote procedures.
- Both align with modern state standards.
- Both offer flexibility to add your own context and culturally relevant problems.

**Realistic First-Year Expectations** (Slide 6)

*What Counts as Success:*
- Implementing 5–10 complete, well-designed lessons: You're doing great.
- 15–20 lessons: Excellent work.
- 20+ lessons: Above and beyond.

*The Reality:*
- You won't implement a full year of lessons like the ones we designed (and that's okay).
- You'll adapt. You'll improvise. You'll learn what works with YOUR students.
- The goal is quality over quantity. Five thoughtful lessons beat 50 rushed ones.

*Regularly Using Instructional Tools:*
- Using ONE of the tools we discussed consistently (talk moves, anticipating student work, goal-writing, selecting/sequencing student work) = success.
- Examples:
  - "This year, I'll deliberately practice talk moves. Every lesson, I'll use at least one rephrase or restate."
  - "I'll write learning goals for every unit and share them with students."
  - "I'll look at student work before the lesson to anticipate misconceptions."
- Consistency beats perfection.

*Building Relationships:*
- Get to know your students well. Good relationships are ESSENTIAL.
- They're the foundation for everything else.
- Parents care about what their kids learn, not just grades. Show them progress in thinking, problem-solving, and mathematical reasoning.

*Embrace Imperfection:*
- Your lessons won't be perfect.
- You'll make mistakes.
- Students will surprise you with thinking you didn't anticipate.
- That's all normal. That's teaching.

### Part C: Wrap-Up Problem & Discussion (25 min)

**Unit 4 Problem (Handout)** — Equivalent Fractions Context

Display the culminating problem (from Unit 4 Course Packet):
"Sam has 1/2 of a pizza. Maria has 3/6 of the same pizza. Do they have the same amount? Explain using a visual model and words."

**Student Work (8 min):**
- Work individually or in pairs.
- Show your thinking using a picture.
- Write an explanation in words.

**Gallery Walk or Whole-Group Share (10 min):**
- Post student work around the room or share 3–4 examples.
- Each: "What does this solution show? Is it correct? Why?"
- Discuss the variety of approaches:
  - Some students use area models (circles, rectangles).
  - Some use linear models (number lines).
  - Some use symbolic reasoning (1/2 = 3/6 because doubling numerator and denominator).
- Celebrate the range of valid approaches.

**Big Takeaway Discussion:**
- This problem (and fractions in general) illustrates what the whole semester has been about.
- We don't teach procedures (e.g., "multiply numerator and denominator by the same number").
- We teach understanding through visual models, student reasoning, and precise language.
- Different students understand differently, and that's okay—the models and discussions bring everyone along.

### Part D: Big Takeaways & Celebration (10 min)

**Reflect on the Course:**

*You've learned:*
1. How children develop number sense and understanding of operations.
2. How to design lessons using the launch-explore-summarize structure.
3. How to use the 5 Practices (anticipating, monitoring, selecting, sequencing, connecting) to facilitate student thinking.
4. How to use talk moves (rephrase, restate, repeat, add on, apply, extend) to guide and support student reasoning.
5. Content knowledge: counting, addition/subtraction, multiplication/division, and fractions—with deep understanding, not surface-level facts.
6. How to look at student work, see the mathematics in it, and use it instructionally.

*These skills transfer:*
- To any grade level, any content area.
- To a school that uses any curriculum.
- To your own teaching journey, wherever it takes you.

## Closing Notes

This semester was intensive. You've done the work—reading carefully, designing lessons, analyzing student thinking, and reflecting on your own learning. That's not small. As you move into student teaching and beyond, hold onto:

1. **Deep understanding over procedures.** Always ask: Why does this work? How do students make sense of it?
2. **Student thinking over your teaching.** Pay attention to what students are actually thinking, not what you planned to teach.
3. **Progress over perfection.** You don't have to be a perfect teacher on day one. Growth comes from reflection and trying again.
4. **Your students matter most.** Build relationships. Know them. Care about them. Everything else follows.

You're ready. Go teach.
