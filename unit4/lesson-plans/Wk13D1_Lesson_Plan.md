# Week 13, Day 1 - Finishing Division & Beginning Fractions

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Review sharing and measurement division with three-digit dividends
2. Understand the difference between sharing (partition) and measurement (quotative) division through concrete modeling
3. Connect division remainders to fractional parts, setting up the transition to fractions
4. Model two-step problem-solving with base ten blocks and open arrays

## Materials

- Unit 3 Course Packet (pp. 15-20)
- Base ten blocks (flats, rods, ones cubes)
- Large grid paper or open array diagrams
- PowerPoint: Wk13D1_MultFact-Algorithm
- Problem handout with sharing and measurement division examples

## Lesson Outline

### Opening (5 min)

Display Slide 2 (Agenda: Large Division). Ask students: "What's the difference between sharing 12 cookies among 3 friends versus putting 12 cookies into bags of 3?" Elicit responses. Establish that both are division, but the unknown is different—this is the day's focus.

### Sharing Division - Modeling (15 min)

**Example: The Bake Sale Problem** (Slide 4)
- $369 shared fairly among 3 classrooms. How much does each classroom get?
- Students work in pairs with base ten blocks: 3 hundreds, 6 tens, 9 ones.
- Action: Distribute hundreds first (give each of the 3 groups one hundred; 0 left). Record: 1 hundred per group.
- Then tens: give each group 2 tens (0 left). Record: 2 tens per group.
- Then ones: give each group 3 ones (0 left). Record: 3 ones per group.
- Each classroom gets $123.
- **Key emphasis:** Draw the original total and the number of groups separately. Show the distribution step-by-step.

**Debrief:** Why did we distribute hundreds first? (They're the biggest.) What if we had a remainder? (That's next week—fractional parts.)

### Measurement Division - Modeling (15 min)

**Example: The Erasers Problem** (Slide 6)
- 329 erasers in bags of 7. How many bags?
- Different unknown: we know the group size (7), need to find how many groups.
- Students use open arrays or a table to organize: Draw 7 across, repeat down; or track: "7 goes into 329 how many times?"
- Model: Take 1 bag (7), check remainder. Take another. Count how many times before you run out.
- Record: 329 ÷ 7 = 47 bags (remainder 0).
- If remainder exists, note it but don't fraction it yet.
- **Key emphasis:** This is "quotative" or "measurement" division—we measure out groups of the known size.

**Debrief:** Why is this different from sharing? (We know the size of each group, not how many groups.) When would we use measurement division in real life?

### Comparison & Connection (10 min)

Display both problems side-by-side. Have students articulate:
- Sharing division: number of groups known, group size unknown.
- Measurement division: group size known, number of groups unknown.
- Both are division. The model we choose (base ten blocks vs. open array) supports the interpretation.
- Both can have remainders—which become fractional parts.

Highlight the notation: VdW convention is 4 × 8 means "four sets of eight." Same idea: in 369 ÷ 3, the 3 is the divisor (number of groups for sharing, or group size for measurement).

### Student Practice (15 min)

**Problems 1–3 from Handout**
- Problem 1: Sharing context (base ten blocks).
- Problem 2: Measurement context (open array or table).
- Problem 3: Mixed context or word problem.
Students work in pairs. Circulate: Are they using concrete models first? Are they recording accurately?

**Extension (if time):** Problem 4—a problem with remainder. How should we record the remainder? (Whole number with remainder, e.g., 47 R 2. Fractionalization comes next week.)

### Closing (5 min)

Summarize: Sharing and measurement are both division. Concrete models (base ten, arrays) help us understand what we're doing. Remainders become fractional parts—the bridge to fractions. Next session, we'll formalize this and introduce fraction notation.

## Differentiation & Notes

- **Struggling:** Provide pre-made base ten block diagrams or partially completed open arrays. Pair with strong partners.
- **Advanced:** Challenge with remainders early. Ask: What does the remainder mean? Can we represent it another way? (Foreshadow fractions.)
- **Common Confusion:** Students often confuse sharing and measurement division. Use the same problem with two questions: "How many does each person get?" (sharing) vs. "How many people can get 3?" (measurement).
- **Timing:** If base ten modeling runs long, show worked examples for measurement division and let students focus on open arrays.
