# Week 10, Day 1 - Fact Fluency and Number Talks

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand the features of fact fluency: accuracy, automaticity, and flexibility
2. See number talks as a classroom structure that supports fact fluency
3. Understand and practice key teacher moves in number talks: eliciting, listening, modeling, connecting
4. Practice assessing fact knowledge and identifying instructional next steps

## Lesson Outline

### Opening / Fact Fluency Discussion (15 min)

Display Slide 3. Ask: "What does it mean to 'know' a fact? Is 7 + 3 = 10 something you know, or something you figure out?"

Collect responses. Establish three features of fact fluency:

1. **Accuracy:** Gets the correct answer
2. **Automaticity:** Retrieves or constructs the answer without counting on fingers or overt strategy use (within a few seconds)
3. **Flexibility:** Can think about the fact in multiple ways and use related facts (e.g., knowing 7 + 3 helps you figure out 7 + 4)

Clarify: Fluency is not memorization. A student who flexibly uses 7 + 3 = 10 to figure out 7 + 4 by thinking "that's one more" is demonstrating fluency, even if they didn't memorize 7 + 4.

Show Slide 4. Discuss: Why should we care about flexibility and not just speed? (Because rigid, fast facts without understanding don't transfer; flexible facts support problem-solving and mental math.)

### Number Talks as a Structure (5 min)

Introduce Slide 5. Number talks are brief (5-15 min), whole-group oral activities where:
- Teacher presents one problem (typically on a number line, with dot cards, or on a board)
- Students think silently for a few seconds
- Students share strategies (not just answers)
- Teacher writes down strategies without judgment
- Class discusses connections between strategies

Purpose: Make thinking visible, build strategy flexibility, practice fact knowledge in a low-pressure setting.

### Modeling a Number Talk: 7-Dot Card (30 min)

Hold up a 7-dot card (Slide 6). Say: "Look at this card for about 3 seconds. What do you see?"

Have students turn to a partner and say what they see (1 min). Then take 3-4 responses aloud. Common answers: "seven dots," "five and two," "four and three," "six and one."

**Key teaching move:** Accept all responses without judgment. All are correct ways of seeing the card.

Now say: "I'm going to show you this card again, but I'm going to cover part of it with my hand. I want you to figure out how many dots I'm covering."

Show the 7-dot card with 4 dots visible and 3 covered (or vary this). Ask: "How many are hiding?" Students think silently (5 sec), then share their strategy.

Record each strategy on the board. Examples:
- "I know 7, and I see 4, so 3 are hiding" (counting up or recognizing the part)
- "I see 4 and I know that 4 + 3 = 7" (fact knowledge)
- "I see the 5 and 2, the hand is covering part of the 5, so 3 are hiding" (visual-spatial decomposition)

Spend 10 minutes on this activity, varying which dots are covered and allowing multiple strategies.

**Key teaching move:** Name the strategies as you write them. "You're using counting up." "You're using a fact you know." This helps students see the range of thinking available.

### Video: 8 + 6 Number Talk (15 min)

Show video of a teacher conducting a number talk with 8 + 6. Students watch and note:
- What strategies do students use?
- What does the teacher do when students share a wrong answer?
- What connections does the teacher make between strategies?

Pause video partway through (after 4-5 strategies have been shared) and ask: "What's going on? What strategies are students using?" Take responses.

Finish the video. Debrief (3-4 min):
- What were the strategies? (e.g., "8 and 2 more to make 10, then 4 more," "6 and 4 to make 10, then add 8," "8 + 8 - 2," "count up from 8")
- What did the teacher do? (Drew diagrams, wrote equations, asked "does that work?," linked strategies to previous ones)
- How does this build fact knowledge? (Students see that 8 + 6 can be solved multiple ways; they learn new strategies from peers; they practice fluency without timed pressure)

### Student-Led Modeling: Subtraction Number Talk (20 min)

Say: "Now we're going to run a number talk together, and I'm going to be quiet for most of it."

Present a subtraction problem on the board: **15 - 7 = ?**

Tell students: Silent think time, then share your strategy with the person next to you (2 min).

Call on 4-5 students to share strategies. Write each on the board without comment. Ask follow-up questions (e.g., "Can you say that again?" "Does anyone see that differently?").

After 4-5 strategies, ask: "Do you see any strategies that use the same idea?" Help students see connections (e.g., "Both of those students counted up from 7").

**Key teaching moves to practice:**
- Wait after asking a question (don't rush to fill silence)
- Revoice without correcting: "So you're saying 7 + 8 = 15, so 15 - 7 = 8?"
- Draw diagrams even if students don't ask
- Link strategies: "How is that like what [student name] said?"

Debrief your teaching (2-3 min): Ask the pre-service teachers, "What was I doing as the teacher?" Reinforce: listen, don't evaluate, draw, connect, make thinking visible.

### Assessing Fact Knowledge and Wrap-Up (5 min)

Display Slide 15. Talk briefly about assessment: Number talks give informal data (what strategies are students using? are they flexible? do they retrieve or construct?). Informal observation is valuable. Some teachers also use fact fluency assessments (fact fluency checks, timed or untimed) to identify which facts need more practice.

Frame the real work: "Once you see where students are, you design instruction. A number talk with 6 + 8 and 8 + 6 next to each other helps students see the commutative property. A number talk with 7 + 3 and 7 + 4 builds flexibility."

## Differentiation & Notes

- If students are hesitant to share in the subtraction number talk, offer a "fist to five" non-verbal response first (hold up fingers 1-5 to show confidence), then ask who used each strategy
- If the group quickly masters 15 - 7, move to a fact with larger numbers or a subtraction fact that requires more decomposition (e.g., 13 - 8)
- If students are struggling to articulate strategies, model self-talk: "Let me tell you how I thought about 15 - 7: I know 15 - 5 = 10, so I need to take away 2 more. 10 - 2 = 8. So 15 - 7 = 8."
- The video should be chosen to match grade level (K-2 emphasis). If the provided video is unavailable, any number talk video showing clear strategy sharing and teacher moves will work
- After this lesson, number talks become an ongoing classroom practice, so planting them here is essential; returning to number talks in Weeks 11-12 will deepen understanding
