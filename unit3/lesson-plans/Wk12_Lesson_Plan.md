# Week 12, Days 1-2 - Division Strategies and Division Algorithm

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~150 minutes total (two 75-minute sessions)

## Goals

1. Distinguish sharing (partition) division from measurement (quotative) division
2. Model both division types using concrete materials and visual representations
3. Understand and teach the division algorithm as a notation for strategies
4. Build student fluency with division facts and multi-digit division

## Lesson Outline - Day 1 (75 minutes)

### Opening: Two Types of Division (5 min)

Display Slide 3. Ask: "What does division mean? If I say '15 ÷ 3,' what story could I tell?"

Take 2-3 responses. Guide students to see that there are two fundamentally different stories:
- **Sharing (Partition):** "I have 15 cookies to share fairly among 3 friends. How many does each friend get?" (15 ÷ 3 = 5 each)
- **Measurement (Quotative):** "I have 15 cookies and I want to put 3 cookies in each bag. How many bags can I make?" (15 ÷ 3 = 5 bags)

Same division problem, different meaning. Both are "15 ÷ 3," both give the answer 5, but the context is different.

Clarify: In sharing, we know how many groups and find the size of each group. In measurement, we know the size of each group and find how many groups.

### Modeling Sharing Division: 4th Grade Level (15 min)

**Problem: 48 ÷ 3** (Context: 48 apples shared fairly among 3 students. How many apples does each student get?)

**Step 1: Set up with base ten blocks**

Show 48 as 4 flats (tens) and 8 rods (ones). Say: "I'm going to share these among 3 students. I want each student to get the same amount."

**Step 2: Distribute**

- Take the 4 flats. "One flat for the first student, one for the second, one for the third, one more for the first." You've distributed 3 flats; 1 flat is left.
- Break up the leftover flat into rods. Now you have 8 rods (original) + 10 rods (from the flat) = 18 rods.
- Distribute the 18 rods among 3 students: 6 rods each.
- Total: Each student gets 1 flat (10) + 6 rods (6) = 16

So 48 ÷ 3 = 16.

**Step 3: Connect to notation**

Write on the board:

```
      16
   -------
3 | 48
    3↓    (3 students, each gets 1 ten)
   ----
    18    (after distributing tens, 18 ones left)
    18    (3 students × 6 ones each)
   ----
     0
```

Narrate: "We start with 48. We give away tens first. 3 goes into 4 once, so each student gets 1 ten. That uses 3 tens, leaving 1 ten. We break that 1 ten into 10 ones, so now we have 18 ones to distribute. 3 goes into 18 six times, so each student gets 6 ones. No remainder."

### Modeling Measurement Division: 4th Grade Level (15 min)

**Problem: 48 ÷ 4** (Context: 48 apples, 4 apples per bag. How many bags can we make?)

**Step 1: Set up with base ten blocks**

Show 48 as 4 flats and 8 rods. Say: "I'm making bags of 4 apples. How many bags will I have?"

**Step 2: Group and count**

- Each flat is 10 apples. How many bags of 4 can I make from one flat? "I can make 2 bags of 4 with 2 apples left over." (10 = 2×4 + 2)
- From 4 flats, I can make 4 × 2 = 8 bags, with 4 × 2 = 8 apples left over.
- Now I have 8 (original rods) + 8 (leftover from flats) = 16 apples left to group into bags of 4.
- 16 ÷ 4 = 4 more bags.
- Total: 8 + 4 = 12 bags.

So 48 ÷ 4 = 12.

**Step 3: Connect to notation**

Write on the board:

```
      12
   -------
4 | 48
    4↓    (groups of 4 in the 40)
   ----
     8    (apples left from 40)
     8    (remaining 8 from the ones)
   ----
     0
```

Narrate: "We're making bags of 4. How many bags from 40? That's 10 ÷ 4 = 2 bags with 2 left. But we're in the tens place, so we got 2 tens (20 apples) in 2 bags, with 2 tens (20 apples) left. Add that to the 8 ones: 20 + 8 = 28. How many bags of 4 from 28? That's 7 bags. Total: 2 + 7 = 9 bags. Wait, let me recompute…"

(Use this as an authentic moment to show students that division notation can be confusing. The "long division" algorithm is efficient but abstract. Concrete work makes it clearer.)

### Key Distinction: Sharing vs. Measurement (5 min)

Display Slide 10. Use a simple example (12 ÷ 3):
- **Sharing:** 12 items, 3 groups. Each group has __ items. (Answer: 4 per group. This is division as partitioning.)
- **Measurement:** 12 items, __ groups of 3. How many groups? (Answer: 4 groups. This is division as repeated subtraction or grouping.)

Same division, but the teacher's language and the real-world context determine which one the student is imagining. Both should be taught because both appear in problems students encounter.

### Whole-Class Discussion: Algorithm Notation (5 min)

Show Slide 11 (or draw an example). Discuss:
- Long division notation is efficient but hides the thinking
- The steps inside (estimating how many groups, subtracting, bringing down) are strategies, not magic
- Different students might organize the work differently (some might use multiple steps to divide the tens; others might break into smaller groups)

### Guided Practice: Student-Led Division Problem (10 min)

Present a new problem: **56 ÷ 4** (sharing context: 56 cookies, 4 friends, how many each?)

Tell students: "You have base ten blocks. Build 56 and show me how you'd distribute it among 4 people. We'll walk through it together."

Observe. Ask guiding questions:
- "How many tens can each person get?"
- "What's left over?"
- "Can we break a flat into rods to share those?"

Record the work on the board in algorithm notation as they share (or walk them through the notation after the concrete work).

### Closing Day 1 (5 min)

Recap: We've modeled two types of division (sharing and measurement) using concrete materials. We saw that the algorithm notation records the steps of this process. Tomorrow we'll practice the algorithm on our own and work with larger numbers.

## Lesson Outline - Day 2 (75 minutes)

### Opening Review (5 min)

Quick review: "Yesterday we divided 48 into 3 equal groups. Each group got 16. Let's see that again for 10 seconds." Show the base ten block setup from Day 1 or a drawing of it.

Then ask: "Today we're going to do more division. You're going to use the notation we practiced yesterday and solve problems on your own or with a partner. The same thinking—share into groups or make groups of a size—stays the same."

### Student-Led Sharing Division Algorithm Work (25 min)

Hand out a set of 5-6 sharing division problems with manageable numbers (all within 100 ÷ 1-digit divisor, no remainders yet):
- 42 ÷ 2
- 84 ÷ 4
- 96 ÷ 3
- 45 ÷ 5
- 56 ÷ 2

**Task:**
- Choose 3 problems
- For each, set up the long division notation
- Work through each step, narrating aloud (or to a partner) what you're doing: "I have __ tens. My divisor is __. How many times? __. That uses __ tens. I have __ tens left. I break them into __ ones. Now I have __ ones to distribute…"
- Show remainder work or note "no remainder"

Circulate. Listen for:
- Are they using place value language (tens, ones)?
- Are they estimating how many times the divisor goes into each place?
- Are they managing remainders?

If a student is fluent, give them a harder problem (e.g., 136 ÷ 4, which requires working with hundreds).

If a student is struggling, pair them with a block set and walk through one problem together, narrating each step of the algorithm as you set up the blocks.

After 15-18 minutes of work time, reconvene briefly (5 min). Ask a student to share one problem and explain the algorithm steps aloud.

### Partner Work on Larger Division (20 min)

Present a problem with a remainder: **67 ÷ 3** (Context: 67 apples shared among 3 students; what does each student get, and what's left over?)

Work through it together if needed:
- 3 goes into 6 twice (2 tens per student)
- Uses 6, so 0 tens left
- 7 ones to distribute among 3 students: 3 × 2 = 6, with 1 left
- Each student gets 2 tens + 2 ones = 22, with 1 apple left over

Or: 67 ÷ 3 = 22 R1.

Hand out 3-4 two-digit ÷ one-digit problems with remainders (e.g., 53 ÷ 4, 87 ÷ 5, 76 ÷ 6).

**Task:**
- Partners work through the algorithm for each problem
- Write the answer as a quotient and remainder (e.g., 22 R1)
- If time, represent one problem with base ten blocks to check

Circulate. Ask:
- "Why is there a remainder? What does it mean in the context?"
- "How would you check this answer?"

### Wrap-Up and Handout Completion (10 min)

Distribute the full Course Packet handout (pp. 25-30) if not already handed out. Go over:
- Section A: Sharing division problems (guided)
- Section B: Measurement division problems (partner or independent)
- Section C: Algorithm work (larger numbers, may include remainders or two-digit divisors depending on the level)

Clarify expectations: "You'll complete as much as you can today. If you finish the handout, you've done the work. If you're still working on Section B tomorrow, that's fine—these are challenging."

Allow students to work independently or in pairs on the handout. Circulate and support as needed.

**Key support moves:**
- If a student can't decide where to start (tens or ones), remind: "Start by seeing how many times the divisor goes into your first group of digits"
- If they make a computational error in the algorithm, let them backtrack rather than jumping in
- Encourage base ten block work for one problem if notation alone is confusing

### Closing (5 min)

Wrap up. Summarize: Division has two meanings (sharing and measurement), but the algorithm is the same—it's just a notation for the thinking we did with blocks. Tomorrow in class or as homework, keep practicing the notation and checking with blocks or context.

## Differentiation & Notes

- Students who are fluent with the algorithm can move to measurement division problems or two-digit divisors earlier
- Students who struggle with place value or the algorithm steps should have base ten blocks or drawings available throughout both days
- If base ten blocks are scarce, set up one demo station where students can check their work; others work with drawings or notation
- Common misconception: Students may subtract the divisor from the wrong place value (e.g., trying to subtract 3 from a 6 in the tens place when the divisor goes into 6 ones first). Physical blocks help clarify which group you're dividing
- Remainders can be complex; start with problems that divide evenly, then add remainder problems once the algorithm is secure
- Real-world context matters: A division problem in a sharing context is different from one in a measurement context. Some students will find one context easier; explicitly name both
- If students finish early: Challenge them with a division problem that needs regrouping (e.g., 234 ÷ 5) or ask them to create a word problem for a given division equation
