# Week 11, Day 2 - Multiplication Strategies and Standard Algorithm

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand four efficient multiplication strategies: complete number, partitioning, compensation, and clustering
2. Model multi-digit multiplication using base ten blocks and area models
3. Understand the standard algorithm as a notation for strategies (not a separate way of thinking)
4. Connect algorithm notation to visual/concrete representations

## Materials

- Unit 3 Course Packet (pp. 20-24)
- Base ten blocks (flats, rods, units; enough for partner work)
- PowerPoint: Wk11D2_Multiplication_Strategies
- Video: "Nikolas and 47 × 36" example (~5 min; showing partial products method)
- Grid paper (for area models)
- Large white boards or chart paper (for whole-class modeling)
- Markers

## Lesson Outline

### Opening: Strategy Review (5 min)

Display Slide 3. Refresh from Day 1: we identified strategies for single-digit facts. Today we're scaling up—what happens when we multiply two-digit numbers?

Quick review: Ask students to think of 4 × 7 using one of these strategies:
- **Complete number** (use a known fact): "I know 5 × 7 = 35, so 4 × 7 = 35 - 7"
- **Partitioning** (break one number into parts): "4 × 7 = (2 + 2) × 7 = 2×7 + 2×7 = 28"
- **Compensation** (adjust up then down): "I know 5 × 7 = 35, so 4 × 7 = 35 - 7"
- **Doubling** (a form of complete number): "2 × 7 = 14, so 4 × 7 = 14 × 2"

Take 2-3 student examples. These same strategies scale to multi-digit factors.

### Strategy Application: Handout Work (15-20 min)

Hand out Course Packet p. 20 (or a printed sheet) with 5-6 multi-digit multiplication problems (e.g., 12 × 4, 23 × 5, 15 × 6).

**Task:** For each problem, solve it using at least two different strategies from the list above. Represent each strategy visually or with equations.

Example:
- 12 × 4 using partitioning: (10 + 2) × 4 = 10×4 + 2×4 = 40 + 8 = 48
- 12 × 4 using complete number: 10 × 4 = 40, plus 2 × 4 = 8, so 48

Circulate. Ask students: "Why does that strategy work? Could you show me with a picture?"

Debrief (5 min): Ask 2-3 students to share their strategy choices. What's the advantage of each strategy? (Partitioning is flexible; complete number leverages known facts; compensation works well when one factor is close to a benchmark.)

### Modeling 1-Digit × 2-Digit Multiplication with Base Ten Blocks (20 min)

**Problem: 8 × 14**

Step 1: Build the problem with base ten blocks. Say aloud: "We have 8 groups of 14."

With the class, make 8 groups of 14 (each group: 1 flat + 4 rods, or 1 tens rod + 4 ones). This takes a minute but is worth it visually.

Step 2: Count the blocks. Students should see:
- 8 flats (8 tens, from 8 × 10)
- 32 rods (from 8 × 4)
- Regroup: 32 rods = 3 flats + 2 rods = 30 + 2

Total: 8 flats + 3 flats + 2 rods = 11 flats + 2 rods = 112

Step 3: Connect to notation. Write on the board as you go:

```
    14
  ×  8
  ----
  8 × 4 = 32    (write below line)
 8 × 10 = 80    (write below line)
  ----
   112
```

Or use expanded form:
```
  14 × 8
= (10 + 4) × 8
= 10×8 + 4×8
= 80 + 32
= 112
```

**Key teaching move:** Narrate the connection. "The blocks show 8 groups of 10 and 8 groups of 4. The notation breaks that apart too—see, 8 × 10 = 80, and 8 × 4 = 32. When we add them, we get 112."

### Partner Work: 1-Digit × 2-Digit Practice (15-18 min)

Hand out a problem for partner work: **14 × 8** (note: same numbers, different order, to explore commutativity)

**Task:**
- Build the problem with base ten blocks (8 groups of 14, or 14 groups of 8—which is easier?)
- Draw or describe the blocks
- Write the multiplication in expanded notation and standard notation
- Check with another pair

Circulate. Listen for language: "Are they saying 'groups of'? Are they seeing the structure?"

If a pair finishes early, give them a 2-digit × 1-digit with a teen number (e.g., 6 × 13).

Debrief (3-5 min): Ask a pair to show the blocks and the notation side by side. "How do the blocks match the notation?"

### Two-Digit × Two-Digit Multiplication: Introduction (15-18 min)

**Problem: 14 × 8** has moved us from 1-digit × 2-digit. Now: **2-digit × 2-digit (47 × 36)** is too large for base tens but not too large for an area model.

**Step 1: Area Model**

Draw a rectangle on a large board or chart paper. Partition it:
- One dimension is 47 (mark as 40 + 7)
- Other dimension is 36 (mark as 30 + 6)

This creates a 2 × 2 grid of smaller rectangles:

```
        30      6
    +-------+-------+
40  | 40×30 | 40×6  |
    +-------+-------+
 7  | 7×30  | 7×6   |
    +-------+-------+
```

Label each sub-rectangle with its product:
- 40 × 30 = 1200
- 40 × 6 = 240
- 7 × 30 = 210
- 7 × 6 = 42

Add them: 1200 + 240 + 210 + 42 = 1692

**Step 2: Partial Products Notation**

Write the same computation in vertical form using partial products:

```
      47
    × 36
    -----
     282  ← 6 × 47 (or broken into 6×40 + 6×7)
    1410  ← 30 × 47 (or broken into 30×40 + 30×7)
    -----
    1692
```

Or, more explicitly:

```
      47
    × 36
    -----
      42  ← 7 × 6
     240  ← 40 × 6
     210  ← 7 × 30
    1200  ← 40 × 30
    -----
    1692
```

**Key teaching move:** Match the grid to the notation. "See how the area model shows all four products separately? The partial products method does the same thing—we just write them out."

### Video: Nikolas and 47 × 36 (5 min)

Show a brief video of a student explaining his partial products work on 47 × 36. After the video, ask: "What strategy is Nikolas using? How does it match what we just drew?" Reference the two partial products notations:
- Some students multiply by ones first, then tens (6, then 30)
- Others multiply by tens first, then ones (30, then 6)
- Both are valid

### Closing and Wrap-Up (5 min)

Summarize:
- Strategies (partitioning, complete number, compensation) work at any scale
- Area models and partial products make the strategy visible
- Standard algorithm notation compresses the work—students need to see the steps inside the compression

Emphasize: Teaching multiplication doesn't mean jumping straight to algorithm. Students should build strategies with base ten blocks and area models first. Notation comes when the thinking is solid.

## Differentiation & Notes

- If base ten blocks are unavailable, use grid paper and drawings of rods and units
- For partners who struggle to regroup (32 rods = 3 flats + 2 rods), help them physically bundle rods into groups of 10
- If a partner finishes early, have them predict what 14 × 9 would be using blocks (one more group), then verify
- The partial products method is one notation; the standard "compressed" algorithm (where students multiply ones, write down, regroup, multiply tens) is another. Both should be shown. The key is that students see the logic inside each notation form
- If the video is unavailable, verbally walk through 47 × 36 using the area model and ask students to predict the four partial products before you show them
- Don't rush the connection between concrete (blocks, drawings) and abstract (notation). This lesson is deliberately slow on that front
