# Week 11, Day 1 - Multiplication Facts: Number Selection and Instructional Planning

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand the importance of strategic number selection in teaching multiplication facts
2. Understand the guided invention approach to developing multiplication fact strategies
3. Develop an instructional plan for teaching multiplication facts to a grade-level cohort

## Materials

- Unit 3 Course Packet (pp. 16-19)
- Video: "Teaching Multiplication Facts Through Strategy" or equivalent (showing 2×7, 4×7, 3×7, 7×7 sequence; ~8-10 min)
- Grade-level assignment cards (K, 1st, 2nd, 3rd grade groups)
- Large post-it paper and markers
- PowerPoint: Wk11D1_Multiplication_Facts_Planning

## Lesson Outline

### Opening: Why Number Selection Matters (5-7 min)

Display Slide 3. Ask: "How would you teach a 3rd grader to know 6 × 7?"

Take responses. Some students might say "practice flashcards," "memorize," "use an array." Then ask: "What if I told you to start with 2 × 7, then 4 × 7, then 8 × 7, then come back to 6 × 7? Why might that order matter?"

Introduce the core idea: **Strategic number selection guides students to discover efficient strategies.** If you teach facts in a deliberate sequence, students are more likely to invent and use strategies (doubling, doubling and halving, breaking apart numbers) rather than relying on rote memorization.

### Video: Multiplication Facts Teaching Sequence (10 min)

Show video of a teacher introducing multiplication facts using a strategic sequence: 2×7, 4×7, 3×7, 7×7.

**Before watching:** Tell students, "Notice three things: the order of the problems, what the teacher shows (drawings, manipulatives), and what connections the teacher makes between problems."

**Key moments to notice:**
- 2×7 is taught first (easy—doubling, or relating to addition)
- 4×7 comes next (student likely sees "that's 2×7 doubled")
- 3×7 comes next (student breaks it into 2×7 + 1×7, or uses some other strategy)
- 7×7 comes last (student synthesizes previous strategies or uses new reasoning)

After viewing (5 min debrief):
- What strategies did students use? (Doubling, building on previous facts, using arrays or area models)
- Why did the teacher sequence the problems this way? (Each builds on prior knowledge; students discover relationships)
- What's the role of the manipulatives or drawings? (They support student reasoning; they make the structure visible)

### Whole-Class Discussion: Number Selection Principles (8 min)

Display Slide 6. Discuss the principles behind strategic number selection:

1. **Start with "easy" facts:** 1×n, 2×n, 5×n, 10×n are easier because students see doubling, skip counting, or place value
2. **Build toward harder facts through related doubles:** 4×n can be seen as 2×n doubled; 3×n often uses 2×n + n
3. **Use commutative property:** Once students know 2×7, they know 7×2; this cuts the memorization load
4. **Use factors with shared properties:** Products of the same factor (2×7, 4×7, 8×7, then 3×7, 6×7) reveal patterns
5. **Save the hardest for last:** 6×7, 7×8, 8×9—facts that don't decompose easily into known facts—come when students have a strong base and can use visual models or guess-and-check

### Group Work: Instructional Planning (55 min)

Divide class into four grade-level groups: **K-1 group, 2nd grade group, 3rd grade group, 4th grade group.**

**Context:** Each group is designing a unit to teach multiplication facts. Use Course Packet pp. 16-19 as a template.

**Task:** For your grade level, develop an **Instructional Plan** that addresses six categories:

#### 1. Big Picture: Multiplication Learning Progression (15 min)
- What have students learned about multiplication before this unit? (Equal groups, arrays, area, properties? Answer depends on grade.)
- What is your goal at the end of this unit? (Fact fluency? Fact familiarity? Strategy flexibility?)
- What comes after this unit? (Mental math with two-digit factors? Division facts?)

Write 2-3 sentences on a post-it for this section.

#### 2. Consistency and Commutative Property (8 min)
- How will you help students see that 3×7 and 7×3 represent the same idea?
- Will you teach both, or will you teach one and let students infer the other?
- What representation (area, array, equal groups) best shows commutativity for your grade?

#### 3. Addition vs. Multiplication Thinking (8 min)
- For students coming from addition fluency, how will you help them distinguish "5 groups of 3" from "5 + 3"?
- What language will you use? (Focus on "groups of" vs. just listing amounts)
- What misconceptions might arise, and how will you address them?

#### 4. Strategy Support and Sequencing (15 min)
- What strategies are developmentally appropriate for your grade? (Doubling, skip counting, arrays, breaking apart, repeated addition, derived facts)
- In what order will you teach facts? Start with five facts that make sense as an entry sequence.
  - Example for 3rd grade: 1×n (entry), 2×n, 5×n, 10×n, then 3×n (built from 2×n + n), then 4×n (doubling), then harder facts
- What manipulatives or visual models support each strategy?

#### 5. Assessment (8 min)
- How will you know if students are fluent, or just memorizing?
- Will you use flashcards, timed tests, or number talks? Explain your choice.
- What does fluency look like for your grade at the end of the unit?

#### 6. Enjoyment and Engagement (5 min)
- How will you make fact practice feel like math (thinking, reasoning) and not just drill?
- What activities, games, or structures will keep students engaged while building fluency?

Assign one person per category within the group to be the "expert" (they lead thinking on that category). Groups can take 10-15 min for whole-group brainstorm, then 40 min to fill in all six categories.

Circulate and ask:
- "Why did you choose that starting sequence?"
- "How do your strategies connect to the number choices?"
- "What will you do when a student says '6 × 7—I'll just memorize it'?"

### Sharing and Synthesis (5-7 min)

If time permits, have each group share their entry sequence (first 5 facts) and rationale. If time is short, collect post-its and note that you'll review them.

**Key synthesis points to make:**
- Smart number selection is the difference between a unit on facts and a unit on fact strategies
- Every grade can use doubling, skip counting, arrays, and decomposition—it's just applied to different number ranges
- Fluency emerges from repeated reasoning, not rote drill

### Closing (2 min)

Next session we'll look at the strategies themselves (partitioning, compensation, clustering) and how to model them with manipulatives. Today's planning work gives you a roadmap for where those strategies fit.

## Differentiation & Notes

- If a group is struggling with "why this sequence?", show them Slide 7 (a pre-made sequence for their grade) and ask them to predict what strategy students might use at each step
- Grade-level assignment matters: K-1 groups will focus on "discovery of the concept of multiplication"; 3rd groups will focus on fluency; 4th groups on applying facts in larger contexts
- If the video is unavailable or misaligned, verbally walk through a sequence (e.g., 2×5, 4×5, 8×5, 3×5, 6×5) and ask students what strategy each suggests
- Time allocation: The group work is the heart of this lesson. If discussion is rich, let it run; if wrapping up early, do a quicker sharing round rather than trying to fill silence
- Have post-it templates ready so groups don't waste time creating structure
