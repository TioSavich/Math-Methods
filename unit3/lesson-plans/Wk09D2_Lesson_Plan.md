# Week 9, Day 2 - Addition Problem Structures

## Course Information
- **Course:** E343 Math Education Methods for Pre-Service Teachers
- **Institution:** Indiana University
- **Class Duration:** ~75 minutes

## Goals

1. Understand the four addition problem types: join, separate, part-part-whole, and compare
2. Understand how varying the unknown position in each structure reveals connections between addition and subtraction
3. Connect problem structures to Indiana Academic Standards for grades K-2
4. Build teacher skill in identifying problem types and planning instruction around them

## Materials

- Unit 3 Course Packet (pp. 3-7)
- Printed problem sort cards (8 problems from Slide 8)
- 8 large posted sheets (one per problem type/unknown combination)
- Markers
- PowerPoint: Wk09D2_Addition_Structures
- Base ten blocks or counters (optional, for modeling)

## Lesson Outline

### Opening / Quick Connection (3 min)

Ask: "How is 3 + 5 = 8 different from 8 - 5 = 3?" Take responses. Establish: they tell different stories. Today we're going to understand the story structures that sit behind addition and subtraction.

### Instructor-Led: Problem Types, Part A (12-15 min)

Display Slide 5. Walk through the four addition problem structures:

1. **Join (Add To):** Start quantity, add more, find the result. Story: "Maria had 3 apples. Her mom gave her 5 more. How many does she have now?" → 3 + 5 = 8
2. **Separate (Take From):** Start quantity, take some away, find what's left. Story: "There were 8 apples. Maria ate 3. How many are left?" → 8 - 3 = 5
3. **Part-Part-Whole:** Two parts combine into a whole. Story: "Maria has 3 red apples and 5 green apples. How many apples does she have altogether?" → 3 + 5 = 8
4. **Compare:** Two quantities, find the difference. Story: "Maria has 8 apples. Her brother has 3 apples. How many more apples does Maria have?" → 8 - 3 = 5

Use Slide 6-9 to show visual models for each (bar diagram, number line, set representation).

**Key point:** Same numbers, different problem structures, often different operations that make sense.

### Part A: Problem Sort (10-12 min)

Hand out Course Packet p. 3 (Part A). Students read 5 printed problems and identify which of the four types each is.

Problems should include:
- Clear join problems
- Clear separate problems
- Clear part-part-whole problems
- Clear compare problems
- One ambiguous or tricky one (e.g., compare could sound like "how many more" or "how many altogether")

Debrief using Slides 10-14. Ask: "What words tip you off to the problem type?" Collect language clues (e.g., "gave her more" = join, "how many left" = separate, "altogether" = part-part-whole, "how many more/fewer" = compare).

### Part B: Varying the Unknown (10-12 min)

Display Slide 15. Show how **changing where the unknown is** changes the operation needed.

**Example in Join structure:**
- "Maria had 3 apples. Her mom gave her some more. Now she has 8. How many did her mom give her?" → 3 + __ = 8 (addition equation but solving requires subtraction thinking)
- "Maria had some apples. Her mom gave her 5 more. Now she has 8. How many did she start with?" → __ + 5 = 8

Walk through a second example (Separate) and a Compare example using Slides 16-18. Stress: same problem structure, different unknown, often different solution strategy.

Hand out Course Packet p. 4-5 (Part B). Students categorize problems by type and identify what is known and unknown, then write the equation with the unknown in the correct position.

Debrief using Slide 19 as answer key (5-7 min).

### Station Rotation Activity (35-40 min)

Post 8 large sheets around the room, each labeled with a problem type and unknown position:

**Join:**
- Sheet 1: Join, find result (result unknown)
- Sheet 2: Join, find added amount (addend unknown)
- Sheet 3: Join, find starting amount (start unknown)

**Separate:**
- Sheet 4: Separate, find amount left (result unknown)
- Sheet 5: Separate, find amount taken (subtrahend unknown)
- Sheet 6: Separate, find starting amount (minuend unknown)

**Part-Part-Whole:**
- Sheet 7: Part-part-whole, find whole
- Sheet 8: Part-part-whole, find one part

Divide students into 8 small groups (2-3 per group). Each group rotates through one station (4-5 min per station).

**Task at each station:**
- Read the problem structure and unknown position written at the top
- Create a word problem that fits that structure
- Draw a representation (bar diagram, sets, number line, array)
- Write the equation with the unknown in the correct position
- Leave space for the next group to add their example

After 4-5 minutes, groups rotate to the next station. They read what previous groups wrote, then add another example of their own.

Complete one full rotation through all 8 stations (32-40 min).

**Debrief:** (3-5 min) Ask: "Which unknown positions felt natural for creating problems? Which felt awkward?" Discuss: result-unknown problems are easiest for students to solve; start-unknown and subtrahend-unknown are hardest. This matters for instructional sequencing.

### Connection: Problem Structures and Basic Fact Knowledge (10 min)

Transition: "So far we've thought about problem structures as stories. Now let's zoom in on basic facts."

**Key insight:** If students understand that 3 + 5 = 8 can be represented as a part-part-whole or join problem, they're more likely to see that 8 - 3 = 5 and 8 - 5 = 3 are the inverse. This connection—that fact families are about the same structure with different unknowns—is central to building flexible fact knowledge.

Show Slide 20: A fact family (3 + 5 = 8, 5 + 3 = 8, 8 - 3 = 5, 8 - 5 = 3) mapped onto a part-part-whole bar diagram. All four equations live in the same diagram; they just ask the question differently.

Quick check: Use a fact family and ask students to identify which problem type (join, separate, part-part-whole, compare) each equation in the family corresponds to.

### Closing (3 min)

Summarize: We've learned four problem structures that sit behind addition and subtraction. Understanding these structures helps us design instructional sequences (start with result-unknown, then add complexity), and it helps students build connected fact knowledge, not isolated facts.

## Differentiation & Notes

- Groups that finish Part B early can move to stations ahead of schedule or create additional examples
- If a station group seems confused about the structure, ask: "Is this about a starting amount and adding more? Or about two parts that make a whole?"
- For students who struggle with compare problems, use concrete sets (counters) and physical matching to show the difference
- The station activity is designed to be loud and active; it's okay if groups don't complete all 8 in order—having them rotate through at least 4-5 different structures is the goal
- Common misconception: students conflate "separate" with "take from" and miss that separate is the general structure (subtracting in any context within it). Use language carefully: "This problem is in the separate structure. Something starts, something is taken away."
