Week 9 — Addition & Subtraction Operation Sense
Day 1

Class Discussion → Indiana Academic Standards for Addition & Subtraction

Vertical Articulation: K–3 Standards

For each grade band, identify what students are expected to know and do regarding addition and subtraction. Record the relevant standards and note how expectations change across grades.

Kindergarten — Number Sense

K.NS.11: Show different ways to represent addition/subtraction with objects, fingers, mental images, drawings, sounds, acting out, verbal explanation, expressions, equations.

Kindergarten — Computation & Algebraic Thinking

K.CA.1–5: Fluent addition and subtraction within 5. Solve word problems within 10 using objects or drawings. Decompose numbers to 10 into pairs. Find number that makes 10 from a given number (1–9). Fluently add/subtract within 5.

1st Grade — Number Sense

1.NS.1–6: Count to 120 from any number. Read/write numerals to represent objects. Understand 10–19 as ten and ones. Count by 1s, 2s, 5s, 10s to 120. Use place value (tens/ones) within two-digit numbers. Identify penny, nickel, dime, quarter by value.

1st Grade — Computation & Algebraic Thinking

1.CA.1–7: Solve real-world problems within 20. Solve word problems that call for addition of three whole numbers whose sum is within 20. Apply commutative and associative properties. Understand subtraction as unknown-addend problem. Relate counting to addition/subtraction. Add/subtract within 20 using strategies (counting on, making ten, decomposing). Understand the meaning of the equal sign.

2nd Grade — Number Sense

2.NS.1–9: Count by 1s, 2s, 5s, 10s, 25s, 100s to 1000. Read/write up to 1000. Place value through hundreds. Compare two three-digit numbers. Determine whether a group up to 20 objects has odd or even number of members. Show that even numbers can be expressed as two equal addends.

2nd Grade — Computation & Algebraic Thinking

2.CA.1–7: Fluent addition/subtraction within 20. Solve one- and two-step word problems within 100 involving addition and subtraction. Determine unknown whole number in add/subtract equation. Use estimation to decide whether answers are reasonable. Understand repeated addition as a precursor to multiplication. Show that the order in which two numbers are added does not change the sum (commutative property). Create, extend, and give an appropriate rule for patterns.

3rd Grade — Computation & Algebraic Thinking (Multiplication Focus)

3.CA introduces multiplication through equal groups, arrays, and area. Two-step word problems appear. Properties of operations (commutative, associative, distributive) become explicit.

What standards did we not fully understand the meaning of?

Discuss in class. 3.AT.2 (solve two-step word problems) is commonly underestimated—students need experience with all four operations, not just addition. 3.AT.5 (determine unknown whole number in multiplication/division equation) connects to algebraic thinking, not just fact recall.

What is gone from 2nd and 3rd grade that was there in a previous grade (assumed to be learned)?

Single-digit addition/subtraction as a primary instructional focus. Counting-based strategies (counting on, counting back) are replaced by reasoning strategies. One-step problems are assumed; two-step problems become the new focus.

What is new in 2nd and 3rd grade that was not there in the previous grade?

2nd grade: Fluent addition/subtraction within 20, two-step addition/subtraction word problems, even/odd numbers, repeated addition as precursor to multiplication. 3rd grade: Multiplication and division within 100, properties of operations, area as multiplication, representing problems with equations using a letter for the unknown.

Vertical Articulation: Multiplication Standards (K–3)

Kindergarten

No explicit multiplication. Foundation laid through counting and grouping.

1st Grade

1.CA.6: Demonstrate fluency with addition/subtraction within 20. No multiplication, but equal groups appear informally in "how many altogether" problems.

2nd Grade

2.CA.5: Understand repeated addition as a precursor to multiplication. Solve problems involving equal groups using repeated addition (e.g., 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12).

3rd Grade

3.CA.1–5: Fluent multiplication/division within 100. Represent problems with equal groups, arrays, area. Apply commutative, associative, and distributive properties. Solve two-step word problems using all four operations.

What standards did we not fully understand?

Discuss in class. 2.CA.5 (repeated addition as precursor) is often taught only as skip counting, but it should ground the equal-groups meaning of multiplication. 3.CA.3 (use properties) requires more than memorizing names—students need to use properties to derive unknown facts.

What is gone from 2nd/3rd grade that was in a previous grade?

Addition/subtraction fact development as primary focus. By 3rd grade, add/subtract fluency is assumed. Counting-based strategies for basic facts give way to reasoning and derived-fact strategies for multiplication.

What is new in 2nd/3rd grade?

2nd grade: Repeated addition, equal-group reasoning. 3rd grade: Formal multiplication and division, properties of operations, area model, two-step problems with all four operations, unknown-number equations ($\_ \times 6 = 42$).

Day 2

Class Discussion → Multiplication Strategies and Standard Algorithm

Mental Computation Strategies

Complete Number Strategy: $7 \times 53$

$7 \times 53$: Think $7 \times 50 = 350$, then $7 \times 3 = 21$. $350 + 21 = 371$. (Keep one factor whole, decompose the other.)

Partitioning Strategy: $6 \times 28$

$6 \times 28$: Partition 28 into $20 + 8$. $6 \times 20 = 120$, $6 \times 8 = 48$. $120 + 48 = 168$. (Distributive property: split one factor by place value.)

Compensation Strategy: $7 \times 37$

$7 \times 37$: Round 37 up to 40. $7 \times 40 = 280$. Compensate: $7 \times 3 = 21$. $280 - 21 = 259$. (Adjust to a friendly number, then correct.)

Cluster Problems: $32 \times 29$

Cluster: $30 \times 30 = 900$; $30 \times 29 = 870$; $2 \times 29 = 58$. Answer: $870 + 58 = 928$. (Build from known facts toward the target product.)

Instructor Led: The Multiplication Algorithm

The multiplication algorithm is based on the distributive property. Students usually first apply it for two-digit times one-digit numbers. The goal is to keep emphasizing place value and meaning.
