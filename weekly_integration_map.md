# E343 Weekly Integration Map

A week-by-week guide to how six frameworks interact across the semester. For each week, this document identifies which frameworks are active, the vocabulary most likely to cause confusion, and the conceptual thread that ties everything together.

**Frameworks tracked:**
1. **Five Practices (5P)** -- Smith & Stein
2. **CDM / Talk Moves** -- Chapin, O'Connor, Anderson
3. **Van de Walle (VdW)** -- *Elementary and Middle School Mathematics*
4. **Indiana Standards** -- Indiana Academic Standards for Mathematics
5. **Illustrative Mathematics (IM)** -- K-5 Curriculum
6. **Lesson Structure** -- Launch-Explore-Summarize (shared across frameworks)

**How to read this document:** Each week shows which frameworks are "live" (being introduced or actively practiced), which are "background" (referenced but not the focus), and which are dormant. The vocabulary tables highlight terms where the same concept has different names across frameworks -- these are the places students get confused.

## Week 2: Learning Goals vs. Performance Goals (Practice 0)
**Unit:** 1 -- Pedagogy Foundations
**Primary frameworks:** Five Practices (Practice 0), Illustrative Mathematics
**Status:** 5P = deepened (Practice 0) | CDM = dormant | VdW = dormant | Indiana = mentioned (standards referenced in assignment) | IM = used as lesson source | Lesson Structure = background

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Learning goal | 5P | What students should UNDERSTAND (conceptual). VdW: "conceptual knowledge." IM: "lesson goals." Indiana: Process Standards target understanding. |
| Performance goal | 5P | What students should be able to DO (observable). VdW: "procedural fluency." Indiana: Content Standards describe performance. IM: "Cool-down performance indicators." |
| SWBAT ("Students will be able to...") | 5P | A marker that you are writing a performance goal, not a learning goal |
| Cognitive demand | 5P | How much genuine thinking a task requires. IM: "invitations to reason." VdW: "teaching through problem solving." |
| Task selection | 5P | Choosing problems that align with your goals AND require real thinking |

**Watch out for:** Students often confuse learning goals with performance goals. A student CAN meet a performance goal (get the right answer) WITHOUT meeting the learning goal (understanding why the procedure works). The example from class: a student can execute the addition algorithm correctly without understanding that regrouping means ten ones make one ten.

**Integration insight:** Goals are the foundation of everything -- if you set a learning goal that targets understanding, you need a task with high cognitive demand to get there. If your goal is only procedural, a worksheet suffices. This is why Practice 0 comes first.

**What you're building toward:** The "Standards, Lesson Goals, and Anticipating" assignment requires you to write both kinds of goals for YOUR lesson. You need to identify an Indiana standard, then write goals that live beneath it.

**Field connection:** When you observe your host teacher's classroom, look for posted goals. Are they learning goals or performance goals? Most classrooms post "I can..." statements, which are performance goals. Where are the learning goals hiding?

**Assignment activity:** Standards, Lesson Goals, and Anticipating assignment introduced (due Week 3). Teaching teams assigned. Weekly check-in due.

## Week 3: Anticipating Student Thinking (Practice 1)
**Unit:** 1 -- Pedagogy Foundations
**Primary frameworks:** Five Practices (Practice 1), Van de Walle (content reading by grade level)
**Status:** 5P = deepened (Practice 1) | CDM = dormant | VdW = introduced (content reading) | Indiana = background (standards in assignment) | IM = used as lesson source | Lesson Structure = background

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Anticipating | 5P | Predicting how students will solve problems BEFORE teaching. Three parts: getting inside the problem, planning to respond, planning to notice. |
| Getting inside the problem | 5P | Solving the task yourself, multiple ways. VdW: "understanding the mathematics." |
| Assessing questions (preview) | 5P | Questions that surface what a student is currently thinking. CDM (coming later): "probing questions" / Talk Moves Steps 1-2. |
| Advancing questions (preview) | 5P | Questions that push a student's thinking toward the lesson goal. CDM (coming later): Talk Moves Steps 3-4. |
| Monitoring chart (preview) | 5P | A planning tool that maps anticipated strategies to specific questions you will ask. |

**Watch out for:** Anticipating is hard because you must think like multiple different children, not like yourself. The most common mistake is only anticipating the "correct" solution. You also need to anticipate common errors and partial understandings.

**Integration insight:** Anticipating is where your mathematical knowledge (from VdW) meets your pedagogical planning (from 5P). You cannot anticipate student thinking about fractions if you do not deeply understand fractions yourself. This week is the first time the course demands you know math content AND teaching strategy simultaneously.

**What you're building toward:** Your lesson plan (due in Unit 2) will require a monitoring chart built from your anticipations. Every practice after this one depends on good anticipating.

**Field connection:** When you conduct your Think Aloud Interview later, you will need to anticipate what students might say. Start thinking about what prerequisite knowledge your lesson depends on.

**Assignment activity:** Standards, Lesson Goals, and Anticipating assignment due (end of week). VdW content reading varies by teaching team grade level.

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Think Aloud Interview (TAI) | Course-specific | A one-on-one diagnostic interview. VdW: "diagnostic interview." 5P: related to anticipating and monitoring. CDM: not addressed. IM: not a curriculum component. |
| Formative assessment | Course-specific | Finding out what students know BEFORE you teach. VdW: "assessment through observation." IM: "Cool-down." 5P: monitoring. |

| Assessing vs. advancing questions | 5P | Assessing = "Tell me what you did." Advancing = "What if the numbers were different?" In the TAI, you ONLY assess -- you are NOT teaching. |

**Watch out for:** The TAI is NOT teaching. You are not helping students understand anything. You are trying to find out what they already know. This feels unnatural. The impulse to correct or guide is strong -- resist it. Use assessing questions only.

**Integration insight:** The TAI forces you to listen before you talk. This is the core of the monitoring practice (coming in Week 6) and the reason Talk Moves (coming in Week 5) matter -- you need techniques for drawing out student thinking without steering it.

## Week 5: Talk Moves -- Steps 1 and 2 (CDM Enters the Picture)
**Unit:** 2 -- Discourse
**Primary frameworks:** CDM / Talk Moves, Five Practices (connecting to monitoring)
**Status:** 5P = background (connecting Talk Moves to monitoring) | CDM = introduced | VdW = background | Indiana = dormant | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = background

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Talk Moves | CDM | Strategic questioning techniques for productive math discussion. 5P: embedded in monitoring and connecting. VdW: "classroom discourse." |
| Step 1: Helping students clarify and share own thoughts | CDM | Includes Revoicing and Say More. 5P: part of assessing questions during monitoring. |
| Revoicing | CDM | Teacher restates a student's idea to check understanding and make it public. 5P: "monitoring tool." VdW: part of "during phase discourse." |
| Say More | CDM | Asking a student to elaborate. 5P: assessing question. VdW: not specifically named. |
| Step 2: Helping students orient to others' thinking | CDM | Includes "Who can repeat?" 5P: part of connecting practice. |
| Who Can Repeat? | CDM | Asking one student to restate what another said. 5P: connecting students to each other. |

**Watch out for:** Talk Moves are NOT just ways to increase talking. They are strategic moves with specific purposes. "Say more" is not the same as "Why?" -- "Why?" often produces silence because it is too open-ended. "Say more about..." or "What do you notice about..." are more productive.

**Integration insight:** Talk Moves are the TOOLS that make the Five Practices work in real time. You cannot monitor effectively if you cannot get students to articulate their thinking. You cannot connect if students have not heard each other's ideas. CDM provides the micro-skills; 5P provides the macro-structure.

**What you're building toward:** You will use Talk Moves in your TAI rehearsal this week and in your actual TAI. Later, your lesson plan will require you to identify which Talk Moves you will use during monitoring and connecting.

**Field connection:** Practice revoicing and "say more" during your TAI. Notice which Talk Moves your host teacher uses (or does not use) during math instruction.

**Assignment activity:** TAI Activities revised version due. Math Content Presentation introduced (due Week 6). TAI rehearsal in class.

## Week 6: Talk Moves Steps 3-4 and Monitoring (Practice 2)
**Unit:** 2 -- Discourse
**Primary frameworks:** CDM / Talk Moves (Steps 3-4), Five Practices (Practice 2: Monitoring)
**Status:** 5P = deepened (Practice 2) | CDM = deepened (Steps 3-4) | VdW = dormant | Indiana = dormant | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = background (monitoring happens during Explore)

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Step 3: Helping students deepen reasoning | CDM | Includes "Press for Reasoning." 5P: advancing questions during monitoring. |
| Press for Reasoning | CDM | Asking a student to justify or prove their thinking. 5P: advancing question. Indiana PS.3: "Construct viable arguments." |
| Step 4: Helping students engage with others' reasoning | CDM | Includes "Do you agree or disagree?" and "Who can add on?" 5P: connecting practice. |
| Agree/Disagree | CDM | Asking students to evaluate another student's reasoning. Indiana PS.3. |
| Who Can Add On? | CDM | Asking students to extend or build on what someone said. 5P: connecting. |
| Monitoring | 5P | Circulating during Explore to track, assess, and advance student thinking. CDM Steps 1-4 are the verbal tools for monitoring. VdW: "during phase / observe and assess." IM: "Work phase." |
| Tracking student thinking | 5P | Using a monitoring chart to record which anticipated strategies appear. |
| Assessing questions | 5P | "Stay and listen" questions -- surface what a student is doing. CDM Steps 1-2. |
| Advancing questions | 5P | "Walk away" questions -- give students something to work on. CDM Steps 3-4. |

**Watch out for:** "Assessing" and "advancing" look similar but serve different purposes. An assessing question asks "What did you do?" An advancing question asks "What would happen if...?" The key distinction: assessing questions can be answered immediately; advancing questions often cannot -- you walk away and let the student think.

**Integration insight:** This is the week where CDM and 5P merge. Talk Moves Steps 1-2 are the tools for assessing student thinking during monitoring. Steps 3-4 are the tools for advancing student thinking. The monitoring chart (from anticipating) tells you what to look for; Talk Moves tell you what to say when you find it.

**What you're building toward:** Your lesson plan requires assessing and advancing questions for each anticipated strategy. The fishbowl rehearsal this week models exactly what monitoring looks like in practice.

**Field connection:** TAI Analysis paper due. Use your TAI experience to reflect on what happens when you assess without advancing (TAI) vs. when you do both (classroom instruction).

**Assignment activity:** TAI Analysis paper due. Week 5 Check-in (delayed to this week). Fishbowl monitoring rehearsal with Ms. Tyus's class videos.

## Week 7: Selecting, Sequencing, and Connecting (Practices 3-5)
**Unit:** 2 -- Discourse
**Primary frameworks:** Five Practices (Practices 3, 4, 5)
**Status:** 5P = deepened (Practices 3-5) | CDM = applied (Talk Moves during connecting) | VdW = dormant | Indiana = dormant | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = explicit (connecting happens in Summarize)

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Selecting | 5P | Purposefully choosing which student work to share in discussion. NOT random volunteers. VdW: part of "after phase." IM: embedded in Synthesis guidance. |
| Sequencing | 5P | Ordering selected work to build a coherent mathematical storyline. Common strategies: most concrete first, most common first, misconception first. |
| Connecting | 5P | The final practice -- tying student solutions to each other AND to the lesson goals. VdW: "after phase." IM: "Synthesis." CDM: Steps 3-4 support connecting. |
| Coherent storyline | 5P | The sequence of presented work should tell a mathematical story that builds toward the learning goal -- not just "here's what Student A did, here's what Student B did." |
| Equity in participation | 5P | Who gets to be positioned as a mathematical thinker? Selection decisions have equity implications. |

**Watch out for:** Connecting is NOT just having students share their work one after another (that is "show and tell"). Connecting requires the teacher to explicitly draw comparisons: "How is Student A's strategy similar to Student B's? How does that relate to our learning goal?" If you skip the comparison, students leave thinking they saw unrelated strategies.

**Integration insight:** Selecting, sequencing, and connecting are where the entire Five Practices framework pays off. Your anticipations (Practice 1) gave you the strategies to look for. Your monitoring (Practice 2) told you who actually used them. Now you choose the right work, put it in the right order, and make the mathematical point explicit. This is the "summarize" phase of Launch-Explore-Summarize -- and it is the hardest part.

**What you're building toward:** Your lesson plan (draft due Week 8) must include a selecting/sequencing rationale tied to your learning goals, just like Ms. Tyus's rationale in the textbook.

**Field connection:** Watch your host teacher's whole-class discussions. Is student work being selected strategically, or does the teacher call on volunteers? Is there a mathematical storyline, or is it show-and-tell?

**Assignment activity:** Lesson Plan introduced. Gallery walk with student work for selecting/sequencing practice. 5P Connecting reading.

## Week 9: Indiana Standards and Addition/Subtraction Problem Structures
**Unit:** 3 -- Operations & Algebraic Thinking
**Primary frameworks:** Indiana Standards, Van de Walle (Ch. 8), Five Practices (applied in lesson planning)
**Status:** 5P = applied (in lesson planning for field) | CDM = applied (in field teaching) | VdW = primary (addition/subtraction content) | Indiana = introduced (content standards + process standards) | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = applied (field teaching)

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Content standards | Indiana | What students should know/do at each grade. VdW organizes by topic, not grade. IM organizes by unit/lesson. |
| Process standards (PS.1-PS.8) | Indiana | How students should engage with math. Parallel CCSS Standards for Mathematical Practice (MP.1-MP.8). 5P operationalizes PS.3 and PS.1. |
| Vertical articulation | Indiana | How a strand develops across grade levels. VdW covers this within chapters. IM does this within unit sequences. |
| Number Sense strand | Indiana | One of five K-2 content strands (Number Sense, Computation & Algebraic Thinking, Geometry, Measurement, Data Analysis). |
| Join problem | VdW | Addition with action (getting more). Indiana/CCSS: "Add to." IM: "Add To." |
| Separate problem | VdW | Subtraction with action (taking away). Indiana/CCSS: "Take from." IM: "Take From." |
| Part-Part-Whole problem | VdW | Two parts combine into a whole, no action. Indiana/CCSS: "Put together / Take apart." IM: "Put Together / Take Apart." |
| Compare problem | VdW | Two quantities compared. Indiana/CCSS/IM: "Compare." (Same term across frameworks.) |
| Unknown position (result, change, start) | VdW | Which part of the equation is missing changes the difficulty. Indiana: not named but implied. |
| Semantic equation vs. calculation equation | VdW | Semantic follows the story (__ + 25 = 40). Calculation can be computed (40 - 25 = __). Both represent the same problem. |

**Watch out for:** Van de Walle calls them "Join" and "Separate." Indiana and CCSS call them "Add to" and "Take from." These are the SAME things. Students will encounter both sets of terms. Also: Indiana standards are NOT curriculum. The standards tell you WHAT to teach; IM and VdW tell you HOW.

**Integration insight:** This week is the biggest vocabulary collision in the course. Four frameworks name the same four problem types differently. The concept is identical -- the labels differ. Mastering this translation is essential because your lesson plan must reference Indiana standards, your math knowledge comes from VdW, and your curriculum materials come from IM.

**What you're building toward:** Understanding problem structures is prerequisite for understanding fact fluency (Week 10), multiplication structures (Week 10-11), and ultimately the algorithms (Weeks 11-13). The standards you study this week tell you where your lesson fits in the K-5 trajectory.

**Field connection:** You are teaching your first lesson this week or next. Your lesson plan should reference an Indiana standard. The problem types help you understand what kinds of problems your students should be solving.

## Week 10: Fact Fluency, Number Talks, and Multiplication/Division Operation Sense
**Unit:** 3 -- Operations & Algebraic Thinking
**Primary frameworks:** Van de Walle (Ch. 9), Indiana Standards, Five Practices (applied)
**Status:** 5P = applied | CDM = applied (number talks use Talk Moves) | VdW = primary | Indiana = active (fact fluency standards) | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = applied

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Fact fluency | VdW | Efficiency + accuracy + flexibility + appropriateness. NOT memorization. Indiana: "demonstrate fluency with mastery." CCSS: "know from memory." |
| Automaticity | VdW | Quick recall -- the END state. Different from fluency, which is the PROCESS that gets you there. |
| Phase 1: Counting strategies | VdW | Counting all, counting on. The earliest phase. |
| Phase 2: Reasoning strategies | VdW | Doubles, near doubles, making 10, pretend-a-10. Indiana: "using strategies such as..." |
| Phase 3: Mastery | VdW | Producing answers efficiently, accurately, flexibly, appropriately. Indiana: "demonstrate fluency with mastery." CCSS: "know from memory." |
| Number Talk | VdW / Course | A daily 10-minute routine: mental computation, strategy sharing, representation. CDM: Talk Moves structure the discussion. 5P: the teacher monitors and connects. |
| Explicit strategy instruction vs. guided invention | VdW | Two approaches to teaching strategies. Explicit: teacher introduces strategy, then students explore. Guided: students invent strategies, teacher facilitates sharing. |
| Operation sense | VdW | Understanding what multiplication/division MEANS, not just how to compute. |
| Equal groups / Array / Area / Comparison / Combination | VdW | Five multiplicative problem structures. Indiana 3.CA.3: "equal-sized groups, arrays, area models, equal intervals." |
| Partition (sharing) division | VdW | Know # of groups, find group size. Indiana: "partitioning, sharing." |
| Measurement (quotative) division | VdW | Know group size, find # of groups. Indiana: implied via "inverse of multiplication." |

**Watch out for:** "Fluency" does NOT mean "memorization." This is the single most important vocabulary distinction in Unit 3. Timed tests measure automaticity (the end state), not fluency (the process). Also: "partition division" and "sharing division" and "fair share division" are the SAME thing. "Measurement division" and "quotative division" are the SAME thing.

**Integration insight:** Number Talks are where ALL frameworks converge. A Number Talk uses CDM Talk Moves (revoicing, say more, press for reasoning) within a Five Practices structure (anticipate strategies, monitor who uses what, select and sequence for discussion, connect strategies to each other). The math content comes from VdW's fact fluency trajectory. The standards tell you which facts students should master at which grade.

**What you're building toward:** Multiplication fact strategies (Week 11), multi-digit algorithms (Weeks 12-13), and ultimately the connection from whole-number division to fractions (Week 13-14).

**Field connection:** Lesson Plan 3 draft due. Observe or lead a Number Talk. Notice which strategies students use -- are they in Phase 1, 2, or 3?

## Week 11: Multiplication Fact Strategies and Multi-Digit Multiplication
**Unit:** 3 -- Operations & Algebraic Thinking
**Primary frameworks:** Van de Walle (Ch. 9-10), Indiana Standards (grades 3-5)
**Status:** 5P = applied | CDM = applied (number talks) | VdW = primary | Indiana = active (3.CA.3, 3.CA.6, 4.CA.1) | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = applied

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Doubling strategy | VdW | Using known doubles to derive unknown facts (e.g., 6x7 = 2x(3x7)). |
| Break apart / Distributive property | VdW | 6x7 = (5x7)+(1x7). Indiana: "properties of operations." CCSS: 3.OA.B.5. |
| Add/subtract a group | VdW | 6x7 = (5x7)+7 or 6x7 = (7x7)-7. Another form of distributive property. |
| Commutative property | VdW | 3x5 = 5x3. Visible in arrays (rotate the array). Indiana 3.CA.5. |
| Associative property | VdW | (2x3)x4 = 2x(3x4). Allows regrouping factors. |
| Area model | VdW | A rectangle whose area represents the product. Dimensions are the factors. Connects to base-ten decomposition for multi-digit multiplication. |
| Partial products | VdW | Breaking a multiplication into sub-problems based on place value (e.g., 47x36 = 40x30 + 40x6 + 7x30 + 7x6). Indiana 4.CA.1: "strategies based on place value." |
| Open array | VdW | A semi-concrete version of the area model -- a blank rectangle with dimensions marked, partial products inside. |
| Standard algorithm (multiplication) | VdW | The traditional vertical method. VdW treats this as the LAST step in a concrete-to-abstract progression. Indiana 5.CA.1. |

**Watch out for:** Students often learn the area model as a procedure disconnected from meaning. The key insight is that partial products ARE the standard algorithm, just written differently. Every step in the standard algorithm corresponds to a section of the area model. If students understand the area model, they understand why the algorithm works.

**Integration insight:** The progression from concrete (base-ten blocks) to semi-concrete (area model) to abstract (standard algorithm) mirrors the 5P sequencing strategy of ordering student work from most concrete to most abstract. The same pedagogical principle applies at two levels: how students learn math AND how teachers present student work.

**What you're building toward:** Division strategies (Week 12-13) reverse this process. The same area model that works for multiplication works for division in reverse. Fractions (Week 14-15) use the same area model for entirely different content.

**Field connection:** Teaching Lesson 2 or 3. Lesson Plan 3 final draft due. Lesson Analysis (Lesson 2) due Sunday.

**Assignment activity:** Lesson Plan 3 final draft due. VdW reading on multiplication strategies and algorithms. Grade 3-5 vertical articulation.

## Week 12: Multi-Digit Multiplication Algorithms and Beginning Division
**Unit:** 3 -- Operations & Algebraic Thinking
**Primary frameworks:** Van de Walle (Ch. 10, 13), Indiana Standards (grades 3-5)
**Status:** 5P = applied | CDM = applied | VdW = primary | Indiana = active (4.CA.1, 4.CA.2) | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = applied

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Number strings / Cluster problems | VdW | Using known facts to build to a harder product (e.g., 10x60 and 30x60 to find 34x50). Indiana: "strategies based on place value and properties." |
| Connected array | VdW | Area model with base-ten blocks physically arranged. The bridge from concrete to semi-concrete. |
| Standard algorithm for multiplication | VdW | The conventional vertical method. VdW connects each line to the corresponding partial product and area model section. |
| Sharing division (large numbers) | VdW | Distribute base-ten blocks into groups. Model with manipulatives first, then record. |
| Measurement division (large numbers) | VdW | Subtract equal groups using open arrays. Record partial quotients. |
| Partial quotients | VdW | A division strategy where you subtract known multiples of the divisor. The precursor to long division. Indiana 4.CA.2. |
| Remainder | VdW | The amount left over after division. Interpreting the remainder depends on the context (ignore it, round up, express as a fraction). |

**Watch out for:** Sharing division and measurement division look different with base-ten blocks but use the same notation (dividend / divisor = quotient). The model you choose depends on the problem structure -- sharing is easier to model with blocks (deal them out), measurement is easier with arrays (subtract groups).

**Integration insight:** This week completes the whole-number operations arc. The critical connection: division with remainders is the bridge to fractions. When 7 cookies are shared among 4 children, each gets 1 with 3 remaining -- and those 3 cookies can be split further, yielding 1 3/4 each. This remainder-to-fraction connection is the pivot point between Units 3 and 4.

**What you're building toward:** Week 13 finishes division and makes the explicit transition to fractions through the division construct. The area model returns for fraction multiplication in Week 15.

**Field connection:** Teaching final lesson(s). Lesson Analysis (Lesson 3) due Sunday.

**Assignment activity:** Lesson Plan revised version due (Weeks 12-14). Lesson Analysis (Lesson 2) due.

## Week 13: Finishing Division and Beginning Fractions
**Unit:** 4 -- Fractions (transition week)
**Primary frameworks:** Van de Walle (Ch. 15), Indiana Standards (grades 3-5), Five Practices (revisited for fraction tasks)

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Division construct of fractions | VdW | a / b = a/b. Division of whole numbers PRODUCES fractions. Indiana 5.NS.2. CCSS 5.NF.B.3. |
| Referent whole / "the whole" | VdW | The quantity a fraction refers to. Indiana: "the same whole." IM: "the whole / the unit." |
| Part-whole construct | VdW | Sharing a whole equally and shading some parts. The first fraction meaning, introduced in grades 1-2. Indiana 3.NS.2. |
| Measurement construct | VdW | Partitioning a whole into equal parts, removing one part (unit fraction), and iterating it. Heavy emphasis starting grade 3. Indiana 3.NS.2: "iterations of unit fractions." |
| Operator construct | VdW | A fraction that "operates on" another number (e.g., 1/3 of $15). |
| Area model (fractions) | VdW | Using the area of a shape (circle, rectangle) to represent fractions. Different from the area model for multiplication, but same visual principle. |
| Length model | VdW | Using a number line or strip to represent fractions. Emphasizes measurement meaning. |
| Set model | VdW | Using discrete objects (e.g., counters) to represent fractions. Emphasizes operator meaning. |
| Unit fraction | VdW / Indiana | 1/b -- the fraction formed by one part when a whole is partitioned into b equal parts. CCSS 3.NF.A.1. The building block of all fractions. |

**Watch out for:** Students often think "fraction" means "part of a whole" (the part-whole construct). This is the FIRST meaning but not the ONLY one. The measurement construct (iterating unit fractions) and the division construct (a/b = a divided by b) are equally important and are where most upper-elementary fraction work happens. Also: "area model" now means something different than it did in multiplication. Same name, different application.

**Integration insight:** Fractions are where everything comes together. The Five Practices structure (anticipate fraction strategies, monitor for different representations, select area/length/set models, sequence from concrete to abstract, connect to learning goals) works exactly the same as it did for whole numbers -- but the mathematics is harder because fractions have five different meanings. The VdW content knowledge you need is extensive, and the standards span grades 1-5.

**What you're building toward:** Equivalent fractions and fraction comparison (Week 14), fraction operations and the final exam (Week 15).

**Field connection:** Lesson Transcripts and Lesson Analysis due. Apply Five Practices thinking to fraction content.

## Week 14: Fraction Constructs, Equivalence, and Comparison
**Unit:** 4 -- Fractions
**Primary frameworks:** Van de Walle (Ch. 15), Indiana Standards (grades 3-4)
**Status:** 5P = applied (selecting/sequencing fraction strategies) | CDM = dormant | VdW = primary | Indiana = active (3.NS.2, 3.NS.4, 3.NS.5, 4.NS.3, 4.NS.4) | IM = dormant | Lesson Structure = applied

**Key vocabulary this week:**

| This week's term | Framework | Same concept in other frameworks |
|---|---|---|
| Partitioning | VdW | Dividing a whole into equal parts. The foundational action for part-whole fractions. Indiana 3.NS.2. |
| Iterating | VdW | Repeating a unit fraction to build larger fractions. 3/5 = 1/5 + 1/5 + 1/5. Indiana 3.NS.2: "iterations of unit fractions." CCSS 3.NF.A.1: "a parts of size 1/b." IM: "copies of a unit fraction." |
| Equivalent fractions | VdW | Two fractions that name the same quantity. Different partitionings, same amount. Indiana 4.NS.3: "fraction models." CCSS 4.NF.A.1: "visual fraction models." |
| Same denominator comparison | VdW / Indiana | Compare numerators when denominators match (3/5 > 2/5). Indiana 4.NS.4. |
| Same numerator comparison | VdW / Indiana | Compare denominators when numerators match (2/3 > 2/5 because thirds are bigger than fifths). Indiana 4.NS.4. |
| Benchmark comparison | VdW / Indiana | Compare each fraction to 0, 1/2, or 1. Indiana 4.NS.4: "benchmark such as 0, 1/2, and 1." |
| Fractions are relative | VdW | 1/2 of $1 and 1/2 of $1000 are different amounts. Comparisons only work when fractions refer to the same whole. |

**Watch out for:** "Equivalent fractions" does NOT mean "multiply the top and bottom by the same number" -- at least, not yet. VdW emphasizes visual models FIRST (area, length, number line) before the computational rule. Students who only learn the rule often cannot explain WHY 2/4 = 1/2. The folding activity (fraction strips on a number line) makes equivalence visible and physical.

**Integration insight:** Fraction comparison strategies mirror the developmental progression from concrete to abstract that has been the theme all semester. Same denominator (simplest) to same numerator (requires understanding piece size) to benchmarks (requires fraction number sense). This progression is the same pattern as the addition fact fluency phases: count all to reasoning strategies to mastery.

**What you're building toward:** The final exam (Week 15) will require you to apply fraction content knowledge AND Five Practices pedagogy to fraction teaching scenarios. Fraction comparison also sets up fraction operations.

**Field connection:** How would you select and sequence student work on an equivalent fractions task? What Talk Moves would help students articulate WHY two fractions are equivalent?

**Assignment activity:** Week 14 Check-in. VdW pp. 354-365. Folding activity and number line work in class.

## The Six Hardest Vocabulary Collisions (Keep This Handy All Semester)

1. **Lesson phases:** VdW says Before/During/After. CDM says Launch/Explore/Summarize. IM says Warm Up/Activities/Synthesis/Cool Down. 5P maps onto all of them (Anticipate before; Monitor during; Select/Sequence/Connect in Summarize). SAME THING, FOUR NAMES.

2. **Problem types:** VdW says Join/Separate/Part-Part-Whole/Compare. Indiana and CCSS say Add To/Take From/Put Together-Take Apart/Compare. SAME FOUR TYPES, DIFFERENT LABELS.

3. **Division types:** VdW says Partition/Measurement. Indiana says Partitioning-Sharing/Inverse of Multiplication. CCSS says Number of Objects in Each Share/Number of Shares. IM says How Many in Each Group/How Many Groups. SAME TWO TYPES, FOUR LABELS.

4. **Fact mastery:** VdW says Mastery (Phase 3). Indiana says "demonstrate fluency with mastery." CCSS says "know from memory." All three LOOK different but mean the same thing: quick, accurate, flexible recall.

5. **Understanding vs. doing:** 5P says Learning Goals vs. Performance Goals. VdW says Conceptual Knowledge vs. Procedural Fluency. Indiana splits this across Process Standards (understanding) and Content Standards (performance). IM uses Lesson Goals (understanding) and Cool-Down indicators (performance). SAME DISTINCTION, FOUR FRAMINGS.

6. **Teacher questions:** 5P says Assessing Questions (surface thinking) vs. Advancing Questions (push forward). CDM says Talk Moves Steps 1-2 (clarify/orient) vs. Steps 3-4 (deepen/engage). VdW says During Phase Questioning. SAME TEACHER BEHAVIOR, THREE FRAMEWORKS.
