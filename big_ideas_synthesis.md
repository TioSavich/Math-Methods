# The Big Ideas: What All Six Frameworks Are Really Saying

## How Van de Walle, Five Practices, CDM/Talk Moves, Indiana Standards, CCSS, and Illustrative Mathematics converge on the same fundamental insights about teaching mathematics

**Purpose:** Students in E343 encounter six frameworks that appear to be separate systems with separate vocabularies. They aren't. They are six perspectives on the same ten insights. This document names those insights and shows how every framework expresses each one -- often using different words for the same idea.

**How to use this document:** When you feel overwhelmed by terminology, come back here. Find the Big Idea, then trace it across frameworks. The words differ; the insight doesn't.

---

### Big Idea 1: Understanding Is Not the Same as Getting the Right Answer

**The insight:** A student can execute a procedure correctly and understand nothing about why it works. Every framework in this course draws a hard line between *doing math* and *understanding math* -- and insists that teaching must aim at understanding.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The entire book is organized around the phrase "Teaching Developmentally." The central argument is that conceptual knowledge (understanding *why*) must precede and accompany procedural fluency (knowing *how*). The fact fluency chapter makes this vivid: Phase 1 is counting, Phase 2 is reasoning strategies, Phase 3 is automaticity -- and you cannot skip Phase 2. Memorizing facts is "no different than memorizing state capitals or facts about dinosaurs." It produces answers but not mathematical knowledge.

- **Five Practices:** Practice 0 (Setting Goals) makes this operational by distinguishing *learning goals* from *performance goals*. A learning goal specifies what students should *understand* ("When subtracting two-digit numbers, tens are subtracted from tens and ones are subtracted from ones"). A performance goal specifies what they should *do* ("Students will correctly add and subtract two-digit numbers"). The whole point: a student can meet the performance goal while completely missing the learning goal -- like the student who adds 137 + 25 = 62 by executing the algorithm without understanding place value.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Chapters 3-6 organize mathematical discussion around four kinds of content: concepts, procedures, solution methods, and reasoning. The key move is that procedures get *talked about*, not just performed. When students articulate *why* they subtracted tens from tens, the procedure becomes an understanding. Steps 3-4 (Press for Reasoning, Agree/Disagree) are specifically designed to push past "I did it this way" to "here's why this works."

- **Indiana Standards:** Two kinds of standards exist for exactly this reason. Content standards describe what students should be able to *do* (performance). Process Standards PS.1-PS.8 describe what students should be able to *think* -- make sense of problems, reason abstractly, construct arguments, model, attend to precision, look for structure, look for regularity. The Process Standards are understanding; the Content Standards are performance. Both are required.

- **CCSS:** Identical structure: Content Standards + Standards for Mathematical Practice (MP.1-MP.8). The MPs are explicitly called "practices" rather than "content" because they describe habits of mind, not isolated skills. MP.1 -- "Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them" -- is an understanding standard disguised as a practice standard.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** Every IM lesson has a lesson-level learning goal (understanding) and a cool-down (performance check). The cool-down is designed so that a student who understood the lesson can complete it, but the goal is deeper. The teacher notes distinguish between what students should "know" and what they should "understand." The Synthesis phase is where understanding gets consolidated; the Cool-down merely checks whether it landed.

**Why students miss this:** They have 16+ years of experience in school systems that grade performance, not understanding. They equate "the student got the right answer" with "the student learned." They write lesson goals like "Students will be engaged in mathematics throughout the lesson" -- which is neither a learning goal nor a performance goal, just a hope about behavior.

**The unlock:** Watch the 137 + 25 = 62 example from class. The student executed the algorithm, got *an* answer, and moved on. They added 7 + 5 = 12, wrote the 2, carried the 1, then did 1 + 3 + 2 = 6. Right procedure, wrong understanding of place value. The performance goal was met (they used the algorithm). The learning goal was not (they don't understand that "carrying" means regrouping ten ones into one ten). *This* is why the distinction exists. If you only assess performance, this student passes and carries the misconception into multiplication, division, and fractions -- where it becomes catastrophic.

### Big Idea 2: Students Construct Understanding by Solving Problems, Not by Being Told How

**The insight:** Mathematical knowledge is not transmitted from teacher to student through explanation. It is built inside the student's mind through the experience of reasoning about problems. The teacher's job is to set up the conditions for that construction, not to deliver the content directly.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** "Teaching through problem solving" is the organizing philosophy. The Before/During/After lesson structure puts the problem *first* -- before the student has been shown how to solve it. This is not accidental or pedagogically trendy; it reflects how mathematical understanding actually develops. Students who are told a procedure first have nothing to reason about. Students who encounter a problem first must *build* a procedure from what they already know.

- **Five Practices:** The entire framework exists because of this principle. If the teacher were just going to explain the math, there would be no need to Anticipate student strategies, Monitor their work, Select solutions, Sequence them, or Connect them. The Five Practices are necessary precisely because the lesson depends on *what students actually do* rather than on what the teacher planned to say. Practice 0's emphasis on high-cognitive-demand tasks ensures students have something genuine to construct.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** "I learn stuff when I do stuff" -- this throwaway line from the TAI rehearsal slides captures the entire CDM philosophy. Talk Moves don't exist to help students parrot the teacher's explanation. They exist to help students *articulate their own reasoning* (Step 1: Say More), *encounter others' reasoning* (Step 2: Who Can Repeat?), *deepen their own reasoning* (Step 3: Press for Reasoning), and *engage with reasoning that differs from their own* (Step 4: Agree/Disagree, Who Can Add On?). The construction happens through articulation and confrontation.

- **Indiana Standards:** PS.1: "Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them." Not "listen to the teacher explain problems." PS.4: "Model with mathematics" -- students build models, not receive them. PS.7: "Look for and make use of structure" -- students discover structure, not memorize it.

- **CCSS:** MP.1 mirrors PS.1. The crucial word is "persevere." If the teacher showed students how to solve the problem first, there would be nothing to persevere through. Perseverance implies productive uncertainty -- the student doesn't yet know the path and must construct one.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** The three-phase lesson structure (Launch-Work-Synthesize) embeds this. During Launch, the teacher introduces the problem and ensures students understand what is being asked -- but explicitly does *not* model a solution method. During Work, students reason. The teacher guidance repeatedly says "Do not demonstrate a solution path" and "Maintain cognitive demand." The math emerges from student work, not teacher lecture.

**Why students miss this:** The Launch-Explore-Summarize structure looks inefficient compared to "I'll just show them how." Pre-service teachers worry: "What if they can't do it? What if they get frustrated? What if they go in the wrong direction?" These worries assume that the teacher's explanation is the learning. It isn't. The student's struggle is the learning (see Big Idea 9).

**The unlock:** Think about how *you* learned to ride a bicycle. No amount of explanation about balance, momentum, and counter-steering would have taught you. You had to get on the bike, wobble, fall, adjust, and eventually ride. Mathematical understanding works the same way -- it is built through the experience of reasoning, not through the experience of listening.

### Big Idea 3: Talk Is a Learning Tool, Not Just an Assessment Tool

**The insight:** When students talk about mathematics, they are not simply *reporting* what they already know. They are *building* knowledge in the act of articulating it. Discussion is instruction, not just its verification. The classroom that talks about math learns more math than the classroom that quietly practices it.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** Classroom discourse is woven throughout, not confined to a single chapter. The After phase (Summarize) is where the most important learning happens -- not through teacher lecture, but through students explaining, comparing, and connecting strategies. Van de Walle's position: if students can't explain their reasoning, they probably don't fully understand it yet. The articulation *produces* the understanding.

- **Five Practices:** The framework's very name -- "Orchestrating Productive Mathematical Discussion" -- treats discussion as the primary vehicle for mathematical learning. The key word is "productive." Not all talk is productive. Show-and-tell (students present sequentially with no comparison) is not productive. The Five Practices exist to turn potentially unproductive sharing into genuine mathematical discourse where ideas are compared, contrasted, and generalized. Connecting (Practice 5) is the culminating act: the teacher helps students see relationships between strategies that students might not see on their own. This is where conceptual understanding crystallizes.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** This is CDM's entire thesis. Chapter 1 lists what productive talk accomplishes: it reveals understanding *and* misunderstanding, boosts memory and connection-making, supports reasoning, supports language development, supports development of social skills. Crucially, it also *generates* new understanding -- students who articulate a partial idea often complete it in the act of speaking. The four-step framework (Clarify/Share, Orient to Others, Deepen Reasoning, Engage with Others' Reasoning) is a deliberate progression from individual articulation to collective knowledge-building.

- **Indiana Standards:** PS.3: "Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others." This is talk-as-learning encoded as a standard. Students don't just solve problems; they construct arguments about *why* their solutions work and critique *whether* others' solutions work. PS.6: "Attend to precision" -- mathematical precision requires mathematical language, which develops through use.

- **CCSS:** MP.3 is identical. MP.6 adds that precision includes "communicating precisely to others." Mathematical communication is a standard, not an elective.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** Every IM lesson includes a Synthesis phase -- a structured whole-class discussion. The teacher guide provides specific discussion prompts designed to surface key mathematical ideas. But the ideas don't come from the teacher; they come from student work. The Synthesis is where student-generated strategies become shared class knowledge.

**Why students miss this:** They experienced math class as silent individual practice followed by teacher correction. "Discussion" sounds like a humanities thing. The common TAI cycle exposes this: Teacher asks "Why?" Student says "Um..." Teacher moves on. The problem isn't that the student doesn't know -- it's that nobody taught them how to *talk* about mathematical reasoning, and the teacher used the wrong question type (see the Wk05D1 slide: "ask *what* questions first... *why* questions are often confusing and don't have a ready answer").

**The unlock:** The Talk Moves give concrete structure to mathematical conversation. "Say More" gets a student past "I added" to "I added the tens first because I know 30 + 40 is 70, and then I added the ones." That articulation forces the student to organize their thinking, making implicit reasoning explicit -- and *that* is when understanding forms. The talk isn't testing the understanding; the talk *is* the understanding.

### Big Idea 4: More Teacher Planning Creates More Student Freedom

**The insight:** It looks like a paradox: the most student-centered classrooms require the most teacher preparation. But it makes sense once you see it. A teacher who has anticipated six possible student strategies can respond flexibly to whichever one appears. A teacher who hasn't anticipated anything can only fall back on "let me show you how." Preparation is what makes responsiveness possible.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The Before phase is explicitly about teacher planning. Choosing worthwhile tasks, understanding the mathematical terrain, knowing what comes before and after in the progression -- all of this is preparation that enables the teacher to step back during the During phase and let students work. Van de Walle advocates solving all the problems yourself before teaching, from multiple angles. The more you know about the math, the more freedom you can give students to explore it.

- **Five Practices:** This is the framework's core insight. Practice 0 (Setting Goals, Selecting Tasks) and Practice 1 (Anticipating) are both *pre-instruction* practices. Together they typically require more time than the lesson itself. Ms. Tyus anticipated *six* student solutions before teaching. She prepared assessing questions and advancing questions for each. She built a monitoring chart. All of this invisible labor is what allowed her, during instruction, to respond to each student's actual thinking rather than imposing her own solution path. Without the preparation, Monitoring collapses into "checking for the right answer." With it, Monitoring becomes a rich assessment-and-teaching act.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Chapter 8 is devoted to planning talk-based lessons. The insight: you can't just "do talk moves" on the fly. You need to anticipate which talk moves will be productive at which moments. If you plan to Press for Reasoning (Step 3), you need to know what reasoning you're pressing for. If you plan to have students Agree or Disagree (Step 4), you need to have selected a mathematical claim worth debating. The planning makes the spontaneity possible.

- **Indiana Standards:** The standards themselves are a planning tool. Vertical articulation (tracing how Number Sense develops from K through 6) enables teachers to know what students should have learned before and what they'll learn after. This knowledge -- which requires study and preparation -- is what allows a teacher to meet students where they actually are instead of where the lesson plan assumes they are.

- **CCSS:** The progressions documents (e.g., the K-5 Number and Operations in Base Ten progression) provide the detailed developmental sequence that planning requires. A teacher who has studied the progression can recognize where a student's strategy falls in the developmental landscape and make an informed next move.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM lessons come with extensive teacher notes that embody enormous planning: anticipated student strategies, suggested monitoring approaches, discussion questions tied to learning goals. A teacher who reads and internalizes this guidance can run a lesson that *looks* improvised but is actually deeply prepared. IM's recommendation: "Read the how-to-use guide" -- because understanding the curriculum's design enables flexible implementation.

**Why students miss this:** Planning feels like rigidity. "If I script everything, won't the lesson be mechanical?" No -- scripting the lecture makes the lesson mechanical. Planning the *response space* -- anticipating what students might do and preparing how to respond -- makes the lesson responsive. The paradox resolves: teacher planning doesn't constrain student thinking. It prepares the teacher to *follow* student thinking wherever it goes.

**The unlock:** Compare two teachers facing the same unexpected student strategy. Teacher A, who didn't anticipate it, says "That's interesting, but let me show you the way we're going to do it." Teacher B, who anticipated something similar, says "Tell me more about what you did there -- how did you know to split the number that way?" Teacher A took control away from the student. Teacher B created space for the student to deepen their own reasoning. The difference between them is preparation.

### Big Idea 5: Multiple Representations Deepen Understanding

**The insight:** Understanding a mathematical idea means being able to see it from multiple angles -- as a physical manipulation, as a picture, as a notation, as a story, as a relationship to other ideas. Moving between representations is not a detour from the math; it *is* the math. The Concrete-Representational-Abstract (CRA) progression is not just a teaching technique; it reflects how mathematical knowledge actually develops.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The CRA framework is a Van de Walle signature. Concrete (base-ten blocks, fraction strips, M&Ms for measuring), Representational (drawings, diagrams, number lines), Abstract (equations, algorithms). The crucial claim: each representation carries different mathematical information. Base-ten blocks make place value *visible* in a way that written numerals do not -- you can see that 137 is one flat, three longs, and seven units. The area model makes the distributive property *visible* in a way that the standard multiplication algorithm does not. Fractions get five models (area, length, set) and five meanings (part-whole, measurement, division, operator, ratio) because no single representation captures the full concept.

- **Five Practices:** Sequencing (Practice 4) often means ordering strategies from concrete to abstract, or from less sophisticated to more sophisticated representations. Ms. Tyus's four selected solutions (B, D, F, G) were sequenced to build a coherent mathematical storyline -- each representation illuminating a different aspect of two-digit subtraction. Connecting (Practice 5) then asks students to see the relationships *between* representations: "How is DuJuan's number line strategy related to Elsie's base-ten block strategy?" The connection between representations is where the deepest learning happens.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Chapter 3 (Mathematical Concepts) and Chapter 4 (Computational Procedures) both emphasize that talk helps students *translate* between representations. When a student says "I drew three groups of four" and another says "I wrote 3 times 4 equals 12," Press for Reasoning (Step 3) can ask: "How does your picture connect to that equation?" This translation work builds the cross-representational understanding that constitutes real mathematical knowledge.

- **Indiana Standards:** PS.4: "Model with mathematics." PS.2: "Reason abstractly and quantitatively" -- specifically the ability to "decontextualize" (move from a situation to an abstract representation) and "contextualize" (move from abstract symbols back to a real situation). The standards expect students to move fluidly between representations.

- **CCSS:** MP.4 (Model with mathematics) and MP.2 (Reason abstractly and quantitatively) mirror Indiana. Content standards at every grade level name specific representations: "using objects, drawings, and equations" (K-1), "using rectangular arrays and/or area models" (3-4), "visual fraction models" (3-5).

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM's activity sequences often progress through representations deliberately. A 3rd-grade multiplication lesson might start with physical groups of objects, move to array drawings, then to area diagrams, and finally to equations. The teacher guide names this as intentional: "Students work with concrete representations before moving to more abstract ones." Each IM lesson's Warm-Up often uses a specific representation (number line, ten-frame, diagram) to activate prior knowledge in a form that connects to the day's work.

**Why students miss this:** They think "concrete" means "for struggling students" and "abstract" means "for advanced students." In fact, concrete representations carry mathematical information that abstract notation compresses away. When you write 3/4, you can't see that it means three copies of one-fourth of a whole. When you fold a paper strip into fourths and mark three of them, you can. The concrete version isn't simpler -- it's more mathematically transparent.

**The unlock:** Consider the fraction 3/5. As a part-whole area model, it's three shaded parts of a rectangle cut into five. As a measurement on a number line, it's three iterations of the unit 1/5. As a division, it's 3 divided by 5. As an operator, it's "three-fifths of" some quantity. As a ratio, it's 3 to 5. These aren't five ways of saying the same thing -- they're five genuinely different mathematical meanings that happen to share a notation. A student who knows all five has a rich, flexible understanding. A student who knows only one has a brittle, partial understanding that will break when the context changes.

### Big Idea 6: Equity Is About Who Gets to Think, Not Just Who Gets to Sit in the Room

**The insight:** Access to mathematical learning is not guaranteed by physical presence in a classroom. It is determined by whose ideas get taken up, who gets positioned as a mathematical thinker, whose strategies get selected for whole-class discussion, and who gets the time and space to articulate reasoning. Equity is structural: it lives in teacher decisions, not just teacher intentions.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** "Creating a mathematical community" is presented as a prerequisite for mathematical learning, not an afterthought. When Van de Walle advocates for multiple solution strategies, the equity implication is that students with different strengths can all contribute. The student who draws a picture and the student who writes an equation are both doing mathematics. If only the equation-writer gets recognized, the picture-drawer learns that their mathematical thinking doesn't count.

- **Five Practices:** Selecting (Practice 3) is where equity lives or dies. The slides make it explicit: "It is important for ALL students at different times to get a chance to share NOT just the students with the clearest work or who are the most verbally articulate." And: "Bias can affect ALL of us so it is critical to pay attention to other factors (e.g., race, gender, other background factors)." The quote from the conference is telling: "Asking 'Who wants to share?' is either the bravest or the dumbest thing to do in teaching." Calling on volunteers systematically over-represents confident, verbally fluent students and under-represents everyone else. Purposeful selection flips this.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Chapter 2 -- "Classroom Norms for Productive Talk" -- names two explicit goals: respectful discourse and equitable participation. The Talk Moves themselves are equity tools. "Who Can Repeat?" ensures that ideas don't belong to one student; they become shared property. Revoicing lets the teacher elevate a student's idea and make it available to the class, especially when the student's own language was tentative or unclear. Wait Time creates space for students who need more processing time -- disproportionately students learning English, students with disabilities, and students who have been taught that speed equals intelligence.

- **Indiana Standards:** PS.3: "Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others." The word "others" is doing equity work -- every student's reasoning is available for engagement, not just the teacher's or the "best" student's. The standard doesn't say "listen to the teacher's argument."

- **CCSS:** MP.3 mirrors Indiana. The progressions documents assume all students develop mathematical understanding through the same general trajectory (counting strategies to reasoning strategies to automaticity), rejecting the notion that some students are "math people" and others aren't.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM's design philosophy includes "invitations to reason" -- tasks that have multiple entry points so that students at different levels of understanding can all engage. The teacher guide frequently notes when to use Turn and Talk (so quiet students process with a partner before whole-class discussion), when to offer sentence frames (so language doesn't become a barrier to mathematical participation), and when to give extra processing time.

**Why students miss this:** They think equity means "be nice to everyone" or "give struggling students easier problems." But equity in mathematical discourse is about *epistemic access* -- who gets to be a knower, whose ideas get amplified, whose reasoning gets taken seriously. A teacher who always calls on the same five students is not being inequitable on purpose; they're being inequitable by default.

**The unlock:** Next time you're in a classroom, track *who talks* and *whose ideas get discussed further*. You'll notice patterns that no one intended but everyone reproduces. The Five Practices' Selecting move gives you a tool to interrupt those patterns deliberately: decide in advance which students will share, based on what mathematical ideas you want the class to engage with and which students deserve to be positioned as thinkers today.

### Big Idea 7: Assessment and Instruction Are the Same Act

**The insight:** In traditional teaching, instruction and assessment are separate events: first you teach, then you test. In the frameworks used in this course, they happen simultaneously. Every question a teacher asks during instruction is simultaneously a teaching move and an assessment move. Monitoring is not pausing instruction to check -- it *is* instruction.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The During phase (Explore) is both the most active instructional time *and* the most active assessment time. "Diagnostic interviews" and "assessment through observation" are described not as special events but as ongoing teaching practices. When a teacher watches a student build 137 with base-ten blocks and asks "Can you show me another way to make that number?", the teacher is simultaneously assessing (does the student understand equivalent representations?) and teaching (prompting the student to think about regrouping).

- **Five Practices:** Monitoring (Practice 2) is the clearest expression of this idea. The teacher circulates, uses assessing questions to surface student thinking, and uses advancing questions to push it further. Assessing questions ("Tell me what you did") are assessment -- they reveal the student's current understanding. Advancing questions ("What would happen if you used a different number?") are instruction -- they move the student's thinking toward the learning goal. Both happen in the same conversation, often in the same sentence. The monitoring chart is simultaneously a teaching plan and an assessment record.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Steps 1-2 (Clarify/Share, Orient to Others) are assessment tools: they reveal what students think. Steps 3-4 (Deepen Reasoning, Engage with Others) are instructional tools: they build new understanding. But they're all "talk moves" -- the same toolkit serves both purposes. The CDM videos from class (Ms. Tyus working with Jocelynn, DuJuan, Leah, Elsie) show a teacher simultaneously discovering what students think *and* moving their thinking forward. The distinction between assessment and instruction dissolves.

- **Indiana Standards:** Content standards are assessable performance expectations. Process Standards are the practices through which students demonstrate and develop understanding. When a student "constructs a viable argument" (PS.3), the teacher can assess their understanding *and* the act of constructing the argument deepens that understanding.

- **CCSS:** Same structure. The Standards for Mathematical Practice are simultaneously things students should learn to do (instruction target) and things that reveal mathematical understanding (assessment evidence).

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** The Cool-Down is a formal assessment instrument, but IM embeds assessment throughout. Teacher notes include "look for" statements during the Work phase: "Look for students who are using the number line to show iteration" is both an assessment prompt (find out who's doing it) and an instructional prompt (once you find them, you can select their work for the Synthesis).

**Why students miss this:** Their mental model of assessment is "the test at the end." They separate teaching time from testing time. This leads to the classic problem: they teach a lesson, give an assessment, discover students didn't understand, and think "I need to reteach." But if they'd been assessing *during* instruction through monitoring and questioning, they would have caught the misunderstanding in real time and adjusted.

**The unlock:** The Think-Aloud Interview (TAI) assignment makes this concrete. The instructions are explicit: "YOU ARE NOT TEACHING. You're not helping them understand anything, you're just trying to find out what they know now." But the TAI *also* says to use Talk Moves -- Revoicing, Say More, Press for Reasoning. These are instructional moves deployed for assessment purposes. The line between "finding out what they know" and "helping them articulate what they know" is not a line at all. The student who says "I added" and is asked to "Say More" and responds "I added the tens first because..." has just learned something about their own reasoning through the act of assessment.

### Big Idea 8: Mathematical Ideas Are Connected, Not Isolated

**The insight:** Mathematics is not a collection of separate topics learned in sequence and then forgotten. It is a web of connected ideas where each concept builds on and informs others. Understanding these connections -- across grade levels, across operations, across representations -- is what separates mathematical knowledge from a bag of tricks.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The structure of the textbook enacts this: chapters on place value connect forward to chapters on multi-digit operations; fact fluency connects to algebraic reasoning; fraction concepts connect to division. Van de Walle's treatment of addition/subtraction problem types makes connections explicit: a "join change unknown" problem (__ + 4 = 12) connects addition to subtraction and prefigures algebraic thinking. The semantic equation (__ + 4 = 12) and the calculation equation (12 - 4 = __) are *the same problem* -- seeing both representations simultaneously is the mathematical connection.

- **Five Practices:** Connecting (Practice 5) is literally named for this idea. It has two purposes: connect student work to the lesson's mathematical goals, and connect student solutions to each other. Ms. Tyus's four learning goals for the two-digit subtraction lesson illustrate: Goal 4 is "Multiple representations can be used to solve problems, and the different representations can be connected to each other." The Connecting practice makes mathematical connections explicit rather than leaving them for students to discover (or not discover) on their own.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** "Who Can Add On?" (Step 4) is a connection-building move. When a student has shared a strategy and the teacher asks "Who can add on?", the resulting contribution often connects the first strategy to a different representation or extends it to a new case. Chapter 3 (Mathematical Concepts) discusses how talk helps students see connections between number patterns, area, and perimeter -- topics that seem separate but share deep structural relationships.

- **Indiana Standards:** Vertical articulation is the structural embodiment of mathematical connection. The Wk09D1 and Wk10D2 activities -- reading the K-2 and 2-3 vertical articulation documents -- ask students to trace how Number Sense and Computation/Algebraic Thinking develop across grades. What's new? What's gone? What's assumed? These questions make the progression visible. PS.7: "Look for and make use of structure" and PS.8: "Look for and express regularity in repeated reasoning" are connection standards -- they ask students to find the patterns that link mathematical ideas together.

- **CCSS:** The CCSS were explicitly designed with progressions in mind. The "Domains" (Operations and Algebraic Thinking, Number and Operations in Base Ten, etc.) thread across grade levels precisely to make connections visible. The progressions documents spell out how addition facts in Grade 2 build on counting in Grade 1 and feed into multiplication in Grade 3. MP.7 and MP.8 mirror Indiana's PS.7 and PS.8.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM's unit and lesson design is built on progressions. A Grade 3 unit on multiplication might span equal groups (concrete), arrays (semi-concrete), area models (more abstract), and the distributive property (fully abstract) -- four lessons that look like different topics but are actually four views of the same mathematical structure. The teacher notes frequently say "this connects to..." and "students will revisit this idea in Grade 4 when..."

**Why students miss this:** The course itself can feel like a sequence of separate topics -- "this week we do Talk Moves, next week we do Monitoring, then we switch to operations content." But the Talk Moves you learned in Week 5 are the same moves Ms. Tyus uses during Monitoring in Week 6, which are the same tools you need for the Summarize phase of your lesson in Week 8, which help students articulate the mathematical connections in the fraction content of Week 14. The frameworks aren't separate units; they're lenses on the same teaching.

**The unlock:** Consider how division connects to fractions. In Week 12, students learn that 7 divided by 4 gives a remainder of 3. In Week 13, they learn that the remainder can be written as 3/4 -- so 7 / 4 = 1 3/4. The division *is* the fraction. This connection (Van de Walle's "division construct" for fractions) means that students who understand sharing division already understand something about fractions, even before the fraction unit starts. The connection was always there; it just needs to be made explicit.

### Big Idea 9: Productive Struggle Is the Mechanism of Learning, Not a Side Effect

**The insight:** When students struggle with a mathematical problem -- when they try an approach that doesn't work, reconsider, try another way, and eventually build understanding -- the struggle itself is the learning. It is not a failure of instruction that students must work through difficulty. It is the *purpose* of instruction that students work through difficulty. Removing the struggle removes the learning.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** "Teaching through problem solving" means deliberately placing students in situations where they don't know the answer and don't know the procedure. VdW's approach to basic facts makes this vivid: the "guided invention" approach doesn't tell students the strategy for 8 x 7. It puts them in a situation where they need 8 x 7, gives them tools (knowledge of 8 x 5, or 4 x 7 doubled), and lets them *invent* a path. The "explicit strategy instruction" approach is slightly more directed, but still requires students to *use* the strategy, not just hear it described. Memorization bypasses the struggle -- and produces brittle knowledge.

- **Five Practices:** Practice 0 (Selecting Tasks) is the origin of productive struggle. The task must have "high cognitive demand" -- meaning students cannot solve it by simply applying a memorized procedure. If the task is too easy, there's nothing to struggle with and nothing to learn. If it's too hard, struggle becomes frustration rather than learning. Setting appropriate goals and matching them with appropriately demanding tasks creates the zone where struggle is productive. The launch phase must "maintain cognitive demand" -- meaning the teacher introduces the task without reducing its difficulty by hinting at the solution.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** Wait Time is the most basic struggle-enabling move. When a teacher asks a question and immediately calls on someone, only students who already know the answer participate. When a teacher waits -- 5 seconds, 10 seconds, uncomfortably long -- students who don't immediately know must *think*. That thinking is the struggle. Press for Reasoning (Step 3) extends the struggle: "I know you got 15, but *why* does 8 + 7 equal 15?" pushes a student past the answer into the reasoning, where the real mathematical work happens.

- **Indiana Standards:** PS.1: "Make sense of problems and *persevere* in solving them." The word "persevere" is doing all the work. It presupposes difficulty. It expects struggle. It values continuation through struggle. A student who gives up has not met PS.1. A student who perseveres and builds understanding has.

- **CCSS:** MP.1 is identical to PS.1, including the word "persevere." The CCSS progressions documents repeatedly use language about students "developing" understanding over time -- not receiving it in a single lesson but building it through sustained engagement with increasingly complex problems.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM's teacher guidance includes a specific directive: "Maintain cognitive demand during the Work phase." This means: when students are stuck, resist the urge to show them how. Instead, use assessing questions to understand their thinking and advancing questions to give them a productive next step -- but not the answer. The guidance also notes that advancing questions "often cannot be answered immediately" and should "give students something to work on for a while." This is struggle by design.

**Why students miss this:** They conflate "struggle" with "suffering." They've experienced math anxiety themselves, and they don't want to inflict it on children. But productive struggle and anxiety are opposites. Anxiety comes from feeling incompetent with no path forward. Productive struggle comes from facing a genuine challenge with sufficient resources (prior knowledge, representations, a supportive classroom, a monitoring teacher) to work through it. The Five Practices framework exists to ensure that struggle stays productive: the teacher monitors to catch students who are stuck, uses advancing questions to provide a foothold without giving away the answer, and creates a classroom norm where being stuck is expected and valued.

**The unlock:** Think about the Ms. Tyus Markers Task. First-graders are subtracting two-digit numbers -- which is genuinely hard for six-year-olds. Some students use base-ten blocks. Some draw pictures. Some use number lines. Some make errors. Ms. Tyus doesn't rescue them. She monitors, she questions, she tracks strategies on her monitoring chart. When a student has an incorrect adjustment for a rounding strategy, she doesn't correct it immediately -- she plans to bring both the correct and incorrect reasoning to the class discussion, where the *comparison* between them becomes the learning. The student's struggle, including the error, is not a problem to be fixed. It is material to be learned from.

### Big Idea 10: A Lesson Is a Story, Not a Checklist

**The insight:** A well-taught mathematics lesson has narrative arc. It begins with a question that creates genuine intellectual need (Launch). It develops as students grapple with that question in diverse ways (Explore). It resolves as the class collectively builds understanding by connecting different approaches to a central mathematical idea (Summarize). The Five Practices are, at their core, a storytelling framework: Anticipate the plot, Monitor how it unfolds, Select the key moments, Sequence them into a coherent narrative, Connect them to the meaning of the story.

**How each framework expresses this:**

- **Van de Walle:** The Before/During/After structure is narrative: set up the problem (rising action), students work through it (climax), class makes sense of what happened (resolution). Van de Walle's advocacy for problem-based tasks is really advocacy for *interesting* problems -- problems that create intellectual need, that make students want to know the answer, that give them a reason to reason. "What is a fair way to distribute 6 large blocks of clay to 27 students?" is a story. "Complete a worksheet of fraction division problems" is not.

- **Five Practices:** The sequencing practice names this explicitly. The slides say: "Your purpose is to tell a coherent math story." Sequencing strategies include "most concrete first, moving to more abstract" (building complexity), "begin with a common misconception" (creating tension), and "put related or contrasting strategies back-to-back to make comparison easier" (developing the theme). The caution -- "don't always use the same sequencing strategy" -- is good storytelling advice: if the audience can predict the arc, it stops being engaging.

- **CDM/Talk Moves:** The four steps of talk moves trace a narrative arc within a single discussion. Step 1 (individual clarification) is the exposition -- getting ideas on the table. Step 2 (orienting to others) is the complication -- now there are multiple ideas in play. Step 3 (deepening reasoning) is the rising action -- ideas are tested and pushed. Step 4 (engaging with others' reasoning) is the climax and resolution -- ideas are compared, agreed with, challenged, and synthesized into shared understanding.

- **Indiana Standards:** The standards are organized in progressions that tell a developmental story. Number Sense in Kindergarten (count to 100, understand quantity) progresses to Grade 1 (place value of two-digit numbers) to Grade 2 (three-digit numbers, regrouping) to Grade 3 (multiplication). Each grade's standards are a chapter in a multi-year mathematical narrative. Understanding vertical articulation means understanding the plot.

- **CCSS:** The CCSS progressions documents are explicitly narrative. The K-5 Operations and Algebraic Thinking progression tells the story of how additive reasoning becomes multiplicative reasoning becomes proportional reasoning. Each grade is a chapter. Within each chapter, the standards build on each other.

- **Illustrative Mathematics:** IM's lesson structure (Warm Up, Activity 1, Activity 2, Synthesis, Cool-Down) is paced like a story. The Warm Up activates prior knowledge (prologue). Activity 1 introduces the core problem (rising action). Activity 2 extends or complicates it (development). The Synthesis pulls it together (climax/resolution). The Cool-Down checks whether the story landed (epilogue). At the unit level, IM sequences lessons to build understanding progressively -- each lesson is a scene in the larger unit narrative.

**Why students miss this:** They think of lesson planning as filling in a template: standard, objective, materials, procedure, assessment. This produces lessons that are technically complete but narratively dead. The student experience is: "today we did this worksheet, and then we did that activity, and then we discussed." There's no thread connecting the parts, no intellectual need driving the work, no resolution that makes the pieces add up to something. The Five Practices template fixes this because it forces the teacher to think about *which student solutions tell which part of the mathematical story*.

**The unlock:** Remember the pizza problem from the Week 8 demo lesson. The Launch creates the story: "Imagine 9 one-pound blocks of cheese. Each pizza needs 1/4 pound. How many pizzas?" Students work through it. Then the teacher *changes* the amount per pizza for subsequent problems, creating a series of related challenges that build toward a generalization about measurement division with fractions. The mathematical punchline -- that dividing by a smaller fraction gives a bigger answer -- emerges from the narrative arc of the lesson, not from a teacher statement. Students experience the insight as the resolution of a story they participated in, rather than a fact they were told.

## The Meta-Insight: Why Six Frameworks?

If these ten ideas are the real content, why does the course use six frameworks to teach them? Because each framework illuminates different facets:

| Framework | Primary lens | What it does best |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Van de Walle** | Mathematical content knowledge | Shows *what* to teach and the developmental sequence |
| **Five Practices** | Instructional planning | Shows *how* to prepare for and orchestrate student-centered lessons |
| **CDM/Talk Moves** | In-the-moment teaching moves | Shows *what to say* when a student is thinking aloud |
| **Indiana Standards** | Accountability and scope | Shows *what students must learn* at each grade level |
| **CCSS** | National coherence and progressions | Shows *how ideas develop* across grades |
| **Illustrative Mathematics** | Curriculum enactment | Shows *what a lesson looks like* when it embodies all of the above |

None is complete alone. Van de Walle gives you the math but not the discussion structure. Five Practices gives you the discussion structure but not the specific talk moves. CDM gives you the talk moves but not the mathematical progressions. The standards tell you the destination but not the route. IM gives you a route but you need the other frameworks to understand *why* it works.

The six frameworks are not six systems to master. They are six windows into the same room. Once you're inside the room, you can look out any window and recognize the same landscape.
