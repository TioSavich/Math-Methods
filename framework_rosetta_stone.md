# The Framework Rosetta Stone

## How Six Frameworks Fit Together as One System for Teaching Elementary Math

*E343: Teaching Mathematics in the Elementary School — Indiana University*

---

## Part 1: The Big Picture

You are learning six frameworks this semester. It feels like six separate courses crammed into one. It is not. These frameworks are six answers to six different questions about the *same* teaching act. Once you see the questions, the frameworks stop competing and start clicking together.

**The six questions and who answers them:**

| Question | Framework | What It Gives You |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| What should students know at each grade? | **Indiana Standards / CCSS** | Learning targets — the destination |
| What does this math actually mean, and how do children develop it? | **Van de Walle** (VdW) | Content knowledge for teaching — the map of the terrain |
| How do I structure a lesson so students do the thinking? | **Five Practices** (5P) | A planning and orchestration system — the game plan |
| What do I literally *say* to students during discussion? | **CDM / Talk Moves** | Specific teacher language moves — the script in your back pocket |
| Where can I find a complete, ready-to-teach lesson? | **Illustrative Mathematics** (IM) | Published curriculum — a lesson that embodies all of the above |
| How do I know if students actually understand? | All of them, together | Each framework contributes a different lens on assessment |

**The key insight:**

These frameworks are not parallel alternatives. They are *layers*. Standards set the destination. Van de Walle helps you understand the math you are teaching and how kids think about it. Five Practices gives you a system for planning and running a lesson. Talk Moves tells you what to say, sentence by sentence, when you are standing in front of real children. And Illustrative Mathematics packages all of these ideas into an actual lesson you can teach tomorrow.

Think of it like cooking. Standards are the nutritional requirements ("this meal needs protein and vegetables"). Van de Walle is the culinary science ("here is why searing meat at high heat creates flavor"). Five Practices is your recipe template ("prep, cook, plate in this order"). Talk Moves is your knife technique ("hold the knife this way, cut on the bias"). And IM is a specific recipe from a good cookbook that already accounts for all of the above.

No single framework is complete alone. But together, they cover everything you need.

## Part 2: The Three-Phase Lesson — The Unifying Structure

Every framework in this course describes the same basic lesson structure. They just slice it differently and name the parts differently. Once you see that, the frameworks collapse into one coherent picture.

### The Structure, Four Ways

| Phase | Van de Walle | Five Practices | CDM / Talk Moves | Illustrative Mathematics |
|-------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **Get ready** | Before | Practice 0: Set goals, select task; Practice 1: Anticipate | (Preparation happens before class) | Lesson preparation + Warm Up |
| **Set up the problem** | Before (Launch) | (Part of Anticipating: plan the launch) | Launch | Launch (for each Activity) |
| **Students work** | During | Practice 2: Monitor (track, assess, advance) | Explore | Work time (Activities 1, 2, ...) |
| **Discuss and connect** | After | Practice 3: Select; Practice 4: Sequence; Practice 5: Connect | Summarize | Synthesis + Cool Down |

This is the same lesson. The names change; the structure does not.

### Walking Through a Real Lesson, Moment by Moment

Imagine you are teaching a third-grade lesson on multiplication using equal groups. Here is how the frameworks layer onto the same 60 minutes.

**Before students arrive — Planning (Van de Walle: Before; Five Practices: Practices 0 and 1)**

You start with the Indiana standard: *"3.C.2: Represent the concept of multiplication of whole numbers with models including equal-sized groups, arrays, area models, and equal intervals on a number line."* You open Van de Walle to the chapter on multiplication and read about how children progress from skip-counting to repeated addition to thinking in groups. You note that some students will draw pictures of individual objects while others will use arrays. You write a learning goal: *"Students will understand that multiplication means finding the total when you have a certain number of equal groups."* You write a performance goal: *"Students will be able to represent a multiplication situation using equal groups or an array and find the product."*

You select a task: "There are 4 bags with 6 apples in each bag. How many apples are there?" This is better than "Solve 4 x 6" because it gives students a story to reason about, not just a procedure to execute.

You anticipate: one student will draw 4 circles with 6 dots in each and count all of them. Another will skip-count (6, 12, 18, 24). Another will use repeated addition (6 + 6 + 6 + 6). A struggling student might draw apples but lose track and get 22. You prepare assessing questions ("Tell me about your drawing") and advancing questions ("Is there a way to figure out how many without counting every single apple?"). You prepare a monitoring chart.

**The first five minutes — Launch (CDM: Launch; IM: Launch; VdW: Before)**

You read the problem aloud. You do *not* show students how to solve it. Instead, you make sure they understand the situation. You ask: "What do we know? What are we trying to find out?" A student says, "We know there are 4 bags." You use a Talk Move: *revoicing*. "So you're saying there are 4 bags, and each one has the same number of apples. Is that right?" The student nods. You say, "Turn and talk to your neighbor about how you might get started." This is *not* instruction yet. This is making sure everyone can access the task.

**The next twenty minutes — Student Work (VdW: During; 5P: Monitoring; CDM: Explore; IM: Activity)**

Students work in pairs. You circulate. One pair has drawn all 24 apples individually and is counting them one by one. You use an *assessing question*: "Can you tell me about your picture?" The student explains. You note on your monitoring chart: "Table 3 — direct modeling, counting all." This is Monitoring — tracking and assessing.

At another table, a student has written 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 = 24 but cannot explain what the 6s represent. You use a Talk Move: *say more*. "Can you say more about where the sixes came from?" The student thinks and says, "Oh, each bag has six apples, so I added the bags up." You use an *advancing question*: "Is there a way you could organize your work so someone else could see the 4 bags?" This pushes toward a more structured representation without telling the student what to do.

**The last fifteen minutes — Discussion (VdW: After; 5P: Select, Sequence, Connect; CDM: Summarize; IM: Synthesis)**

You have been watching the room and updating your monitoring chart. Now you *select* three pieces of work to share: the direct model (drawing all apples), the skip-counting approach, and the repeated addition approach. You *sequence* them from most concrete to most abstract — pictures first, then skip-counting, then repeated addition. This is deliberate: it builds a storyline from "I can see every apple" toward "I can use numbers to represent the groups."

During the discussion, you use Talk Moves constantly. After the first student shares their drawing, you ask the class: *"Who can repeat what Jaylen just told us about her picture?"* (orienting to others' thinking). After the second student shares skip-counting, you ask: *"Do you agree or disagree that this skip-counting shows the same thing as Jaylen's picture? Why?"* (engaging with others' reasoning). After the third student shares repeated addition, you *connect*: "Look at Jaylen's picture. Look at Marcus's equation, 6 + 6 + 6 + 6. Where are Jaylen's bags in Marcus's equation?" This is the *connecting* practice — tying student solutions to each other and to the learning goal.

You close with a Cool Down (IM) or exit ticket: "Draw a picture AND write an equation for this problem: There are 3 boxes with 5 crayons in each box." This lets you assess whether students can now move between representations — your learning goal.

**That was one lesson. All six frameworks were at work simultaneously.**

## Part 3: Framework Overlap Matrix

For each major teaching activity, here is which framework addresses it and what it calls it. A dash (—) means the framework does not directly address that activity.

### Setting Learning Goals

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | Goals should target understanding, not just doing. Conceptual knowledge vs. procedural fluency. |
| **Five Practices** | *Learning goals* = what students will understand. *Performance goals* = what you can observe them doing. Both are needed; they are not the same thing. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | — (Talk Moves operate during instruction, not during goal-setting) |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | Content standards define grade-level expectations. Process standards describe mathematical habits of mind. |
| **IM** | Each lesson states goals explicitly. The cool-down is designed to assess the goal. |

### Choosing and Designing Tasks

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | "Teaching through problem solving." Tasks should have high cognitive demand — students should have to *think*, not just follow procedures. |
| **Five Practices** | Practice 0. Tasks must align to learning goals. Cognitive demand is everything: a low-demand task limits the ceiling of what students can learn. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | — (assumes a worthwhile task is already chosen) |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | PS.1: "Make sense of problems and persevere in solving them" — this implies tasks should require genuine sense-making. |
| **IM** | Provides ready-made, high-demand tasks. You can also modify them. |

### Launching a Lesson

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | The "Before" phase: make sure students understand the problem without showing them how to solve it. Activate prior knowledge. |
| **Five Practices** | Part of Anticipating (Practice 1): plan how you will introduce the task so students can access it but are not told a method. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | "Launch": establish the context, clarify vocabulary, make sure everyone can get started. Use revoicing and "say more" to check understanding. |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | — (standards describe outcomes, not instruction) |
| **IM** | Each activity has a "Launch" section with specific teacher instructions for how to introduce the task. |

### Monitoring Student Work

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | The "During" phase: observe, assess, question. Do not rescue students from productive struggle. |
| **Five Practices** | Practice 2 — Monitoring. Three parts: *tracking* (noting who does what on a monitoring chart), *assessing* (asking questions to surface current thinking), and *advancing* (asking questions to push thinking forward). |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | Steps 1-2 talk moves work here: revoicing and "say more" help you figure out what a student is thinking. Press for reasoning (Step 3) pushes students to explain. |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | — (standards do not describe teacher actions during instruction) |
| **IM** | Teacher guides include "Advancing Student Thinking" prompts for each activity — questions to ask when students are stuck or need a push. |

### Leading Whole-Class Discussion

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | The "After" phase: students share, compare, and consolidate. Teacher guides but does not dominate. |
| **Five Practices** | Practices 3, 4, and 5. *Select* which student work to share (purposefully, not randomly). *Sequence* it to tell a coherent math story (often concrete to abstract). *Connect* solutions to each other and to the learning goal. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | This is Talk Moves territory. All four steps apply: clarify and share (revoicing, say more), orient to others (who can repeat?), deepen reasoning (press for reasoning), engage with others (agree/disagree, who can add on?). |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | PS.3: "Construct viable arguments and critique the reasoning of others" — this is what students practice during discussion. |
| **IM** | "Synthesis" section of each activity and lesson. Specifies what mathematical ideas should emerge from discussion. |

### Assessing Student Understanding

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | Diagnostic interviews, observation during work, analyzing student artifacts. Understanding is not the same as getting the right answer. |
| **Five Practices** | Monitoring IS formative assessment. Assessing questions surface thinking. The monitoring chart records evidence. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | Steps 1-2 (revoicing, say more) are assessment tools: they make student thinking visible to you and to the class. |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | Content standards describe what to assess. Process standards describe how students should be thinking. |
| **IM** | "Cool Down" at the end of each lesson: a brief independent task aligned to the lesson goal. This is your formal check. |

### Planning for Equity

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | Teaching through problem solving is inherently more equitable — multiple entry points. Use multiple representations (concrete, visual, symbolic). |
| **Five Practices** | Selecting: be intentional about who gets to present. All students should be positioned as mathematical thinkers over time, not just the fastest or most articulate. Bias can affect all of us. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | Turn and Talk gives every student a voice, not just those who raise hands. Revoicing elevates quiet students' ideas. "Who can repeat?" distributes attention. |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | — (standards apply to all students but do not address pedagogical equity) |
| **IM** | Built-in supports for English learners, differentiation suggestions, and multiple representations in every lesson. |

### Managing Productive Struggle

| Framework | What It Says |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Van de Walle** | "Teaching through problem solving" requires that students struggle with genuine problems. The teacher's job is to resist rescuing. |
| **Five Practices** | Advancing questions keep students working without telling them the answer. Assessing questions help you gauge whether the struggle is productive or frustrating. |
| **CDM / Talk Moves** | "Say more" and "press for reasoning" keep students thinking instead of giving up. These moves communicate that you believe they can figure it out. |
| **Indiana / CCSS** | PS.1: "Make sense of problems and *persevere* in solving them." Perseverance is the standard. |
| **IM** | Activity design maintains cognitive demand. "Advancing Student Thinking" prompts help teachers support without over-scaffolding. |

## Part 4: When to Reach for Which Framework

Here is your practical cheat sheet. When you are stuck on a specific problem, reach for the framework that solves it.

### "I need to design a lesson from scratch."

Reach for **Van de Walle** (understand the math content and how kids develop it) + **Five Practices** (structure for planning). Start by identifying the standard (Indiana), then read VdW to understand the math deeply. Use the Five Practices framework to plan: set goals, select a task, anticipate student strategies, prepare a monitoring chart, and plan your discussion.

### "I need to plan what to say during a discussion."

Reach for **CDM / Talk Moves**. The four steps give you a repertoire of specific moves:
- Step 1 — *Revoicing* and *Say More*: help a student clarify their thinking
- Step 2 — *Who Can Repeat?*: orient the class to one student's idea
- Step 3 — *Press for Reasoning*: push a student to explain why, not just what
- Step 4 — *Agree/Disagree* and *Who Can Add On?*: get students engaging with each other's thinking

You do not need to use all of these in every lesson. Pick one or two to practice at first. They work in whole-class discussion, small-group check-ins, and one-on-one conversations.

### "I need to understand what students should know at a particular grade level."

Reach for **Indiana Academic Standards**. Content standards tell you what students should know and do at each grade. Content strands (Number Sense, Computation, Geometry, Measurement, Data Analysis) organize the math. Process standards describe how students should be thinking across all content areas.

Van de Walle helps here too: it explains the *progression* of ideas across grades (what comes before, what comes after), which the standards alone do not make visible.

### "I need a ready-made lesson I can teach this week."

Reach for **Illustrative Mathematics**. IM is a complete, freely available curriculum that embeds the principles of all the other frameworks. Each lesson has a Warm Up, Activities with Launch/Work/Synthesize structure, a Synthesis discussion, and a Cool Down assessment. Teacher guides include anticipated student strategies, discussion questions, and differentiation supports.

Remember: you will rarely teach an IM lesson exactly as written. You will still need to make choices about what is worth the instructional time, how to adapt for your students, and what to emphasize. That is where your knowledge of the other frameworks comes in.

### "I am preparing for my field placement and need to put it all together."

Start with the **standard** your lesson addresses (Indiana). Read the relevant **Van de Walle** section to understand the math and how students think about it. Find or adapt a task from **IM** or from VdW. Plan the lesson using the **Five Practices** structure (goals, anticipating, monitoring chart, selection/sequencing plan, connecting questions). Prepare your talk by identifying two or three **Talk Moves** you will practice during the lesson.

Your lesson plan assignment asks you to do exactly this — not because we want you to become an expert in six frameworks, but because this is what planning a strong math lesson actually involves.

## Putting It All Together

When this course asks you to write a lesson plan, you are not being asked to apply six different frameworks. You are being asked to plan one coherent lesson that draws on different tools for different parts of the job:

1. **Where am I going?** Check the Indiana standard. Read Van de Walle to understand the math.
2. **What task will get students there?** Choose or adapt from IM, guided by the Five Practices criteria for high-demand tasks.
3. **What might students do?** Anticipate strategies and misconceptions (Five Practices, informed by Van de Walle).
4. **How will I launch the task?** Plan your launch so students understand the problem without being told how to solve it (CDM + Five Practices).
5. **What will I do while students work?** Circulate, track thinking on your monitoring chart, ask assessing and advancing questions (Five Practices + Talk Moves).
6. **How will I run the discussion?** Select and sequence student work to tell a mathematical story. Use Talk Moves to get students explaining, questioning, and building on each other's ideas (Five Practices + CDM).
7. **How will I know they got it?** Use the cool-down, your monitoring chart, and what you heard during discussion (IM + Five Practices).

That is not six frameworks. That is one lesson, planned well.
